# Pilot Operation Protocol

## Operational Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Operational template
**Status:** Draft for review and refinement through actual pilot operations
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Bioregional Strategy, Stewardship Lease Requirements, A3 PRD, MRV Specification, Federation Membership Agreement, Personnel and Compensation Philosophy

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies how Robotanica conducts a first-wave bioregional engagement: from initial regional candidacy through pilot operations to demonstrated outcomes.

The Bioregional Strategy specified the framework for selecting where to work. The Stewardship Lease Requirements specified the legal instrument that governs the relationship. The MRV Specification specified outcome measurement. None of those documents specifies the operational sequence — what actually happens, in what order, with which parties, against what milestones.

This Protocol is that sequence. It is a **template**, not a script. Specific engagements adapt to bioregional, jurisdictional, and partnership context. The template specifies the durable structure within which adaptation occurs.

The Protocol covers operations through end of Phase 1 and into Phase 2 per the Founding Capital Plan. Subsequent operational phases (multi-region scaling, federation expansion) build on but extend beyond this Protocol.

The Protocol is **a v0 starting point**. It will be refined substantially as actual pilot operations generate experience. Annual review is structural.

---

## 2. The Engagement Lifecycle

A bioregional engagement follows seven stages:

```
[1] Regional Candidacy
   ↓
[2] Pre-Engagement Diligence
   ↓
[3] Partnership Formation
   ↓
[4] Lease and Consent
   ↓
[5] Operational Launch
   ↓
[6] Pilot Year (First-Year Cycle)
   ↓
[7] Continuation, Expansion, or Withdrawal
```

Each stage has entry criteria, work to be done, exit criteria, and decisions made by named bodies. The stages are sequential but with feedback — discoveries in later stages can return earlier stages to revision.

This document specifies each stage.

---

## 3. Stage 1 — Regional Candidacy

### 3.1 Purpose

Identify a region as a candidate for engagement, justify the candidacy against Bioregional Strategy criteria, and authorize Stage 2 diligence work.

### 3.2 Entry Criteria

- The Federation governance body and Stewardship Office determine that a new regional engagement is timely.
- Capacity exists to support an additional region without compromising existing engagements.
- Funding is identified or pursued for the engagement.

### 3.3 Work to Be Done

- **Initial research:** Bioregional category, climate trajectory, ecological state, political and legal geography.
- **Partner mapping:** Existing federation members in or near the region; mission-aligned organizations operating there; indigenous and local communities active in stewardship.
- **Initial outreach:** Quiet conversations with established advisors, community contacts, and partner organizations to assess interest and openness.
- **Justification document:** A regional candidacy document covering: why this region, against what criteria from Bioregional Strategy §4, with what initial partners, at what scale.

### 3.4 Decisions and Bodies

The candidacy document is reviewed by:

- Stewardship Office (primary review, partnership and Lease feasibility).
- Science Council (ecological appropriateness, recipe alignment).
- Federation governance (capacity, budget, strategic fit).

Decision: candidacy advances to Stage 2, candidacy revised, or candidacy paused.

### 3.5 Exit Criteria

- Candidacy approved.
- Initial partners identified (at minimum: at least one federation member operating or willing to operate in the region; potential community partners identified).
- Budget allocated for Stage 2 diligence.
- Internal lead or team assigned.

### 3.6 Time Frame

Approximately 1-3 months from initial consideration to candidacy decision.

---

## 4. Stage 2 — Pre-Engagement Diligence

### 4.1 Purpose

Develop the depth of understanding required to engage responsibly. Test the candidacy against detailed reality. Identify dealbreakers before commitments are made.

### 4.2 Entry Criteria

- Candidacy approved.
- Diligence team assembled.
- Initial partner relationships warm enough to support diligence work.

### 4.3 Work to Be Done

#### 4.3.1 Ecological Diligence

- Detailed bioregional assessment: ecosystem types, current state, historical state, climate trajectory.
- Candidate parcels identified for pilot operations.
- Ecological reference state and counterfactual baseline established (per MRV §9).
- Recipe candidates identified from the Codex; gaps noted where new recipe development may be required.
- Connectivity, refugia, and bioregional context examined.

#### 4.3.2 Political and Legal Diligence

- Jurisdictional analysis: property law, environmental regulation, tax treatment.
- Indigenous and customary rights mapping.
- Existing protected areas and overlapping designations.
- Government counterpart identification.
- Legal counsel engagement for jurisdictional Lease drafting.

#### 4.3.3 Community Diligence

- Indigenous and local community presence.
- Community governance structures.
- Existing relationships with conservation, restoration, or aligned organizations.
- Community priorities, concerns, and capacities.

#### 4.3.4 Operational Diligence

- Logistics: transportation, communications, power, water.
- Existing nursery and biological production capacity.
- Federation member capacity and willingness.
- Worker safety considerations.
- Climate and seasonality affecting operations.

#### 4.3.5 Risk Diligence

- Political stability and trajectory.
- Conflict or security considerations.
- Climate event risk (fire, drought, flood frequency).
- Pest and disease pressure.
- Macroeconomic context (currency, inflation, banking).

### 4.4 Diligence Team

The diligence team typically includes:

- Stewardship Office lead.
- Bioregional ecologist (Council representative or invited expert).
- Legal counsel familiar with the jurisdiction.
- Community liaison (often from a partner organization or indigenous council).
- Operations lead.

Teams are kept small to maintain focus; subject experts are engaged as advisors.

### 4.5 Diligence Output

A diligence report covering:

- All five diligence categories.
- Identified candidate parcels with characteristics.
- Identified candidate partners (community, operator, scientific).
- Identified candidate recipes.
- Risk register with mitigations.
- Cost estimate (per Unit Economics Model).
- Timeline projection.
- Recommendation: proceed to Stage 3, modify scope, or withdraw.

### 4.6 Decision

Decision by Federation governance informed by Stewardship Office and Council. Decision is recorded in the public record.

### 4.7 Exit Criteria

- Diligence complete and recorded.
- Candidate parcels and partners identified.
- Recipes feasible.
- Risks understood and acceptable.
- Authorization to proceed to Stage 3.

### 4.8 Time Frame

Approximately 3-6 months. Indigenous and community diligence — done properly with respect — is the longest component.

### 4.9 Withdrawal During Diligence

Diligence may surface dealbreakers:

- Indigenous community categorically declines.
- Legal jurisdiction incompatible with Charter (per Bioregional Strategy §4.5).
- Security risks beyond bounds personnel can accept.
- Cost profile incompatible with funding.
- Ecological mismatch with available recipes.

Withdrawal at this stage is honorable. The diligence work itself contributes to the Codex. Communities and potential partners are informed honestly per Public Communication Doctrine §13.

## 5. Stage 3 — Partnership Formation

### 5.1 Purpose

Establish the core relationships through which the engagement will operate. Move from "candidate partners" to "committed partners."

### 5.2 Entry Criteria

- Diligence complete.
- Engagement authorized.
- Specific partners identified.

### 5.3 The Three Critical Partnership Categories

#### 5.3.1 Community Partnership

The most consequential. Where indigenous or local communities are involved (the typical case), this is the foundation.

**Engagement:**

- Initial sustained conversations led by community liaisons, not Robotanica leadership.
- Disclosure of Charter, mission, and what engagement would entail.
- Listening — communities articulate their priorities, concerns, conditions.
- Co-development of how restoration would work in this specific context.
- Multiple meetings over months, in the community's preferred language and forums.
- No pressure for decisions on Robotanica's timeline.

**Output:**

- Mutual understanding documented in a Partnership Memorandum.
- Specific terms negotiated for benefit-sharing, data sovereignty, decision-making, and community involvement.
- Co-developed plan for FPIC formal process.

#### 5.3.2 Operator Partnership

The federation member(s) who will perform day-to-day work.

**Engagement:**

- Federation Membership Agreement reviewed and accepted.
- Operator capabilities verified (capacity for proposed scale, recipe expertise, equipment).
- Operator-Landowner relationships compatible with intended Lease.
- Operator-community relationships compatible with FPIC and ongoing partnership.

**Output:**

- Operator commitment as federation member (Federation Membership Agreement signed).
- Operator scope defined for the engagement.

#### 5.3.3 Landowner Partnership

The party with formal title, where this is distinct from the community.

**Engagement:**

- Initial outreach explaining the project and proposed Lease structure.
- Detailed conversations on terms, compensation, monitoring, succession.
- Landowner consultation with their own advisors (legal, financial).
- No pressure for decisions; Landowner has full information.

**Output:**

- Landowner candidate for Lease formation in Stage 4.

### 5.4 Other Partnerships

Additional partners may be engaged at this stage:

- Scientific institutions (universities, research centers) for technical support and verification.
- Government counterparts (environmental ministries, local authorities).
- Financial partners (regional banks, payment processors).
- Mission-aligned NGOs operating in the region.

### 5.5 Output

- Partnership Memoranda with each critical partnership category.
- Federation Membership Agreement signed by Operator(s).
- Government and other relationships engaged at appropriate level.
- Specific timeline for Stage 4 Lease formation.

### 5.6 Decision Bodies

Stewardship Office leads partnership formation. Federation governance reviews and ratifies major commitments. Council reviews ecological alignment.

### 5.7 Exit Criteria

- Community partnership established with documented FPIC pathway.
- Operator relationship established.
- Landowner candidate ready for Lease conversation.
- Other relationships at appropriate level.

### 5.8 Time Frame

Approximately 4-9 months. The community partnership is the time-determining element. Indigenous FPIC processes that move faster than the community itself wishes are violations.

## 6. Stage 4 — Lease and Consent

### 6.1 Purpose

Form the legal instrument (Lease) through which the engagement operates, with all required consents.

### 6.2 Entry Criteria

- Partnerships established.
- Jurisdictional Lease variant available (drafted by counsel in Stage 2).
- Recipe candidate ready for binding.
- FPIC process underway.

### 6.3 The Lease Formation Process

#### 6.3.1 Lease Drafting

- Counsel adapts the jurisdictional variant to the specific Lease (parties, parcel, recipe, term, compensation).
- Draft reviewed by:
  - Landowner with their counsel.
  - Indigenous or local community with their counsel and governance.
  - Federation parties.
  - Operator parties.

#### 6.3.2 Recipe Binding

- Specific recipe selected from Codex (with version).
- Recipe applicability reviewed by Council if any concerns.
- Recipe instance created in Gaia, linking the Lease to the recipe at version.

#### 6.3.3 FPIC Formal Process

Where applicable, FPIC follows the community's own governance process:

- Formal notification to the community.
- Community consultation period per their governance.
- Community decision through their processes.
- Formal recording of consent in A3 with full provenance.

The FPIC process cannot be rushed for Robotanica's timeline.

#### 6.3.4 Other Consents

- Sovereignty declarations recorded (per Protocol §10).
- Public registry registration where applicable.
- Required regulatory consents in the jurisdiction.

#### 6.3.5 Signing Ceremony

The signing of the Lease is itself meaningful:

- Conducted in person where feasible.
- Witnessed by community elders, government counterparts, and other relevant parties.
- Cryptographically recorded in A3 with all signatures.
- Public record entry created.

### 6.4 Output

- Lease in `signed` state in A3.
- Recipe instance bound in Gaia.
- All consent records preserved.
- Sovereignty declaration in place.
- Public record entries.

### 6.5 Decision Bodies

Stewardship Office holds the Lease as Federation counterparty. Federation governance ratifies the engagement. Council confirms recipe binding.

### 6.6 Exit Criteria

- Lease signed.
- Recipe bound.
- Consents recorded.
- Authorization to proceed to operations.

### 6.7 Time Frame

Approximately 3-6 months. FPIC process is the primary determinant.

### 6.8 No Lease Without FPIC

Where FPIC applies and is not given, the Lease is not formed. This is structural per Charter §3.3 and Lease Requirements §3.2. Communities have no obligation to consent on Robotanica's timeline; if consent is not given, the engagement does not proceed in this form.

## 7. Stage 5 — Operational Launch

### 7.1 Purpose

Begin actual operations: site preparation, biological production, planting, and establishment of monitoring.

### 7.2 Entry Criteria

- Lease signed.
- Recipe bound.
- Operator capacity ready.
- Biological inputs available.
- Field operations team in place.

### 7.3 Pre-Planting Operations

#### 7.3.1 Site Preparation

Per recipe specifications:

- Soil assessment and preparation.
- Invasive removal where needed.
- Hydrological work where needed.
- Fencing or protection where needed.

Site preparation is recorded in OpenFactory as Operations linked to the Mission.

#### 7.3.2 Biological Production Initiation

- Biological inputs (seedlings, inoculants, etc.) sourced or produced.
- Provenance documented per Genetic Strategy.
- Biosecurity protocols followed.
- Quality checks performed and recorded.

#### 7.3.3 Sensor and Monitoring Deployment

- Environmental sensors deployed per recipe and MRV protocol.
- Connection to Gaia established.
- Baseline observations recorded.

#### 7.3.4 Verifier Engagement

- Independent verifier identified and accredited per MRV §7.
- Verifier engaged for initial site verification.
- Baseline verification documented.

### 7.4 Planting

The actual restoration intervention:

- Conducted per recipe specifications.
- Cohort created in Gaia at planting.
- Each Operation recorded with full provenance.
- BiologicalBatch genealogy preserved through to Cohort transition (per OpenFactory §6.6).
- Holobiont supplementation per recipe.

### 7.5 Initial Monitoring

Establishment-period monitoring begins immediately:

- Daily or weekly site visits during the establishment period.
- Sensor data flowing to Gaia.
- Photographs and qualitative observations recorded.
- Operator reports to Federation per ServiceContract.

### 7.6 Output

- Cohort established (or in establishment).
- Full operational genealogy in Gaia.
- Active monitoring ongoing.
- ServiceContract in execution per A3.

### 7.7 Exit Criteria

- Establishment-period monitoring underway.
- Operations stabilized in routine cadence.
- No immediate dealbreakers (catastrophic site failure, security incident).

### 7.8 Time Frame

Operational launch period is recipe-specific:

- Tropical fast-establishing recipes: weeks for planting, months for establishment confirmation.
- Temperate slower-establishing recipes: planting in appropriate season, establishment over a full year.
- Boreal recipes: limited to short operational windows; multi-year establishment.

## 8. Stage 6 — Pilot Year

### 8.1 Purpose

The first full year (or seasonal cycle) of operations. Demonstrate the engagement at sustained operation, accumulate evidence, identify issues for adjustment.

### 8.2 The First-Year Cycle

#### 8.2.1 Routine Operations

- Tending operations per recipe (irrigation, weeding, pest management, supplementation).
- Continuous monitoring through sensors and field observations.
- Quarterly operational reports.
- Anomaly response when issues arise.

#### 8.2.2 First Verification Cycle

- First routine verification by independent verifier.
- Establishment rates measured and reported.
- Verification report published (subject to sovereignty constraints).

#### 8.2.3 Community Engagement Continuing

- Quarterly or appropriate-cadence engagement with community partners.
- Co-review of progress and any concerns.
- Adjustment of approach where community input warrants.

#### 8.2.4 Federation Reporting

- Routine reports to Federation governance.
- Public record updates.
- Lessons captured for Codex and other engagements.

### 8.3 First-Year Decisions

#### 8.3.1 Recipe Adaptation

If the recipe is producing outcomes inconsistent with expectations:

- Operational deviation logged.
- Council review for material changes.
- Recipe revision proposal where needed.

#### 8.3.2 Scale Adjustment

The pilot may scale up (more parcels under similar terms), scale down (operational stretch), or hold (continue at pilot scale until more is learned).

#### 8.3.3 Partnership Adjustment

Partnerships may need adjustment based on first-year experience. Adjustments are made in good faith with affected parties.

### 8.4 Output

- First-year operational record complete.
- First verification cycle complete.
- Cohort established and entering juvenile phase.
- Lessons captured.
- Decision on continuation or adjustment.

### 8.5 Exit Criteria

- First-year cycle complete.
- Establishment rates documented.
- Decision made on Stage 7 trajectory.

### 8.6 Time Frame

12-18 months from operational launch. May extend for slower-establishing recipes.

## 9. Stage 7 — Continuation, Expansion, or Withdrawal

### 9.1 Purpose

Decide the engagement's trajectory beyond pilot. Multi-year continuation, expansion to additional parcels, or honorable withdrawal.

### 9.2 Decision Criteria

#### 9.2.1 Outcomes

- Establishment rates within or above target.
- Recipe performing as expected.
- No material methodology issues.

#### 9.2.2 Partnerships

- Community partnership healthy.
- Operator partnership effective.
- Landowner relationship constructive.

#### 9.2.3 Strategic Fit

- Engagement still aligned with Federation priorities.
- Capacity available for continuation or expansion.
- Funding available for continuation or expansion.

### 9.3 Continuation

If continuation is decided:

- Existing Lease continues per its term.
- Multi-year operational plan developed.
- Cohort moves into long-horizon stewardship phase.

### 9.4 Expansion

If expansion is decided:

- Additional parcels engaged (return to Stage 1 for new Leases within same region).
- Capacity scaled to support expansion.
- Lessons from pilot informing expansion.

### 9.5 Honorable Withdrawal

If withdrawal is decided:

- Lease termination through agreed procedures (Lease Requirements §6).
- Transition planning to ensure parcel is not abandoned (Lease Requirements §6.6).
- Succession to other federation members where applicable.
- Honest communication with community, Landowner, and federation about withdrawal reasoning.
- Lessons captured for the corpus.

Withdrawal is sometimes the right answer. The Charter accepts this. Withdrawal done well is honorable; withdrawal done poorly violates the no-abandonment provision.

## 10. Cross-Cutting Concerns

### 10.1 Communication

Per the Public Communication Doctrine throughout. Specifically:

- Stage 1 candidacy and decisions are recorded in the public record.
- Stage 2 diligence outputs are summarized in the public record (subject to sovereignty constraints).
- Stage 3 partnerships are announced as agreed by partners.
- Stage 4 Lease signing is publicly recorded.
- Stage 5 operational launch is publicly noted.
- Stages 6-7 outcomes are reported on routine cadence.

### 10.2 Indigenous and Community Sovereignty

Sovereignty is honored at every stage:

- Stage 2 diligence respects community decisions about what is and is not researched.
- Stage 3 partnership formation operates on community time, not Robotanica time.
- Stage 4 FPIC is structural; no Lease without it where applicable.
- Stage 5+ operations honor sovereignty in data, decisions, and adaptations.

### 10.3 Worker Safety

Per Personnel Philosophy §8:

- Risk assessment per region before deployment.
- Safety protocols documented.
- Refusal authority for personnel.
- Evacuation provisions where applicable.

### 10.4 Risk Management

Risks identified in diligence are tracked through operations. Materialization triggers response per documented protocols. Catastrophic risks may necessitate honorable withdrawal.

### 10.5 Documentation

Throughout, full documentation in:

- Gaia (observations, parcel and cohort lifecycle).
- A3 (Lease, governance decisions, dispute records).
- OpenFactory (production runs, operations).
- Public record (governance decisions, milestone events).

This is operational discipline; documentation creates the multi-decade durability the rest of the corpus depends on.

### 10.6 Personnel

Personnel deployed to engagements are supported per the Personnel Philosophy:

- Compensated per regional dignity floor and parity rules.
- Family support where relocations occur.
- Connection to community structures preserved.

## 11. Pilot-Specific Considerations

The first-wave pilots (per Founding Capital Plan) are special:

### 11.1 Higher Resource Intensity

Pilots are indirect-cost-heavy per the Unit Economics Model. Pilot per-hectare costs are not representative of mature operations.

### 11.2 Higher Visibility

First pilots will be observed closely by funders, media, federation prospects, and external critics. Public communication discipline (per Public Communication Doctrine) is especially important.

### 11.3 More Flexibility

Pilots are explicitly experimental. Adjustments are expected. The Theory of Change is being tested in concrete form.

### 11.4 Dual Outcomes

Pilots aim for both:

- **Ecological outcomes** at the parcel: cohort establishment and trajectory.
- **Methodological outcomes** for the federation: lessons that improve subsequent engagements.

Both are valuable; failure on one but success on the other is partial success.

### 11.5 First-Wave Selection

Per the Bioregional Strategy §6.2, the first wave demonstrates:

- At least three different bioregional categories.
- At least one with significant indigenous and community partnership.
- At least one Global South and one Global North region.
- No concentration in any single jurisdiction.

This requires multiple parallel engagements in Stages 1-3, with first signed Leases sequenced based on partnership readiness.

## 12. The Founding Sequence

Concretely, the first pilot operations follow this sequence:

### 12.1 Months 0-3 (Phase 0 of Founding Capital Plan)

- Three to five candidate regions identified per Bioregional Strategy.
- Initial outreach to candidate partners and communities.
- Initial counsel engagements for Lease drafting.

### 12.2 Months 3-9

- Diligence advances on top 2-3 candidates.
- First jurisdictional Lease drafts developed.
- Partnership conversations deepen.

### 12.3 Months 9-15

- First diligence reports complete.
- First partnership formations.
- First FPIC processes underway.

### 12.4 Months 15-21

- First Leases signed.
- First operational launches.
- First Cohorts established.

### 12.5 Months 21-30

- First-year cycles complete.
- First verifications complete.
- First decisions on continuation, expansion, or withdrawal.

### 12.6 Months 30+

- Continued operations across multiple regions.
- Federation membership growing beyond pilot operators.
- Cross-regional learning integrated into Codex.

This sequence will not survive contact with reality. It is the plan.

## 13. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in pilot operations:

- **Right number of first-wave engagements.** Three? Five? Seven? Too few risks concentration; too many risks operational stretch.
- **Sequencing of FPIC vs. legal drafting.** Both must happen; whether one substantially precedes the other depends on context.
- **Verifier accreditation for first pilots.** Verifier capacity may not exist in some regions; first verifications may rely on imported verifiers with cooling-off periods.
- **Government engagement timing.** Some governments require early engagement; others react to fait accompli. The right approach varies.
- **Operator-Landowner-Community three-way dynamics.** Where operators and Landowners and communities are distinct, the three-way relationship is complex. The Lease handles the legal aspects; the operational aspects are subtler.
- **Recipe development versus selection.** Some pilot regions may have no fully-developed Codex recipe yet; pilot operations may need to co-develop recipes with the Codex itself in flight.
- **Crisis during pilot.** Pilots are visible; if a pilot has a public failure (catastrophic site loss, community dispute), the public dimension is heightened. Crisis communication discipline (Public Communication Doctrine §8) is especially important.
- **Federation structure during early operations.** Many federation governance bodies are still being established during first pilots. Decisions normally made by these bodies may need transitional handling.

## Appendix D — Glossary (Pilot-Specific)

- **Candidacy** — A region under consideration for engagement, before commitment.
- **Diligence team** — Cross-functional team conducting Stage 2 work.
- **Engagement** — A specific Robotanica engagement with a region, typically through one or more Leases.
- **First-wave region** — A region in the founding-period set of pilot engagements.
- **First-year cycle** — The first 12-18 months of operations after launch.
- **FPIC formal process** — The community-governance-led consent process for Lease formation.
- **Operational launch** — The transition from preparation to active operations.
- **Partnership Memorandum** — Documented mutual understanding with a critical partner before formal Lease.
- **Pilot operations** — Phase 1 / early Phase 2 operations characterized by experimental nature and high indirect cost.

---

*A pilot is the moment when the corpus meets the world. It is the test of whether the Charter, the protocols, the platforms, and the disciplines actually work when applied to an actual parcel with actual people facing actual ecological complexity. The Protocol does not specify what happens; it specifies the structure within which what happens is held responsible to what was promised. Specific engagements adapt; the structure endures.*
