# Funding Strategy

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Strategic and governance specification
**Status:** Draft for ratification
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Threat Model, MRV Specification, A3 PRD, Federation Membership Agreement

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies how Robotanica is funded. It defines:

- The kinds of funding that are compatible with the mission.
- The kinds that are explicitly refused.
- How funding diversity is maintained structurally.
- How funding decisions are governed.
- How accountability flows from funders through the project to ecological outcomes and back.

The Charter (§3.6) commits Robotanica to "refuse funding whose conditions would compromise the principles of Article II or the commitments of Article III." This document operationalizes that commitment.

The Threat Model identifies single-point funders (§3.8) and greenwashing partnerships (§6.2) as among the most consequential capture risks. This document is a primary structural defense against those risks.

The trillion-tree objective requires capital at a scale that no single source can provide. The funding strategy must therefore be inherently diverse. But diversity alone is not enough: the wrong mix of small captures is still capture. The strategy must be diverse *and* disciplined.

---

## 2. The Capital Problem

### 2.1 Scale

Restoring biosphere at the scale Robotanica targets requires capital flowing across decades. Order-of-magnitude estimates suggest:

- Initial platform development: hundreds of millions of dollars over five years.
- Pilot operations and first-region scaling: low billions over a decade.
- Full-scale operations toward the headline OKR: trillions over multiple decades.

These numbers are not strategy — they are scope. The strategy is what kinds of capital can flow at this scale without compromising the mission.

### 2.2 Time Horizon

Capital must support a horizon longer than any single funder is structurally aligned to. Most philanthropic funders operate on 3-10 year horizons. Most patient capital on 7-15 years. Sovereign instruments can extend further but introduce political volatility. The trillion-tree horizon is multi-decadal.

The strategy must therefore include mechanisms that re-fund continuously, not single instruments that pay for everything.

### 2.3 Mission Discipline

Capital comes with conditions. Even unconditional capital comes with relationship — the expectation that the funder remains pleased, the avoidance of decisions that would offend, the gradual accommodation of preferences.

The strategy must structurally limit any one funder's leverage and explicitly refuse capital whose terms would compromise the work.

## 3. Funding Principles

These principles bind funding decisions and may not be set aside for capital convenience.

### 3.1 No Single-Source Dominance

**FUND-001** No single funder, foundation, or aligned cluster of funders may provide more than **20% of total Robotanica capital** at any time, measured across operating funds, capital projects, and credit pre-purchases.

**FUND-002** Where temporary deviations are unavoidable (founding, crisis), they are time-bound (maximum two years), publicly disclosed, and subject to active rebalancing plans.

### 3.2 Transparency by Default

**FUND-010** All funding relationships above a defined materiality threshold (default: $250,000 annually or any equity stake) are disclosed in the public record.

**FUND-011** Disclosure includes: the funder, the amount and form, the term, the conditions if any, the use of funds.

### 3.3 Mission Compatibility

**FUND-020** Funding may not be accepted from sources whose primary activity is incompatible with biosphere restoration, regardless of the form or attractiveness of the offer.

**FUND-021** "Primary activity" is judged by the funder's business model, ongoing practices, and stated direction — not by isolated investments, public commitments, or aspirational statements.

### 3.4 Mission Sovereignty

**FUND-030** Funding may not grant operational, governance, or selection rights beyond those publicly defined for the funder's category in the Federation Membership Agreement.

**FUND-031** Funding may not grant exclusive rights to recipes, biological material, data, or platform features that the Charter requires to remain open (Charter §5.3).

### 3.5 Reversibility

**FUND-040** Funding terms must be reversible: Robotanica must be able to return funds and exit the relationship if conditions prove incompatible with the mission, even at material cost.

**FUND-041** Multi-year funding commitments may not include penalties so severe that exit becomes impractical.

### 3.6 Beneficiary Discipline

**FUND-050** Where funding generates outputs benefiting the funder (carbon credits, ecosystem service claims, brand association), the benefit is proportionate to the funding and never exceeds what the underlying work actually delivers, per the MRV Specification.

**FUND-051** Funding does not entitle funders to outcomes that would not have occurred without their contribution; additionality discipline applies.

## 4. Funding Sources

Funding sources are organized by their structural relationship to the mission. Each category has different alignment characteristics and different capture risks.

### 4.1 Founding and Patient Capital

Capital provided with explicit acceptance of the long horizon and modest financial return.

**Forms:**

- **Philanthropic grants** from foundations and high-net-worth individuals committed to ecological restoration.
- **Patient equity** from mission-aligned investors accepting decade-plus horizons and target returns substantially below market rate.
- **Sovereign and multilateral grants** from governments and multilateral institutions with restoration mandates (Green Climate Fund, World Bank Forest Carbon Partnership, regional development banks).

**Role:** Founding and scaling capital. Funds platform development, pilot operations, and capacity-building work that does not yet generate operating revenue.

**Capture risks:** Donor preferences shape strategy. Philanthropic capital from a single major source creates dependence. Multilateral funding can come with policy conditionality or jurisdictional constraints.

**Mitigations:** Diversify across philanthropic sources; cap any single foundation's contribution per FUND-001; maintain at least three independent founding funders during early phases.

### 4.2 Restoration-Linked Revenue

Capital generated by the work itself, paid in exchange for verified outcomes.

**Forms:**

- **Carbon credit revenue** from credits issued under MRV discipline and sold into voluntary or compliance markets.
- **Biodiversity credit revenue** from emerging biodiversity markets where the methodology is defensible.
- **Ecosystem service payments** from beneficiaries of specific ecosystem services (downstream water users, pollination beneficiaries, flood-risk-reduced communities).
- **Resilience and adaptation funding** from public and private sources investing in climate resilience.

**Role:** Sustaining operating revenue once outcomes are demonstrable. Replaces founding capital over time.

**Capture risks:** Carbon and biodiversity markets are at risk of methodological erosion under buyer pressure. Buyers prefer cheaper credits; cheaper credits often mean less rigorous MRV. Robotanica's MRV discipline may make its credits more expensive than competitors', creating market pressure to relax.

**Mitigations:** MRV Specification's discipline is non-negotiable; credits are issued only on verified outcomes (KP-021); progressive issuance and reconciliation reserve protect against permanence pressure (KP-022, KP-050); refuse to participate in markets whose discipline is structurally too weak.

### 4.3 Sovereign and Public Investment

Capital from governments, sovereign wealth funds, and public institutions.

**Forms:**

- **Sovereign biosphere bonds** — long-duration (50+ year) sovereign debt instruments where principal and interest are conditional on verified ecological outcomes. (Currently theoretical; possible to develop with willing sovereign partners.)
- **National restoration budgets** — direct government appropriations for restoration work in their territory.
- **Multilateral development bank financing** — concessional loans and grants from World Bank, regional development banks, and similar institutions.
- **Procurement contracts** — governments paying for restoration as a public service in defined territories.

**Role:** Large-scale, long-horizon capital. Particularly valuable for restoration in jurisdictions where the public sector is the appropriate party to commission the work.

**Capture risks:** Political volatility (changes of government can disrupt funding). Conditionality (loans often come with macroeconomic policy conditions unrelated to restoration). Sovereign immunity complications.

**Mitigations:** Diversify across sovereign sources (FUND-001 caps any single source); bilateral terms protecting Robotanica's mission in case of political change; preference for grants and outcome-conditional bonds over conventional sovereign loans.

### 4.4 Commercial Partnerships

Capital from companies engaged with Robotanica's work in business-aligned ways.

**Forms:**

- **Companies whose primary business depends on biosphere health** (regenerative agriculture cooperatives, ecotourism, sustainably-managed forestry, water utilities) — partnership where business interests and Robotanica's mission align.
- **Companies seeking insetting** — companies with sourcing relationships where restoration in their supply chain serves both their and Robotanica's interests.
- **Companies purchasing credits at scale** — enterprise buyers of carbon or biodiversity credits, treating them as part of restoration-linked revenue (§4.2) rather than greenwashing.

**Role:** Significant scaling capital with appropriate business alignment.

**Capture risks:** This is the highest-capture category. The line between alignment and greenwashing is contested and shifts. Major emitters offer attractive partnerships that test the boundary.

**Mitigations:** Categorical refusals (§5); structured review of partnership offers by Stewardship Office and Federation governance; commercial terms that limit funder leverage; transparent disclosure of all major partnerships and the rationale for accepting.

### 4.5 Operating Revenue

Capital from services Robotanica directly provides.

**Forms:**

- **Federation membership fees** at scales appropriate to member category and capacity (small operators pay nominal fees; large institutional members contribute proportionately).
- **Service revenue** from technical assistance, MRV consulting, and platform-as-a-service offerings to non-Federation parties.
- **Training and certification revenue** for operators, verifiers, and scientific contributors.
- **Data and research revenue** from non-sovereign data made available to commercial parties under defined terms.

**Role:** Sustainable baseline operating capital, less subject to the cycles of philanthropy and credit markets.

**Capture risks:** Lower than other categories but real. Dependence on a small number of paying federation members can create indirect leverage.

**Mitigations:** Fee structures designed to broadly distribute revenue; refuse service relationships that compromise mission; member fee scales that don't favor wealthy members disproportionately.

### 4.6 Community and Crowd Capital

Capital from individuals and aligned communities supporting the work directly.

**Forms:**

- **Crowdfunding** at modest individual amounts, aggregating to material totals.
- **Community shares and cooperative capital** from groups investing collectively.
- **Indigenous and local community co-investment** — communities investing in restoration on their own lands with appropriate financial returns.

**Role:** Mission-aligned capital that broadens the base of stakeholders. Modest scale individually; significant in aggregate over time.

**Capture risks:** Low. Distributed by nature.

**Mitigations:** Standard crowd-funding governance; transparent reporting; discipline in not over-promising returns to small investors.

### 4.7 Reserve and Contingency Capital

Capital held in reserve against specific risks and obligations.

**Forms:**

- **Reconciliation reserve** (per MRV §10.3 and A3 §6.6) — credits and equivalent value held against future reconciliation events.
- **Operational reserves** — sufficient to operate through funding disruptions for at least 12-18 months.
- **Catastrophe insurance and reinsurance** — purchased coverage for catastrophic site loss, jurisdiction failure, or other large discrete events.

**Role:** Protection against discrete shocks; preservation of long-term operational continuity.

**Capture risks:** Insurance markets may not price restoration-related risks rationally; reserve management creates investment-decision pressure.

**Mitigations:** Reserves held in conservatively-managed instruments aligned to the mission (no fossil-fuel or extractive-industry exposure); insurance purchased only where the underlying market is honest; Federation-level mutual arrangements where commercial markets are absent.

## 5. Categorical Refusals

The following funding categories are categorically refused. The list is not exhaustive but is binding.

### 5.1 Fossil Fuel Industry

**REF-001** Funding from companies whose primary business is fossil-fuel extraction, refining, or fossil-fuel-dominated electricity generation is refused, regardless of the company's restoration commitments or branding initiatives. This refusal applies to funding offered through subsidiaries, foundations, or affiliated entities where the funding traces to fossil-fuel revenue.

### 5.2 Industrial Agriculture and Forestry Without Regenerative Discipline

**REF-002** Funding from large-scale industrial agriculture or industrial forestry companies whose practices are fundamentally extractive (monoculture-dominant, soil-degrading, dependent on synthetic chemistry without transition commitments) is refused. Funding from companies in these industries that are credibly transitioning to regenerative practices is reviewed case-by-case.

### 5.3 Mining and Extractive Industries Without Restoration Discipline

**REF-003** Funding from mining and extractive industries is refused absent demonstrable, audited restoration discipline in their core operations. The Federation's restoration work cannot serve to legitimize ongoing extraction without restoration.

### 5.4 Authoritarian Sovereign Sources

**REF-004** Funding from sovereign sources that are documented authoritarian regimes is refused, regardless of the apparent absence of conditions. The relationship itself creates capture risk that conditions cannot remove.

### 5.5 Funding Requiring Methodology Compromise

**REF-005** Funding requiring weakened MRV, weakened sovereignty protection, weakened Charter principles, or any other compromise of the protocol is refused, regardless of size.

### 5.6 Funding Requiring Exclusive Commercial Rights

**REF-006** Funding requiring exclusive commercial rights to recipes, biological material, software, data, or platform features that the Charter requires to remain open is refused.

### 5.7 Funding Requiring Concealment

**REF-007** Funding requiring concealment of the relationship, of failures, or of any other category of information that the Charter requires to be public is refused.

### 5.8 Funding from Parties Under Active Investigation

**REF-008** Funding from parties under active major investigation or litigation related to environmental harm, indigenous rights violations, or comparable matters is paused pending resolution. This is not categorical refusal; it is a hold pending evidence.

### 5.9 Procedural Refusal Discipline

**REF-010** Refusals are decided by the Federation governance body informed by the Stewardship Office and, where mission-relevance warrants, the Science Council.

**REF-011** Refusals are recorded in the public record (with funder identity disclosed unless the funder requests confidentiality and the Federation grants it; refusals to disclose the disclosure decision itself are not granted).

**REF-012** Refusals are not appealable by the offering party. They may be revisited if the underlying conditions change materially.

## 6. Structural Defenses

### 6.1 Funding Diversity Mandate

**STR-001** At all times, Robotanica maintains funding from at least five independent sources across at least three categories (per §4). Periodic dependence-risk review by the Stewardship Office monitors compliance.

### 6.2 Funding Committee Independence

**STR-010** Major funding decisions (single sources above a defined threshold; new funder categories; partnerships of strategic significance) are reviewed by a Funding Committee:

- The Funding Committee is appointed by the Federation governance body.
- The Committee includes representation from the Stewardship Office, the Science Council (as applicable), and at least one external independent member with no funder ties.
- Operational leadership advises but does not vote.

**STR-011** Committee decisions are recorded as `GovernanceDecision` entities and published in the public record.

### 6.3 Funder-Operator Separation

**STR-020** Funders may not also serve as Operators on parcels their funding supports. This separation prevents the structural conflict of self-funding self-validating restoration.

**STR-021** Where overlapping interests are unavoidable (e.g., a community providing both funding and stewardship), the overlap is structured with additional verification independence.

### 6.4 Annual Funding Review

**STR-030** The Stewardship Office publishes an annual review of:

- Total capital raised, by category and funder
- Concentration metrics (largest funder share, top-five share)
- Refusal record (categories of refusals issued)
- Dependence-risk assessment
- Forward funding plan and capital needs

**STR-031** The review is public.

### 6.5 Funder Sunset

**STR-040** Major funder relationships are reviewed every five years. Continued funding is not automatic; renewal requires affirmative review of mission alignment, conduct, and continuing fit.

### 6.6 Reconciliation Reserve Funding

**STR-050** The reconciliation reserve (per MRV §10.3 and A3 §6.6) is funded as a defined fraction of credit revenues, before any other downstream allocation. The reserve is structurally insulated from operational pressure to draw it down.

## 7. Phasing

| Phase | Primary Capital Sources | Secondary Sources | Capital Needs |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 — Founding | Philanthropic; patient equity; founder capital | Crowd-funding for community alignment | Platform development, founding team, first jurisdictional Lease drafting, first pilot |
| 1 — Pilot | Philanthropic; first sovereign grants | First small operating revenue; first credit issuance | Pilot operations, scaling team, first verifier accreditations |
| 2 — Federation | Diversified philanthropic + sovereign + initial commercial partnerships | Operating revenue at scale; mature credit issuance | Multi-region operations, expanding fleet, federation onboarding |
| 3 — Multi-region scale | Mature credit revenue; sovereign biosphere bonds (if developed); operating revenue | Continuing philanthropic; commercial partnerships under discipline | Planetary fleet operations, biological production at scale |
| 4 — Sustaining | Operating revenue + credit revenue + ecosystem service payments | Continuing diverse sources for resilience | Continuing operations; reconciliation reserves; long-term stewardship |

Phase boundaries are exit criteria, not dates. Capital strategy adapts to phase but the principles do not change across phases.

## 8. Compensation Flow Discipline

How capital flows downstream is as important as how it comes in.

### 8.1 Landowner Compensation

**COMP-001** Landowners are compensated per the Stewardship Lease (Lease Requirements §9). Lease structures may not be free-land arrangements (Lease Requirements §9.2); compensation is material.

**COMP-002** Where Robotanica receives external funding for restoration on a Landowner's parcel, a defined fraction passes through to the Landowner in lease payments, revenue shares, or in-kind benefits.

### 8.2 Indigenous and Community Benefit-Sharing

**COMP-010** Where parcels involve indigenous or local communities, benefit-sharing per Lease Requirements §9.3 is structural, not optional. Defined fractions of revenue and outputs flow to communities on terms negotiated under FPIC.

**COMP-011** Community benefit-sharing has priority over funder returns: where revenue is constrained, community shares are paid before funder returns are reduced beyond the floor.

### 8.3 Operator Compensation

**COMP-020** Operators are compensated per Service Contracts under the Lease. Compensation includes margins sufficient for sustainable operation, not extraction.

**COMP-021** Operator compensation may include performance components, but never structured to incentivize quality compromise or methodology compromise.

### 8.4 Personnel Compensation

**COMP-030** Robotanica personnel and Federation personnel are paid fairly per Charter §3.5, including those whose labor occurs in remote regions and through long quiet years of monitoring.

**COMP-031** Compensation philosophy is detailed in the forthcoming Personnel and Compensation Philosophy document; this Strategy refers to it.

### 8.5 No Surplus Extraction

**COMP-040** Where funding generates surplus beyond operational and reserve needs, surplus is reinvested in mission-aligned activities (additional restoration, R&D, capacity-building, reserve increases) rather than distributed.

**COMP-041** Equity holders' returns (where equity exists) are bounded; Robotanica is not structured to maximize shareholder return.

## 9. Funder Transparency and Accountability

### 9.1 Funder Disclosure

**TRANS-001** Funders above the materiality threshold (per §3.2 FUND-010) are publicly disclosed.

**TRANS-002** Disclosures include the funder's other major activities (in summary), to surface potential mission tension.

### 9.2 Use-of-Funds Reporting

**TRANS-010** Funders receive use-of-funds reports proportionate to their funding scope. Reports use the same metrics and discipline as the public record; funders do not receive favorable accounting.

**TRANS-011** Where funders are entitled to specific outcome reports (e.g., credit purchasers receiving verification reports), the reports follow MRV specifications.

### 9.3 Funder Engagement

**TRANS-020** Funders may meet with Robotanica leadership, may participate in defined Federation forums, may comment publicly. They may not direct operations, governance, or research.

**TRANS-021** Funder engagement is documented; significant engagement is summarized in the public record.

### 9.4 Funder Discipline

**TRANS-030** Funders found to be using their funding to attempt mission compromise (REF-005, indirect pressure, public-facing campaigns inconsistent with the Charter) are subject to relationship termination, with funds returned per the reversibility commitment (§3.5).

## 10. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions where decisions will have outsized downstream impact:

- **Sovereign biosphere bond design.** No precedent exists at scale. Designing an instrument that pays principal and interest based on verified ecological outcomes, with appropriate sovereign credit-risk treatment, is a substantial financial-engineering project. Pilot designs with willing sovereign partners are a precondition to relying on this category.
- **Carbon and biodiversity market evolution.** The discipline of these markets is unsettled. Robotanica may need to lead market reform, or operate outside formal markets, depending on how reform progresses.
- **Indigenous community capital structures.** How indigenous communities co-invest in restoration on their own lands and receive returns aligned to their governance is a category of work that crosses finance, sovereignty, and customary law. Specific instrument designs are needed per community.
- **Insetting versus offsetting.** Companies seeking insetting (emissions reductions in their own supply chains via Robotanica's work) versus offsetting (purchasing credits to offset unmodified emissions) deserve different treatment. The boundary is contested and must be specified.
- **Funder concentration during founding.** The 20% cap (FUND-001) may be impractical during founding. Transitional provisions are needed; their abuse must be guarded against.
- **Compensation philosophy across regions and roles.** Personnel compensation is referenced; the underlying philosophy is in another document. Open question: how is field-steward compensation in low-cost regions calibrated relative to engineer compensation in high-cost regions, fairly?
- **Patient equity at scale.** Patient capital exists in modest amounts. The strategy assumes its availability at scale; this assumption may be optimistic.
- **Greenwashing line in concrete cases.** §5.1-5.3 give principles; specific cases will test them. The decision-making process is specified, but the line itself will be drawn case-by-case in the public record.
- **Reserve sizing.** What fraction of credit revenue should flow to reconciliation reserve? Actuarial work is needed.

## Appendix A — Refusal Examples

To illustrate the refusal discipline, the following hypothetical offers would be refused under §5:

- A $500M ten-year offer from a major oil company in exchange for co-branded restoration initiatives. Refused under REF-001.
- A $100M grant from an industrial-agriculture company in exchange for prominent placement on outcome reports. Reviewed; refused under REF-002 unless the company meets transition criteria.
- A $1B sovereign loan from an authoritarian government with no explicit conditions. Refused under REF-004.
- A $50M offer from a green-technology company conditioned on exclusive licensing of Robotanica's robotic seedling-planter design. Refused under REF-006.
- A $20M offer with a confidentiality clause prohibiting disclosure of the funding amount. Refused under REF-007.

The following hypothetical offers would not be categorically refused, though each may face substantive review:

- A $100M philanthropic grant from a major foundation focused on biodiversity. Reviewed for concentration risk and conditions.
- A $200M mission-aligned investment from a regenerative agriculture cooperative. Reviewed for funder-operator separation.
- A $50M pre-purchase of carbon credits by an enterprise buyer. Reviewed for insetting/offsetting framing and MRV alignment.
- A $30M sovereign grant from a democracy with strong restoration policy. Reviewed for conditionality and political volatility.

## Appendix B — Glossary (Funding-Specific)

- **Founding capital** — Capital provided during early phases before significant operating revenue exists.
- **Funding Committee** — The committee reviewing major funding decisions per §6.2.
- **Funding diversity mandate** — The structural requirement of multiple independent sources per §6.1.
- **Insetting** — Emissions reductions within a company's own supply chain.
- **Materiality threshold** — The level above which funding relationships require public disclosure (default: $250,000 annually or any equity stake).
- **Offsetting** — Purchasing credits to offset emissions occurring outside the buyer's operations.
- **Operating revenue** — Capital from services Robotanica directly provides.
- **Patient capital** — Capital with explicit acceptance of long horizons and modest returns.
- **Reconciliation reserve** — Reserves held against future reconciliation events per MRV §10.3.
- **Restoration-linked revenue** — Capital paid in exchange for verified ecological outcomes.
