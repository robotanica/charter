# The Long Care

## A manifesto

---

The biosphere is the thin living layer of the world. It is failing. We are failing it.

The damage is not abstract. It is in specific places: the deforested slope, the silted river, the salted soil, the burned forest, the emptied reef, the broken pasture. It happened over generations. It cannot be undone in a season.

We commit to its long care.

---

We are people. We tend land. We do this together, across regions, languages, and traditions. We do this knowing the work outlives us.

We are not heroes. We are not saviors. We are not the only ones doing this work. We are one federation among many possible. We welcome others. We welcome forks of ourselves.

---

The work is restoration.

Restoration means trees that grow into adults, soils that come back, waters that run clean, holobionts that return, communities that endure on their lands. It means specific outcomes, in specific places, verified over decades.

It does not mean planting seedlings and walking away. It does not mean credits without survival. It does not mean projects ending when funders move on. It does not mean pretty stories about pristine nature with no people.

We refuse the easy versions of this work. We accept the hard one.

---

We commit to ten things:

**One trillion adult trees.** Trees that survive into maturity. Counted across decades, not at planting. The number is large because the damage is large.

**Local first.** Local provenance. Local communities. Local knowledge. The land is specific. Care is specific. Universal recipes do not exist.

**Indigenous and local sovereignty.** Communities determine the work on their lands. Their knowledge is theirs. Their consent is required. Their voice speaks for itself.

**Open by default.** Methods, recipes, software, data, results. Open licenses. No patents on biological material. The work belongs to the biosphere, not to its tenders.

**Evidence over advocacy.** What is true matters more than what is convenient. Failures reported as honestly as successes. The credit market is shaped by discipline, not by demand.

**Reversibility.** No irreversible interventions. No GMO releases. No ecological gambles whose outcomes we cannot recover from.

**Plurality of knowledge.** Indigenous, traditional, scientific, practitioner — each first-class. None subordinate to another. None translated against its will.

**Patience.** The work is multi-decadal. The metrics are at maturity, not at quarter-end. The capital is patient or it is refused.

**No founder windfalls.** No extractive equity. No personality cults. The project survives the founders. The work outlives the work's beginnings.

**Fair pay for those who do the work.** Field stewards. Scientists. Operators. Communities. Every person who does this work eats well, lives in dignity, and can refuse what they should refuse.

---

We refuse what compromises this:

- Funding from those who would have us look away.
- Methods that would scale at the cost of survival.
- Recipes that would reduce land to monoculture.
- Verification that would falsify outcomes.
- Communications that would polish the failures away.
- Markets that would commodify what should be cared for.
- Authorities that would silence the people whose lands these are.
- Governance that would centralize what should be plural.

We refuse these knowing that refusal has cost. The cost is the cost of doing the work honestly.

---

We are organized as a federation, not a corporation, not a charity, not a movement.

A federation has many members. Members keep their identities. Members can leave. Forks of the federation are welcomed as kin, not enemies. The federation is held together by shared protocol and shared discipline, not by central command.

The federation is governed by:

- A Charter we ratify together.
- A Council of scientists and elders that can stop us when we are wrong.
- A Stewardship Office that holds the work to its commitments.
- An ethics review that holds our agents to their bounds.
- Public records that make our decisions visible.
- The voices of those whose lands we tend.

These are imperfect structures. We will adjust them as we learn. The Charter we will not adjust.

---

We work in a world of climate change. Of rising seas. Of fires that did not burn before. Of communities displaced. Of species lost. Of forests that may not return as they were.

The work is not after climate change. The work is in climate change. Some of what we plant will not survive. Some of what we restore will be lost again. We will document this honestly. We will reconcile. We will continue.

The work matters in any future. In a livable climate, restoration helps secure the livability. In a harder climate, restoration buys time and refuge for what can be saved. We do not know which future we are preparing for. We prepare for both.

---

The trillion is far. The path is long. We cannot promise the destination.

We can promise this: that we will work toward it with discipline, that we will not lie about our progress, that we will care for the land and the people on it, that we will refuse what should be refused, that we will continue when others give up.

We cannot do this alone. We do not need to. We are not alone.

---

To anyone who would join the work — as steward, scientist, operator, community partner, funder, federation member, or any other role — the question is not whether the trillion is achievable. The question is whether you would commit to its long care.

If you would, we welcome you.

The work continues.

---

*This manifesto is offered openly. It may be translated, shared, adapted by federation members, posted in restored places, and read aloud. The federation is named in each language by the words that carry these meanings best. In English, the federation is called the long care. Other languages will name it as their own languages name what we are doing. Every name is the federation's name.*

*The work continues.*
