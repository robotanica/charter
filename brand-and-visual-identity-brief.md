# Brand and Visual Identity Brief

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Strategic and creative specification
**Status:** Draft for review and visual development
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Public Communication Doctrine, Vision Document

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies the principles and intent of Robotanica's brand and visual identity. It defines:

- The principles that should guide visual design choices.
- The aesthetic territories Robotanica claims and refuses.
- The relationship between brand and the substantive corpus.
- Operational guidance without becoming a complete style guide.

The corpus has been silent on brand. Most of it is technical, structural, governance-oriented. But Robotanica will have a public face: a logo, a website, document templates, signage at field sites, materials at conferences. These visual choices communicate. Done badly, they undermine the substance the rest of the corpus has worked to establish. Done well, they reinforce it.

This Brief is **strategic, not finished design**. It commissions and constrains the design work; it does not perform it. Specific visual systems (logo, color palette, typography, iconography, photography style) are produced through design work informed by this Brief. The Brief provides the strategic floor; designers build the visual house.

The Brief is also a **refusal document** as much as a creation document. The visual territories Robotanica must not occupy are as important as those it does. Most environmental and climate-tech projects fail visually in characteristic ways; refusing those failures is half the work.

---

## 2. Why Brand Matters

### 2.1 The Substance-Brand Relationship

Brand can either reinforce or undermine substance.

A project with rigorous discipline and shoddy visuals produces cognitive dissonance: the substance is undermined by the impression that no one cared about the surface. Stakeholders process this as: maybe the substance isn't as careful as it claims.

A project with flashy visuals and weak substance produces a different problem: the surface reads as marketing, and substance is suspected of being thin behind it.

The discipline is alignment: visuals should feel like the substance — patient, plural, honest, multi-decadal, community-rooted, not promotional.

### 2.2 Functional Requirements

Brand serves functions:

- **Recognition** at conferences, in publications, in field signage.
- **Document templates** for the 30+ documents in the corpus, plus future operational documents.
- **Public-facing presence** (website, public report) accessible to many audiences.
- **Field signage and printed materials** in multiple languages and contexts.
- **Federation member coherence** — visual elements that signal federation membership without overwhelming member-specific identity.
- **Multi-cultural adaptation** — brand that works across the languages and cultures Robotanica operates in.

These functions constrain what the brand can be.

### 2.3 The Capture Risk

Brand, like other domains, can be captured:

- Funder pressure to look professional in ways inconsistent with mission.
- Marketing pressure to use techniques that feel manipulative.
- Industry pressure to conform to clean-tech or sustainability sector aesthetics.
- Conformity pressure to look like other restoration or conservation projects.

The Brief structures against these.

## 3. Principles

These principles bind brand and visual decisions.

### 3.1 Substance Over Surface

**BRAND-001** Visual identity reinforces substance; it never substitutes for substance. Where surface and substance conflict, surface yields.

### 3.2 Plural, Not Uniform

**BRAND-002** Visual identity expresses the federation's plurality. It does not imply uniform global culture; it works alongside diverse cultural expressions of federation members and communities.

### 3.3 Patient, Not Urgent

**BRAND-010** Visual language conveys patience and long-horizon work. Urgency, alarm, crisis aesthetics are refused. The work is multi-decadal; the visuals show it.

### 3.4 Honest, Not Polished

**BRAND-020** Visual language permits and shows reality, including roughness. Over-polishing reads as inauthentic and conflicts with reporting symmetry (Public Communication Doctrine §3.2).

### 3.5 Place-Specific, Not Abstract

**BRAND-030** Visual references favor specific bioregions, ecosystems, and communities over abstract "nature." Restoration is place-specific; visuals reflect this.

### 3.6 Community-Centered, Not Extractive

**BRAND-040** Where visuals depict people, indigenous and local communities are shown as protagonists of restoration in their lands, not as exotic imagery serving Robotanica's storytelling. Consent and collaboration on representation are structural per Public Communication Doctrine §13.

### 3.7 Functional, Not Decorative

**BRAND-050** Visual elements serve communication. Decoration without function is refused.

### 3.8 Open-Source and Adaptable

**BRAND-060** Brand assets are open-licensed (Creative Commons or equivalent) where consistent with mission. Adaptation by federation members and partners is expected.

### 3.9 Refuses Greenwashing Aesthetics

**BRAND-070** Visual language refuses the established greenwashing aesthetic vocabulary: pristine forests, abstracted leaves, gradients of green, drone shots of canopy with no people. These have become signals of unverified environmental claims.

### 3.10 Respectful of Sources

**BRAND-080** Where visual elements draw from indigenous or traditional cultural patterns, attribution and consent are structural, not decorative.

## 4. Aesthetic Territories

What the brand should and should not feel like.

### 4.1 Refused Territories

**Greenwashing aesthetic.** The visual vocabulary of unaccountable environmental claims:
- Green gradients with white space.
- Stylized leaf and tree silhouettes.
- Drone shots of pristine canopy with no people.
- Abstract globe imagery with green overlays.
- Soft sans-serif typography with environmental keywords in lowercase.

This vocabulary has been corrupted by organizations whose claims didn't match their substance. Robotanica's discipline is undermined by visual association with this vocabulary.

**Clean-tech corporate aesthetic.** The visual vocabulary of capital-seeking sustainability tech:
- Bright primary colors signaling tech sector.
- Geometric abstractions implying systems-level thinking without rooting in places.
- Gradient meshes signaling sophistication.
- Rendered landscapes that look like architectural fly-throughs.
- Optimistic future imagery suggesting problem solved.

This vocabulary signals capital alignment that conflicts with Charter principles regarding capital.

**Conservation-NGO traditional aesthetic.** The visual vocabulary of older conservation organizations:
- Wildlife photography (charismatic megafauna, no people).
- Earth-tone photographs of "untouched" landscapes.
- Serif typography signaling tradition and authority.
- Color palettes evoking 1970s natural history publications.

This vocabulary has its own history and place but does not reflect Robotanica's federation, sovereignty, and multi-decade dimensions.

**Climate-emergency aesthetic.** The visual vocabulary of urgency and crisis:
- Red signaling alarm.
- Apocalyptic photography.
- Countdown imagery.
- Block typography for urgency.

This is honest about climate stakes but conflicts with the patient, multi-decadal work Robotanica undertakes.

**Tech-utopian aesthetic.** The visual vocabulary of tech-solutionism:
- Robotic imagery prominent.
- Digital overlays on natural imagery suggesting tech transformation.
- Precision-engineered visual systems.
- Optimistic AI-tech-sustainability fusion imagery.

This conflicts with the Charter's care about reversibility, plurality, and the centrality of communities.

### 4.2 Adjacent But Not Same

**Open-source software aesthetic.** Documentation, technical clarity, accessibility — adjacent values. Robotanica's brand can borrow some discipline but should not feel purely "software project."

**Indigenous-led restoration aesthetic.** Some indigenous-led restoration projects have visual languages rooted in their cultures. Robotanica's brand respects these but does not appropriate them. Federation members from those communities may use their own visual languages alongside Robotanica's where appropriate.

**Library and archive aesthetic.** The discipline of preservation and the longevity of institutions. Adjacent — Robotanica is, in some sense, a library of restoration practice. But too austere as primary identity.

### 4.3 Suggested Territories

The brand should feel like:

**Field practice, not boardroom.** Visual references to actual work in actual places. Imagery of people doing work, soil being worked, equipment in use, results being measured. Not photographs of glass-walled meeting rooms.

**Documentary, not promotional.** The aesthetic of careful observation and documentation. Reference points: field naturalist notebooks, scientific illustration, longitudinal photography of specific sites over time, careful map-making.

**Patient, not urgent.** Time-aware visuals: side-by-side photographs from different years, growth charts spanning decades, multi-page documents that take time to read. Not dashboards with real-time numbers.

**Plural, not unified.** Visual elements that work in many cultural contexts, that allow federation members to bring their own aesthetic without breaking. Color and typography choices that don't require Western-centric assumptions.

**Honest about complexity.** Visual elements that don't oversimplify. Maps with appropriate detail. Charts that show ranges and uncertainty. Photographs that include unphotogenic reality.

## 5. Specific Visual Elements

The Brief commissions specific elements; it does not finalize their design.

### 5.1 Mark / Logo

**The mark should:**

- Be recognizable but not dominant.
- Work in multiple sizes (large signage to small document footers).
- Work in monochrome (printing, watermarks) and color.
- Feel deliberate but not over-designed.
- Avoid the failed vocabularies of §4.1.

**The mark should not:**

- Be a stylized leaf or tree.
- Use green gradients.
- Imply technology-saving-nature narrative.
- Use a globe icon.

**Possible directions:**

- A wordmark using carefully-chosen typography.
- A symbol drawing from observational practice (the kind of mark a naturalist might leave in a notebook).
- A glyph referencing time depth (concentric rings, layered observation, specific bioregional element used with consent).

These are starting points; design exploration is welcomed.

### 5.2 Typography

**Body text:** A serif or transitional typeface that is:

- Highly readable at length (the corpus is text-dense).
- Available across many languages (Latin, Cyrillic, Greek, Arabic, indigenous scripts where relevant).
- Open-licensed where possible.
- Not associated with corporate or NGO conventions.

**Headlines:** May contrast with body text to provide hierarchy without being attention-grabbing.

**Possible references:** Government typography (Source Serif, Public Sans), academic publishing typography, library catalog typography. Not advertising typography.

### 5.3 Color Palette

**The palette should:**

- Include earth and ecology references but not dominated by green.
- Work in print and digital.
- Be accessible (color-blind safe, sufficient contrast).
- Be limited (not many colors; restraint).
- Include neutral colors heavily (the work is more often white space and text than color).

**The palette should not:**

- Default to green/blue environmental palette.
- Include bright primary colors signaling tech.
- Use gradients heavily.

**Possible directions:**

- Anchor colors drawn from soil, biochar, parchment, slate, or specific ecological references.
- Accent colors used sparingly for emphasis.
- Substantial use of black/white/cream for text and structure.

### 5.4 Photography Style

**Photography should:**

- Show actual work, actual places, actual people.
- Include human presence prominently (people doing work, communities in their landscapes).
- Document specific moments rather than constructing idealized ones.
- Span time horizons (return visits to same sites years apart).
- Include both successes and failures.

**Photography should not:**

- Be drone footage of canopy with no people.
- Idealize wilderness as untouched.
- Use stock imagery suggesting "nature" abstractly.
- Use HDR or heavy color grading suggesting marketing.
- Include personnel or community members without consent.

**Photography commissioning:**

- Local photographers preferred where work is happening.
- Indigenous and community photographers preferred for community work, with full attribution and compensation.
- Specific consent for each use.

### 5.5 Document Templates

**Templates should:**

- Reflect the document discipline of the corpus (numbered sections, normative requirements, glossaries, cross-references).
- Be readable at length (the documents are long).
- Be accessible (screen readers, print formats, low-bandwidth contexts).
- Be adaptable to translation.
- Be federation-coherent without member-specific override.

**Templates should not:**

- Be visually complex with multi-column layouts.
- Use excessive color for hierarchy.
- Require specialized software or licenses.

**Practical recommendation:** Markdown source with multiple rendering targets (HTML, PDF, EPUB). Style sheets per medium. Open-source toolchain.

### 5.6 Iconography

**Icons should:**

- Be functional (indicating document type, status, action).
- Be limited in number (not a sprawling library).
- Be open-licensed.
- Avoid the established environmental and tech vocabularies.

### 5.7 Map Style

Maps are central to Robotanica's communication.

**Maps should:**

- Be cartographically careful (appropriate projections, accurate boundaries, honest sources).
- Show place, not abstraction.
- Honor sovereignty (sensitive locations coarsened per Protocol §10).
- Be open-source where possible (OpenStreetMap, public satellite imagery).
- Be accessible across languages.

**Maps should not:**

- Use dark mode imagery suggesting "data center" aesthetic.
- Display real-time markers suggesting live tracking.
- Imply omniscient surveillance.

### 5.8 Data Visualization

**Charts and visualizations should:**

- Show uncertainty and ranges, not point estimates.
- Use restrained color and clear labeling.
- Be readable in print and on small screens.
- Avoid the "infographic" visual vocabulary that prioritizes engagement over information.

## 6. Brand at Different Touchpoints

### 6.1 Public Website

The public website is many people's first encounter. It should:

- Open with substance (mission, current state, key documents) not marketing copy.
- Lead with the public record rather than manicured success stories.
- Allow deep exploration without requiring it.
- Work without JavaScript.
- Load on slow connections.
- Be accessible to screen readers.

The website should not:

- Open with a video.
- Use carousel sliders.
- Track visitors beyond minimum analytics.
- Have hidden navigation.
- Require account creation for any read access.

### 6.2 Documents (Internal and Public)

Documents are the primary medium of the corpus. Templates should:

- Default to long-form readability.
- Use the same typography and structure across documents (consistency).
- Be available in multiple formats.
- Be translatable without requiring redesign.

### 6.3 Field Signage

Signage at restoration sites should:

- Be functional (information that matters at the site).
- Be appropriate to local context (language, cultural practice).
- Be honest about what is and is not promised.
- Acknowledge community partners prominently.

Signage should not:

- Be primarily promotional.
- Imply Robotanica is the protagonist of the work.
- Override or compete with community-determined signage.

### 6.4 Reports and Publications

Annual reports, decadal reviews, and similar publications should:

- Use consistent template.
- Permit substantial length (reporting symmetry per Public Communication Doctrine §3.2).
- Include failures with same prominence as successes.
- Be available in multiple formats and languages.

### 6.5 Event Presence

Conference presence, federation gatherings, public events should:

- Be modest in branded presence.
- Allow community partners and federation members to bring their own visual identities alongside.
- Avoid swag and merchandise that feels promotional.

### 6.6 Social Media

If social media is used:

- Discipline of Public Communication Doctrine applies fully.
- Visual style consistent with brand principles.
- Refuses engagement-optimization aesthetics.
- Acknowledges the medium's compromises honestly.

### 6.7 Video and Film

Video should follow photography principles. Additionally:

- Avoid documentary-style narration that romanticizes.
- Include people speaking for themselves where possible.
- Translate respectfully, not paternalistically.
- Allow length where content warrants.

## 7. Brand Relationship to Federation Members

Federation members have their own identities. The brand respects this.

### 7.1 Federation Members Retain Identity

**FED-001** Federation members retain their own brand and visual identity.

**FED-002** Federation membership is signaled through optional adoption of federation-shared visual elements (mark in materials, document template, etc.), not through requiring federation-uniform visual presence.

### 7.2 Optional Co-Branding

**FED-010** Members may co-brand work with Robotanica where appropriate:

- For specific federation-coordinated initiatives.
- For events with federation-wide scope.
- For materials referencing federation membership.

**FED-011** Co-branding is opt-in, not required.

### 7.3 Forks Welcome to Adapt

**FED-020** When forks emerge per Charter §5.4, they are welcome to adapt brand assets:

- Open licensing supports this.
- Forks may modify or replace visual elements.
- Robotanica does not assert trademark in ways that prevent honest forking.

### 7.4 Indigenous and Community Voice Primary

**FED-030** Where work is on indigenous or community lands, the community's visual identity (where they wish to assert one) is primary. Robotanica's brand presence is appropriate and modest.

## 8. Cultural and Linguistic Adaptation

The brand operates across cultures and languages.

### 8.1 Translation

**TRANS-001** Brand materials are translated into the languages of operating regions:

- Per Public Communication Doctrine §3.9.
- With cultural adaptation, not just literal translation.
- By translators consulted on cultural fit.

### 8.2 Cultural Adaptation

**TRANS-010** Where cultural adaptation is needed beyond translation:

- Adaptation occurs through dialogue with cultural participants.
- Adaptation respects source culture and target culture both.
- Adaptation is documented for consistency.

### 8.3 Multilingual Identity

**TRANS-020** The brand should not assume one primary language:

- The English version is one expression of the brand, not the canonical version.
- Federation materials may have multiple "versions" rather than translations.
- Visual elements should work across scripts.

### 8.4 Indigenous Language Visibility

**TRANS-030** Where Robotanica works on indigenous lands, indigenous languages have visibility:

- Bilingual or multilingual signage where appropriate.
- Indigenous-language versions of public materials.
- Indigenous toponyms in maps.

## 9. Implementation

### 9.1 Brand Development

Brand development from this Brief proceeds through:

1. **Designer engagement** — qualified designers are engaged to develop visual systems from this Brief.
2. **Stakeholder consultation** — federation members, communities, and others provide input on draft systems.
3. **Iteration and refinement** — multiple design iterations with feedback.
4. **Federation review** — final designs reviewed by Stewardship Office and Federation governance.
5. **Adoption** — adopted designs documented in detailed style guide.

### 9.2 Style Guide

Once visual systems are developed, a detailed style guide documents:

- Logo usage rules.
- Typography specifications.
- Color palette codes.
- Document template structures.
- Photography commissioning guidelines.
- Map style specifications.
- Application examples.

The style guide is operationally living; it is updated as new contexts emerge.

### 9.3 Brand Assets

Brand assets are:

- Stored in accessible repositories.
- Version-controlled.
- Open-licensed where consistent with mission.
- Available in multiple formats.

### 9.4 Brand Governance

Brand decisions are governed by:

- **Stewardship Office** for routine brand decisions.
- **Federation governance** for major brand changes.
- **Communications Office** for operational brand application.

### 9.5 Annual Brand Review

**GOV-001** Brand presence is reviewed annually:

- Application across touchpoints.
- Effectiveness in communicating substance.
- Cultural and linguistic adaptation status.
- Member and community feedback.
- Brand asset updates.

---

## 10. What This Brief Is Not

### 10.1 Not a Style Guide

This Brief commissions design work; it does not perform it. Specific visual systems are not detailed here.

### 10.2 Not a Marketing Document

This Brief is not a marketing positioning document. Robotanica is not selling a product. Its public face serves substance, not market positioning.

### 10.3 Not a Final Identity

This Brief is v0. As brand develops and as Robotanica encounters reality, the Brief is revised. Visual identity evolves.

### 10.4 Not Sufficient

Brand alone does not establish trust or align stakeholders. The substance of the corpus does that. Brand reinforces what substance has earned.

---

## 11. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in brand and visual identity:

- **Specific designer or design firm.** This Brief commissions but does not select designers. The selection process is itself a question — open call, invited firms, in-house development?
- **Cultural designers from operating regions.** Whether to engage designers from each operating region for adaptation is operationally complex.
- **Brand evolution discipline.** How brand changes over decades is unclear; substantial evolution is likely as the federation matures.
- **Technology accessibility.** As new media emerge (AR, VR, etc.), brand application to these requires consideration.
- **Tone of voice.** Visual identity has a sibling discipline of verbal tone. The Brief mentions this; specific tone-of-voice guidance is not yet developed.
- **Brand and AI.** As AI agents draft communications and assist with design, brand guidance for AI is needed but not specified.
- **Crisis brand application.** During crises (per Public Communication Doctrine §8), brand may need to flex toward more straightforward communication. Specific guidance is not yet developed.

*Brand reinforces substance or undermines it. The Brief specifies what reinforcement requires: refusing the established failed vocabularies, claiming territories of careful observation and patient work, respecting plurality, and serving communication rather than promotion. The visual identity that emerges from this Brief should look like the work: rooted in places, careful about claims, plural in culture, patient about time. It should look like restoration done well — disciplined, modest, and quietly confident.*
