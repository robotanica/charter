# Onboarding and Capacity-Building Specification

## Operational Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Operational specification
**Status:** Draft for review
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Federation Membership Agreement, Personnel and Compensation Philosophy, Pilot Operation Protocol, Plurality of Knowledge Protocol

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies how parties become ready to participate in Robotanica's federation. It defines:

- The onboarding processes for each federation member category.
- The capacity-building work that prepares regions, communities, and individuals to engage.
- The training, mentorship, and knowledge transfer structures.
- The discipline against extractive or paternalistic capacity building.

The Federation Membership Agreement specified what membership requires — commitments, conformance, conduct standards. Nothing in the corpus specified how parties actually become ready to make those commitments. The result: an operational gap between joining the federation and operating effectively within it.

This Specification fills that gap.

The document is **honest about asymmetry**. New federation members and partner communities frequently have less institutional capacity than mature federation members. Capacity building addresses this asymmetry, but the discipline must guard against capacity building becoming dependency-creation, knowledge extraction, or cultural displacement.

The document is **community-respecting**. Capacity building with indigenous and local communities follows different patterns than capacity building with operators or scientific contributors. Communities determine what capacity they want; Robotanica supports rather than directs.

---

## 2. Why This Matters

### 2.1 The Gap Between Specification and Capacity

Federation Membership Agreement specifies what Operators must do (deliver verified outcomes per recipe and Lease, maintain insurance, contribute to Codex, accept verification). For a small organization not currently doing federation-quality restoration, "doing this on day one" is not realistic. They need pathway from current capacity to federation-readiness.

Same applies to Verifiers, Scientific Contributors, communities forming partnerships, and personnel new to the work. Each needs structured progression from initial engagement to full participation.

### 2.2 The Risk of Bad Capacity Building

Capacity building has well-documented failure modes:

- **Dependency creation.** Building capacity in ways that make the recipient permanently dependent on the supplier.
- **Cultural displacement.** "Building capacity" by replacing local approaches with external models.
- **Knowledge extraction.** "Capacity exchange" where the recipient supplies knowledge while the supplier supplies credentials.
- **Paternalism.** Treating recipients as needing rescue rather than as partners.
- **Token participation.** Inviting recipients into structures designed without them.

The Specification structures against these failures.

### 2.3 The Multi-Decade Horizon

Capacity building for multi-decade work differs from capacity building for short-term projects:

- People entering the work today may continue for decades; capacity must support careers, not just task completion.
- Communities partnering today will outlast specific personnel; capacity must build community institutions, not just individuals.
- Methods learned today must evolve as conditions evolve; capacity must include capacity-to-learn.

## 3. Principles

These principles bind onboarding and capacity-building work.

### 3.1 Capacity Goes Both Ways

**ONB-001** Capacity flows are mutual. Robotanica builds capacity in others; others build capacity in Robotanica. The federation is enriched by all participants, not just the most institutionally established.

### 3.2 Communities Determine Their Capacity

**ONB-002** Indigenous and local communities determine what capacity they want and on what terms. Capacity building with communities is responsive, not directive.

### 3.3 Capacity Is Sustained, Not Delivered

**ONB-010** Capacity building is sustained engagement over time, not delivery of training packages. The capacity that matters is sustained practice.

### 3.4 No Cultural Displacement

**ONB-020** Capacity building respects existing cultural, methodological, and operational approaches. Federation capacity is added to existing approaches; it does not replace them.

### 3.5 Knowledge Attribution Honored

**ONB-030** Where capacity building involves knowledge exchange, attribution flows in both directions. Capacity recipients are not converted into nameless knowledge contributors.

### 3.6 Capacity Building Is Compensated

**ONB-040** Time and effort spent in capacity building is compensated. Communities, partner organizations, and personnel engaged in capacity building are paid for the time, not expected to contribute as gift.

### 3.7 Federation Standards as Ceiling, Not Floor

**ONB-050** Federation-readiness standards are achievement targets, not entry requirements. Parties below current standards are supported toward them; immediate exclusion is not the response to gap.

### 3.8 Indigenous-Led Capacity Initiatives Supported

**ONB-060** Where indigenous communities or federations operate their own capacity-building initiatives, Robotanica supports rather than competes. Cross-community indigenous capacity building is community-led.

### 3.9 Open Materials

**ONB-070** Training materials, curricula, and capacity resources are open. Per Charter §5.1, materials are openly licensed unless cultural sensitivity warrants restricted distribution.

### 3.10 Continuous Iteration

**ONB-080** Onboarding and capacity processes are reviewed and improved continuously. Federation members provide feedback on the experience; processes adapt.

## 4. Federation Member Onboarding

Each federation member category has a tailored onboarding process.

### 4.1 Operators

**Initial qualification:**

- Demonstrated capability in restoration practice (existing project portfolio, qualifications).
- Charter alignment confirmed through interview and reference checks.
- Initial financial and operational due diligence.

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Application and dialogue** (1-3 months): Operator submits interest with capability documentation. Mutual exploration of fit.
2. **Charter and protocol orientation** (1-2 months): Operator personnel engage with the corpus, particularly Federation Membership Agreement, MRV Specification, Stewardship Lease Requirements, and relevant Bioregional Strategy.
3. **Pilot engagement** (3-6 months): Initial small-scale engagement on a parcel, with intensive support and supervision. Outcomes verified per MRV.
4. **Capability development** (6-18 months): Specific capability-building in areas where Operator's current capacity falls below federation expectations. May include training, mentorship, technical support.
5. **Membership** (after 12-24 months): Full federation membership granted upon demonstrated capability, Charter alignment, and operational readiness.

**Required capabilities at full membership:**

- Recipe execution per Codex specifications.
- MRV-aligned monitoring and reporting.
- Indigenous and community partnership skills (where operating in such contexts).
- Worker safety and personnel discipline per Personnel Philosophy.
- Insurance and operational discipline.
- Public communication discipline.

**Continuing capacity development:**

- Annual capability review.
- Cross-region exchange opportunities.
- Methodology training as Codex evolves.
- Failure analysis from federation-wide events.

### 4.2 Verifiers

**Initial qualification:**

- Existing technical accreditation (where applicable to jurisdiction).
- Demonstrated independence from federation interests.
- Charter alignment.

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Application and independence verification** (1-2 months): Documentation of independence per MRV §7.
2. **Methodology orientation** (1-3 months): Engagement with MRV Specification, recipe-specific verification protocols, sovereignty considerations.
3. **Shadow verification** (3-9 months): Verifier shadows experienced federation verifiers, performs verifications under supervision with senior verifier sign-off.
4. **Independent verification with review** (6-12 months): Verifier performs independent verifications subject to peer review.
5. **Full verifier status** (after 12-18 months): Independent verification authority across the relevant scope.

**Continuing capacity development:**

- Methodology updates as MRV evolves.
- Cross-region verifier exchange.
- Technique training (remote sensing, biological assessment, statistical methods).
- Participation in verifier networks.

**Indigenous verifiers:**

- Per MRV §7.6 and Plurality of Knowledge Protocol §13.1, indigenous verification follows community-determined protocols.
- Capacity building for indigenous verifiers is community-led; Robotanica supports as requested.
- Indigenous verifier accreditation is parallel to scientific verifier accreditation, equally valid.

### 4.3 Scientific Contributors

**Initial qualification:**

- Academic, institutional, or community standing in relevant ecological discipline.
- Peer-reviewed publication or equivalent indicator of scientific competence.
- Charter alignment.

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Engagement and orientation** (1-2 months): Discussion of mutual interest, Codex contribution norms, peer-review processes.
2. **Codex engagement** (2-6 months): Participation in Codex review processes, perhaps as reviewer first, then as contributor.
3. **Independent contribution** (6+ months): Scientific Contributor publishes Codex contributions under their own attribution.

**Continuing capacity development:**

- Cross-disciplinary engagement.
- Federation-wide research priorities awareness.
- Data access for federation-relevant research.
- Indigenous knowledge collaboration training where applicable.

**Indigenous scientific contributors:**

- Communities and indigenous-led research institutions engaged as parallel-and-equal scientific contributors.
- No requirement to translate indigenous frameworks into Western scientific frameworks (per Plurality of Knowledge Protocol §5).
- Compensation per partnership agreements.

### 4.4 Fabricators

**Initial qualification:**

- Manufacturing capability for relevant equipment, robotics, or biological production.
- Quality discipline appropriate to scale.
- Charter alignment.

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Capability assessment** (1-3 months): Existing capability evaluated against federation needs.
2. **Standards orientation** (1-3 months): OpenFactory standards, documentation discipline, IP considerations.
3. **Pilot production** (3-9 months): Small-scale production with quality verification.
4. **Federation supplier status** (after 6-12 months): Established federation supplier with ongoing relationship.

**Continuing capacity development:**

- Scaling support as federation needs grow.
- Open-source design contribution opportunities.
- Cross-fabricator collaboration.

### 4.5 Funders

**Initial qualification:**

- Mission alignment per Funding Strategy.
- Capacity to commit at scale appropriate to engagement.
- Refusal categories not applicable.

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Mutual exploration** (per Founding Capital Plan §6).
2. **Funding agreement formation**.
3. **Reporting and engagement cycle** (ongoing).

**Funder onboarding is lighter than other member categories** — funders are not building operational capacity but establishing relationship structures.

**Continuing capacity development:**

- Education on credit market discipline.
- Site visits where appropriate.
- Federation events where invited.
- Reporting that builds funder understanding over time.

### 4.6 Civil Society Participants

**Initial qualification:**

- Mission alignment.
- Charter compatibility.

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Engagement and orientation** (1-2 months).
2. **Defined participation scope** (per the participant's interest and capacity).
3. **Ongoing engagement** with appropriate periodic check-ins.

**Civil society participation is intentionally light-weight** to support broad federation reach without operational burden.

### 4.7 Researchers

**Initial qualification:**

- Research interest aligned with federation work.
- Institutional affiliation or community endorsement.
- Charter compatibility (particularly regarding sovereignty).

**Onboarding sequence:**

1. **Research proposal review** (1-3 months): Proposal compatibility with federation principles, sovereignty considerations.
2. **Data access agreements**: Specific scope and constraints per sovereignty framework.
3. **Active research period**: With ongoing federation engagement.
4. **Publication and contribution back**: Research findings contributed to Codex and Federation public record.

## 5. Community Capacity Building

Community capacity building requires specific protocols beyond federation member onboarding.

### 5.1 Communities Are Not Members in the Same Sense

**COMM-001** Indigenous and local communities engaged in restoration partnerships are not "federation members" in the same operational sense as Operators or Verifiers.

**COMM-002** Communities are partners with sovereignty over their participation. Federation membership categories may not adequately capture community engagement; community-specific terms apply.

### 5.2 Community-Determined Capacity Building

**COMM-010** Capacity building with communities is responsive to community-articulated needs:

- What capabilities does the community want to develop?
- What support do they want from Robotanica or federation members?
- What pace and form is appropriate?
- What attribution and compensation structures?

**COMM-011** Robotanica does not impose capacity-building agendas on communities. Programs are developed in dialogue.

### 5.3 Common Capacity-Building Areas

Where communities express interest, capacity-building areas may include:

- **Technical restoration knowledge** in formats compatible with community learning (often through participatory practice rather than classroom).
- **MRV literacy** so communities can engage with verification processes effectively.
- **Codex contribution** so community knowledge enters the Codex on community terms.
- **Federation governance literacy** so communities can engage with broader federation as desired.
- **Scientific collaboration skills** for communities that want to work with scientific institutions.
- **Legal and rights awareness** regarding Lease, sovereignty, and partnership.
- **Technology training** for tools the community wants to use.

### 5.4 Community Personnel Development

**COMM-020** Where communities provide personnel to operations on their lands, personnel development is supported:

- Training appropriate to community context.
- Compensation per Personnel Philosophy §5.6.
- Career development within or beyond federation work as the person chooses.
- Family and community support during work.

### 5.5 Multi-Generational Capacity

**COMM-030** Community capacity building considers multi-generational continuity:

- Current generation building skills for current work.
- Knowledge transfer to younger generation.
- Elder knowledge supported in transmission.
- Educational partnerships with community schools where invited.

### 5.6 No Capacity-Extraction Patterns

**COMM-040** Capacity building does not become knowledge extraction:

- Communities sharing knowledge as part of capacity engagement retain sovereignty over that knowledge.
- "Capacity building" that produces transferable knowledge for Robotanica's use without ongoing community partnership is refused as a pattern.

### 5.7 Cross-Community Learning

**COMM-050** Where communities want to learn from each other (with each community's consent), Robotanica supports:

- Funding for community exchange visits.
- Translation and logistics support.
- Documentation as the communities determine.

But Robotanica does not become a clearinghouse for indigenous knowledge across communities (per Plurality of Knowledge Protocol §6.8).

## 6. Regional Capacity Building

Beyond individual onboarding, capacity building at regional scale is needed.

### 6.1 Regional Restoration Capability

**REG-001** Each engaged bioregion benefits from regional capacity:

- Multiple operator options.
- Local biological production.
- Regional verification capacity.
- Regional governance presence (Regional Coordination Body per Bioregional Strategy §7.2).
- Local scientific collaboration.

**REG-002** Building regional capability is multi-year work; engagement of a region typically begins with one or two partners and expands as capability develops.

### 6.2 Local Biological Production Development

**REG-010** Per OpenFactory PRD §6.2, biological production is regional. Building local production capability includes:

- Local nurseries (community-aligned where applicable).
- Local biological supplier networks.
- Quality discipline transfer.
- Sustainable financial model for local production.

### 6.3 Local Verification Capacity

**REG-020** Local verification capacity is built per region:

- Training local verifiers.
- Indigenous verifier accreditation.
- Inter-verifier networks within regions.
- Reduces dependence on imported verification (which has cost and cultural implications).

### 6.4 Local Scientific Collaboration

**REG-030** Regional scientific institutions are engaged:

- Universities and research centers in or near the bioregion.
- Indigenous-led research institutions.
- Government agencies with relevant scientific capacity.

Regional scientific collaboration produces region-specific Codex content and supports community engagement.

### 6.5 Local Operational Workforce

**REG-040** The operational workforce in each region is substantially local:

- Hiring within the region.
- Training pathways for regional personnel.
- Career development within the region.
- Indigenous and community personnel preferential where applicable.

This is partly a Personnel Philosophy commitment (§13.4) and partly a Bioregional Strategy implementation. Operationally it requires sustained capacity building.

## 7. Training and Knowledge Transfer

Specific training and knowledge transfer structures.

### 7.1 Curriculum Components

Training across federation member categories shares some components:

- **Charter and corpus orientation**: Foundational concepts every member should understand.
- **Methodology training**: Specific to role (recipe execution, verification, scientific methods, fabrication).
- **Sovereignty and partnership skills**: How to work with indigenous and local communities, with attention to FPIC, knowledge sovereignty, communication.
- **Safety and ethics**: Personnel safety, ethical refusal, whistleblower channels.
- **Failure handling**: How to handle failures with discipline (per Public Communication Doctrine §9 and MRV §14).
- **Federation participation**: How to engage with federation-wide governance, decision-making, communication.

### 7.2 Training Modes

**MODE-001** Training uses multiple modes appropriate to content and learners:

- **Documented self-study** for foundational corpus material.
- **Mentored apprenticeship** for operational skills.
- **Workshop and field-based** for hands-on capabilities.
- **Cross-region exchange** for advanced practitioners.
- **Community-led learning** where indigenous or local pedagogies apply.

### 7.3 Training Materials

**MAT-001** Training materials are:

- Open-licensed where possible (Charter §5.1).
- Multi-language with culturally-appropriate adaptation.
- Updated as Codex and federation practice evolve.
- Co-developed with relevant community partners where appropriate.

### 7.4 Training Resource Allocation

**RES-001** Training is funded as part of operations, not as separate "capacity building" budget that competes with restoration:

- Training costs included in regional operational budgets.
- Cross-region training supported through federation budget.
- Community-led training compensated through partnership agreements.

### 7.5 Knowledge Transfer Across Regions

**XR-001** Knowledge transfer across regions occurs through:

- Personnel mobility (per Personnel Philosophy §13.3).
- Cross-region exchange visits.
- Annual federation gathering (per Vision Document §4.4).
- Shared Codex updates.
- Regional Coordination Body inter-region communication.

**XR-002** Knowledge transfer respects sovereignty: indigenous and community knowledge transfers between regions only with all relevant communities' consent.

## 8. Mentorship Structures

Sustained mentorship supports career development.

### 8.1 Mentorship Pairs

**MENT-001** Each personnel member entering federation work is paired with at least one mentor:

- Within their direct organization initially.
- Cross-region mentor as career develops.
- Indigenous community elder where personnel work in indigenous-partnered contexts.

### 8.2 Mentor Compensation and Recognition

**MENT-010** Mentor time is compensated:

- Mentorship is considered part of senior personnel's role.
- Specific time allocation in compensation structures.
- Recognition in evaluation and tenure consideration.

### 8.3 Mentor Selection

**MENT-020** Mentors are selected with mentee input:

- Mentees indicate preference where possible.
- Multiple potential mentors offered.
- Mentee can change mentor if relationship is not productive.

### 8.4 Cross-Cultural Mentorship

**MENT-030** Cross-cultural mentorship occurs where mentor and mentee are from different cultural backgrounds:

- Cultural humility training for mentors.
- Mutual learning emphasized.
- Refusal to engage in cultural translation that damages either party.

## 9. Federation Member Continuity

Capacity building does not stop at full membership; it continues throughout participation.

### 9.1 Continuing Education

**CONT-001** Federation members engage in continuing education:

- Annual training requirements per role category.
- Cross-region exchange opportunities.
- Methodology updates as field evolves.
- Failure analysis from federation events.

### 9.2 Capacity Reviews

**CONT-010** Federation members undergo capacity reviews:

- Annual self-assessment by member.
- Periodic federation review (every 3-5 years).
- Continuous performance feedback through MRV outcomes.

### 9.3 Capability Evolution

**CONT-020** As federation work evolves (new methodologies, new bioregions, new technologies), members evolve:

- New capability training where applicable.
- Career path support for individuals.
- Organizational development support for member entities.

### 9.4 Mentoring the Next Cohort

**CONT-030** Established federation members are expected to mentor newer members:

- Sharing experience.
- Shadowing opportunities for new members.
- Cross-regional teaching where appropriate.

## 10. Onboarding for Special Roles

Some roles require specialized onboarding.

### 10.1 Stewardship Office Personnel

Personnel joining the Stewardship Office onboard through:

- Charter and corpus deep engagement.
- Cross-functional exposure to federation operations.
- Apprenticeship under existing Stewardship Office personnel.
- Specific training in indigenous and community partnership.

### 10.2 Council Members

Per Science Council Charter §6, Council members onboard through:

- Independent Selection Committee process.
- Council orientation including methodology, governance, and federation history.
- Mentorship from outgoing or experienced Council members.

### 10.3 AIERB Members

Per AI Ethical Review Framework §15.1, AIERB members onboard through:

- Selection per AIERB protocols.
- Technical and ethical orientation.
- Engagement with AI capability landscape.

### 10.4 Senior Leadership

Senior leadership onboarding includes:

- Comprehensive corpus engagement.
- Federation history and relationships.
- Operations site visits.
- Senior peer mentorship.
- Six-month transition period before full responsibility.

## 11. Onboarding Failure Handling

Sometimes onboarding does not succeed.

### 11.1 Recognition of Misalignment

**FAIL-001** Misalignment may emerge during onboarding:

- Capability gaps that cannot be closed within reasonable time.
- Charter alignment problems detected during deeper engagement.
- Practical incompatibility between member organization and federation.

### 11.2 Honest Conversation

**FAIL-010** Where misalignment is real, honest conversation precedes any decision:

- Specific issues named.
- Possible adjustments explored.
- Member's perspective heard.

### 11.3 Decision Outcomes

**FAIL-020** Decision outcomes may include:

- Continued onboarding with adjusted timeline.
- Different membership category if more appropriate.
- Mutual decision not to proceed.
- Withdrawal of membership offer.

### 11.4 Withdrawal Without Stigma

**FAIL-030** Withdrawal from onboarding does not stigmatize the party:

- Public record reflects mutual decision where appropriate.
- No defamation or negative public communication.
- Possibility of future engagement preserved.

### 11.5 Capacity-Building Continuation

**FAIL-040** Where capacity-building was underway, completion of in-progress work is honored:

- Training that has begun is not abandoned mid-stream.
- Partnerships in formation are concluded with respect.

## 12. Governance

### 12.1 Onboarding Governance

Onboarding decisions are governed by:

- **Stewardship Office** for routine onboarding across member categories.
- **Federation governance** for major member additions and changes.
- **Council** for Scientific Contributor and Verifier accreditation.
- **AIERB** for AI-adjacent roles.

### 12.2 Annual Onboarding Review

**GOV-001** The Stewardship Office publishes an annual review:

- New members onboarded by category.
- Capacity-building activities undertaken.
- Investment in onboarding and capacity building.
- Onboarding outcomes (completion, withdrawal, etc.).
- Forward priorities.

### 12.3 Continuous Improvement

**GOV-010** Onboarding processes are continuously improved:

- New members provide feedback on their experience.
- Federation members suggest improvements.
- Annual review surfaces patterns.
- Process changes are documented and tracked.

## 13. Resource Requirements

Capacity building requires real resources.

### 13.1 Personnel for Capacity Building

**RES-001** Capacity building requires dedicated personnel:

- Training coordinators.
- Subject-matter experts who teach.
- Mentors with allocated time.
- Community liaison personnel.
- Translation and language support.

### 13.2 Material Resources

**RES-010** Material resources include:

- Training materials development.
- Training delivery (venue, technology, travel).
- Translation and localization.
- Ongoing maintenance of materials.

### 13.3 Funding

**RES-020** Capacity building is funded through:

- Operational budgets including allocation for training.
- Federation-wide budget for cross-region activities.
- Specific philanthropic grants for capacity building where available.
- Compensation flowing to communities for their capacity contributions.

### 13.4 Resource Discipline

**RES-030** Capacity building does not become a substitute for hiring qualified personnel:

- Training prepares people for work; it does not replace people who already have capability.
- Federation members are not asked to provide capacity-building work without compensation.
- Capacity building has a budget; it is not an unfunded mandate.

## 14. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in onboarding and capacity building:

- **Capacity-building scale.** As the federation grows, capacity-building infrastructure must scale. Specific design for scale (200 members? 2000?) is not yet detailed.
- **Cultural adaptation of training.** Training developed in one cultural context requires adaptation for others. The adaptation process and resourcing are not fully specified.
- **Indigenous capacity-building leadership.** Whether and how to develop indigenous-led capacity building beyond what individual communities provide is an open opportunity.
- **Cross-language coordination.** Federation operations across languages require translation infrastructure that scales with language diversity.
- **Verification capacity at scale.** Building enough verifier capacity to support federation growth without compromising independence is operationally complex.
- **Operator capacity in difficult bioregions.** Some bioregions have weak existing operator ecosystems. Capacity building from near-zero is much harder than capacity building on existing foundations.
- **AI-supported learning and tools.** AI agents may eventually support capacity building (per AI Framework). The role of AI in training and onboarding is not yet specified.
- **Career mobility within and beyond the federation.** Personnel develop careers; some stay long-term, some leave. Mobility infrastructure (recognition of federation experience by external employers) is uncertain.
- **Onboarding cost recovery.** Capacity building costs significant resources; whether and how to charge for it (and from whom) is unresolved. Default is funded operationally; some categories may warrant cost recovery.

## Appendix C — Glossary (Onboarding-Specific)

- **Apprenticeship** — Sustained mentored learning combining instruction and supervised practice.
- **Capacity building** — Sustained engagement to develop capability in individuals, organizations, or communities.
- **Capacity exchange** — Mutual capacity flows between parties.
- **Cohort** — A group of new federation members onboarded together (distinct from restoration cohort).
- **Mentorship** — Sustained one-to-one or one-to-few learning relationship.
- **Onboarding** — The process by which a new member becomes ready to participate.
- **Orientation** — Initial introduction to corpus and federation.
- **Shadow verification** — Verifier in training observing and gradually performing under senior supervision.

---

*Onboarding and capacity building are how the federation grows in members and capability over decades. They are not auxiliary to the work; they are the work of sustaining the federation across time. The discipline is to build capacity mutually, respect community sovereignty, refuse extraction, and invest the resources that capacity development actually requires. Without capacity, federation grows only through importation of pre-formed capability — an unsustainable pattern. With capacity built well, the federation grows through development of partners and personnel into the work.*
