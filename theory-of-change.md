# Theory of Change

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Causal model
**Status:** Draft for review
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, White Paper, MRV Specification, Bioregional Strategy, Genetic Strategy

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document articulates Robotanica's **theory of change**: the causal chain by which the work the Federation does produces the mission outcomes the Federation seeks.

Most foundations and grant-makers expect this. The corpus has been silent on it — the causal logic was implicit across multiple documents. This document makes it explicit.

A theory of change is a **falsifiable claim about how the world works**. If the claim is wrong, the work fails. The discipline is to:

- State the chain explicitly.
- Identify the assumptions at each link.
- Cite evidence supporting each link.
- Name what would falsify each link.
- Specify how Robotanica monitors the chain and updates as evidence accumulates.

This document is not a marketing narrative. It is a working hypothesis subject to revision. Where evidence is weak or contested, the document says so.

---

## 2. The Causal Chain

The chain has six links from intervention to ultimate outcome. Each link is a separate claim with its own evidence and assumptions.

```
[Intervention] → [Establishment] → [Survival to Maturity] → [Ecosystem Recovery] → [Biosphere Function] → [Civilizational Outcome]
```

The chain is read forward: interventions lead to establishment, establishment leads to survival, and so on. It can also be read backward: civilizational outcomes depend on biosphere function, which depends on ecosystem recovery, which depends on adult-tree survival.

Each link has its own evidence base and its own potential to break. Robotanica's discipline is to monitor the integrity of every link, not just the headline metric (adult trees).

## 3. Link 1 — Intervention to Establishment

**Claim:** Robotanica's interventions (planting, tending, monitoring under recipes drawn from the Codex) reliably produce established cohorts.

### 3.1 What "Intervention" Includes

- Site selection within ecologically appropriate bioregions per the Bioregional Strategy.
- Recipe selection and binding, with FPIC where applicable.
- Biological production at quality (per OpenFactory and the MRV).
- Planting per recipe protocol.
- Tending through the establishment period.
- Holobiont supplementation (mycorrhizae, soil biota, companions).

### 3.2 What "Establishment" Means

A cohort is **established** when it has survived initial transplant shock and is judged to have a reasonable prospect of long-term survival per MRV §4.4. Operationally: continuous survival through one growing season plus one defined stress period (drought, dormancy, etc.).

### 3.3 Evidence for the Link

- **Miyawaki method** has demonstrated establishment rates of 70–90%+ for native multi-species plantings across multiple climates.
- **FMNR (Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration)** has demonstrated establishment of millions of hectares in the Sahel with simple intervention.
- **Loess Plateau restoration** demonstrated establishment at scale through documented practice.
- **Holistic Management** has demonstrated grassland establishment in brittle environments.
- **Conventional plantation silviculture** routinely achieves 80%+ establishment under standard conditions.

The link is well-supported by evidence. Establishment rates of 60–90% are achievable under good practice.

### 3.4 Assumptions

- Recipes are appropriate for site conditions (assumption: Codex discipline + Science Council review).
- Biological production produces quality material (assumption: OpenFactory quality control + MRV verification).
- Field operations execute the recipe with fidelity (assumption: Operator accountability + monitoring).
- Holobiont supplementation succeeds (assumption: appropriate inoculants + soil context).
- Climate at the site during establishment is consistent with recipe assumptions (assumption: not catastrophic departure).

### 3.5 What Would Break This Link

- Recipes that don't match site conditions (intervention without ecological grounding).
- Poor-quality biological material.
- Inadequate or absent tending during establishment.
- Operator misalignment with recipe.
- Catastrophic climate event during establishment (drought, frost, fire).

### 3.6 Robotanica's Monitoring

Establishment is measured per cohort in Gaia. Establishment rates are tracked across recipes, bioregions, and operators. Failures trigger investigation per MRV §14. Recipe revisions follow when systematic failure patterns emerge.

## 4. Link 2 — Establishment to Survival-to-Maturity

**Claim:** Established cohorts, with appropriate stewardship across decades, mature into adult trees at rates sufficient to deliver the survival-to-maturity metric.

### 4.1 What "Survival-to-Maturity" Means

Per Charter §1.3 and MRV §4.2: trees that have reached species-specific reproductive, structural, or functional maturity thresholds and remain alive at the time of counting.

### 4.2 The Time Horizon

Maturity timelines vary widely:

- Tropical pioneers: 3–10 years.
- Many temperate species: 20–50 years.
- Old-growth species: 50–150+ years.

The horizon is decades, not years. The link is the longest in the chain.

### 4.3 Evidence for the Link

This is the **weakest link in the chain**, in the sense that historical reforestation projects show survival-to-maturity rates that are wildly variable:

- **Well-managed restoration** has achieved 60–80% survival to maturity in some contexts.
- **Industrial reforestation** has commonly achieved 30–50% survival, with wide variance.
- **Carbon offset projects without rigorous discipline** have sometimes achieved <20% survival to maturity.

The variance reflects different factors:

- Recipe quality (matched to ecology vs. mismatched).
- Continuous tending (vs. plant-and-walk-away).
- Climate trajectory (stable vs. shifting).
- Disturbance regime (fire, drought, pest).
- Land use change (was the site protected from clearing?).

### 4.4 Assumptions

- Stewardship continues through the full maturity timeline (assumption: multi-decade Lease structure).
- Climate at the site does not catastrophically depart from recipe assumptions (assumption: climate-aware provenance + monitoring + recipe revision).
- Disturbance is managed (assumption: recipe-specified disturbance protocols).
- Genetic diversity is sufficient to handle environmental variation (assumption: Genetic Strategy §5).
- Land use change does not eliminate the cohort (assumption: Stewardship Lease binding successors).

### 4.5 What Would Break This Link

- Lease termination before maturity.
- Operator failure with succession failure.
- Catastrophic climate divergence (drought beyond recipe assumption, fire frequency increase).
- Pest or disease outbreak the recipe didn't anticipate.
- Land use conversion despite Lease.

### 4.6 Robotanica's Monitoring

Per MRV §10 (permanence as a trajectory). Survival is monitored continuously per MRV §4.3 (currency windows). Cohort survival rates are reported per MRV §6. Permanence reporting projects forward survival trajectories. Reconciliation per MRV §14 when projections diverge from outcomes.

### 4.7 The Discipline

This link is where Robotanica's structural commitments matter most:

- Multi-decade Stewardship Lease binding through maturity (Lease Requirements §4).
- Reconciliation reserve for site failures (MRV §10.3).
- Climate-aware provenance (Genetic Strategy §6).
- Permanence verification (MRV §10).

The link is hard. The structural commitments are the bet that hardness can be navigated.

## 5. Link 3 — Survival-to-Maturity to Ecosystem Recovery

**Claim:** Mature cohorts under appropriate recipes contribute to ecosystem recovery: biodiversity restoration, soil restoration, hydrological restoration, holobiont integrity.

### 5.1 What "Ecosystem Recovery" Means

A target ecosystem state, defined per recipe, with measurable indicators per MRV §5.7. Includes:

- Multi-species canopy structure characteristic of the bioregion.
- Native species recolonization.
- Soil organic carbon and microbiome recovery.
- Water cycle restoration where degraded.
- Pollinator and herbivore reestablishment.
- Functional ecosystem services (water provisioning, soil stabilization, climate regulation).

### 5.2 Evidence for the Link

- **Loess Plateau** demonstrated ecosystem-scale recovery from severe degradation.
- **Costa Rica's reforestation** has demonstrated significant biodiversity recovery alongside forest cover.
- **Atlantic Forest restoration** has demonstrated ecosystem function returning over decades.
- **Native multi-species plantings** consistently outperform monoculture plantings for ecosystem function.

The link is reasonably well-supported, with the qualifier that **ecosystem recovery is gradient, not binary**. Recovery to historical reference state may be impossible (extinct species, climate shift); recovery to functional analog states is more feasible.

### 5.3 Assumptions

- Recipes specify multi-species, ecologically-grounded plantings (per Codex discipline).
- Holobiont supplementation succeeds (mycorrhizae establish, microbiome matches).
- Connectivity is sufficient for species recolonization.
- Disturbance regime supports rather than prevents ecosystem development.
- Surrounding landscape supports recovery (e.g., presence of seed sources).

### 5.4 What Would Break This Link

- Plantings that survive but do not function ecologically (monoculture-dominant outcomes).
- Holobiont mismatch (mycorrhizae fail to establish, soil microbiome wrong).
- Isolation from species sources (no connectivity).
- Continued external pressure (extraction adjacent, fragmentation).

### 5.5 Robotanica's Monitoring

Multi-indicator biodiversity assessment per MRV §5.7. Soil carbon and microbiome monitoring. Connectivity assessment within the Bioregional Strategy. Long-horizon ecosystem outcome reporting (decadal reviews per MRV §6.2).

### 5.6 The Hard Cases

Not all sites recover to full ecological function within the Lease horizon. Some sites recover partial function. Some achieve "novel ecosystem" states that are not historical analogs but are ecologically productive. The Charter accepts this gradient (Charter §1.4 — "Beyond Trees" framing).

## 6. Link 4 — Ecosystem Recovery to Biosphere Function

**Claim:** Aggregate ecosystem recovery across millions to billions of hectares contributes to planetary biosphere function: carbon sequestration, biodiversity, hydrological cycle, climate regulation.

### 6.1 What "Biosphere Function" Means

Functional contribution at planetary scale:

- **Carbon storage** in biomass and soil.
- **Biodiversity preservation** at gene, species, and ecosystem levels.
- **Hydrological regulation** at watershed and continental scales.
- **Climate regulation** through albedo, evapotranspiration, atmospheric chemistry.
- **Resilience** of human and natural systems against climate disruption.

### 6.2 Evidence for the Link

This link is **scientifically robust at the principle level** but **uncertain at the magnitude level**:

- IPCC and IPBES assessments consistently identify forest restoration as a meaningful nature-based climate solution.
- Major scientific literature documents ecosystem service contributions to biosphere function.
- The Loess Plateau case demonstrates regional-scale biosphere function recovery.

The uncertainty is in **how much restoration at what scale produces what magnitude of biosphere effect**. Estimates of restoration's potential climate contribution range across an order of magnitude in the literature.

### 6.3 Assumptions

- Aggregate recovery is meaningful — that the sum of restored hectares produces a planetary effect proportionate to its scale.
- Restoration does not produce hidden offsetting losses (leakage per MRV §11).
- Climate change does not outpace restoration's contribution (the "running to stay still" risk).

### 6.4 What Would Break This Link

- Leakage that nets out gains (per-MRV-§11).
- Climate change outpacing restoration's contribution to biosphere function.
- Restoration concentrated in low-leverage regions while high-leverage regions degrade.
- Restoration that "succeeds" locally while ecosystem service flows are interrupted.

### 6.5 Robotanica's Monitoring

Aggregate outcomes reported in MRV §6 and across the public record. Connection to global biosphere indicators (IPCC, IPBES) tracked. Leakage assessment per MRV §11. Bioregional balance per Bioregional Strategy.

### 6.6 The Honest Uncertainty

This link is the largest source of uncertainty in the chain not because the principle is contested but because **the specific magnitude depends on factors outside Robotanica's control**: total carbon emitted by humanity, biodiversity threats from non-restoration causes, climate trajectory, etc.

Robotanica's contribution to biosphere function is real and meaningful but not deterministic. The discipline is to do the work well; the magnitude of impact at planetary scale depends on context.

## 7. Link 5 — Biosphere Function to Civilizational Outcome

**Claim:** Restored biosphere function contributes to civilizational outcomes: climate stabilization within livable bounds, biodiversity preserved for future generations, ecosystem services maintained, human wellbeing supported.

### 7.1 What "Civilizational Outcome" Means

The ultimate "why" of the mission. Includes:

- Climate change limited to bounds where human civilization continues functioning at scale.
- Biodiversity preserved for future generations and ecological resilience.
- Ecosystem services (water, food production support, disease regulation, etc.) maintained.
- Human wellbeing including dignity, livelihoods, and cultural continuity for those whose lives depend on ecosystem function.

### 7.2 Evidence for the Link

This link is **scientifically supported at the directional level** but **deeply contested at the magnitude and necessity level**:

- IPCC scenarios identify nature-based solutions as part of pathways consistent with limiting warming.
- Biodiversity loss is documented as undermining ecosystem services.
- Hydrological cycle restoration is documented as supporting human wellbeing in degraded regions.

The **necessity question** — is restoration required for civilizational outcomes, or is it sufficient on its own — has clear answers: restoration is **necessary but not sufficient**. Civilizational outcomes also require:

- Drastic emissions reduction (no amount of restoration substitutes for this).
- Sustainable consumption patterns.
- Equitable resource distribution.
- Effective governance.
- Continuing scientific and cultural development.

Restoration is one of several necessary components.

### 7.3 Assumptions

- Other necessary components (emissions reduction, sustainable consumption, equitable governance) progress sufficiently alongside restoration.
- Climate trajectory does not exceed bounds where ecosystem-based interventions become moot.
- Biodiversity at sufficient scale is preserved alongside restoration.

### 7.4 What Would Break This Link

- Emissions reduction failure: even successful restoration cannot offset continued accelerating emissions.
- Climate trajectory exceeding plan: tipping points beyond which restoration's contribution is insufficient.
- Biodiversity catastrophe in non-restoration domains: marine collapse, agricultural ecosystem collapse, disease.

### 7.5 Robotanica's Posture

Robotanica is not the only actor working on civilizational outcomes. Restoration is one component of necessary action. The work matters even when other components fail; it matters more when other components also progress.

The Charter (§2.6 evidence over advocacy) commits Robotanica to this honesty. Restoration is what Robotanica does; restoration is real but bounded contribution to civilizational outcomes; civilization needs many things including restoration.

## 8. The Reverse Chain: Risk Cascades

The chain can also be read backward as a cascade of risks. A failure at any link reduces or eliminates outcomes at subsequent links.

```
Insufficient civilizational action
  ← Inadequate biosphere function
    ← Insufficient ecosystem recovery
      ← Insufficient survival to maturity
        ← Insufficient establishment
          ← Inadequate intervention
```

This reverse reading is useful for risk analysis. If interventions are inadequate, all subsequent links fail. If establishment fails, all subsequent links fail. The chain is only as strong as its weakest link.

The **practically weakest link** is Link 2 (establishment to survival-to-maturity), as established. The **least controllable link** is Link 7 (biosphere to civilizational), where Robotanica's contribution is real but not determinative.

The chain's strength lies in **structural commitments that protect each link**:

- Codex discipline protects Link 1.
- Multi-decade Lease structure protects Link 2.
- Multi-species native recipes protect Link 3.
- Bioregional balance protects Link 4.
- Acknowledgment of restoration's role within larger civilizational action protects honesty about Link 5.

## 9. The Federation's Theory of Change

Beyond the ecological chain, Robotanica has a **theory of change about the federation itself**:

### 9.1 Federation Hypothesis

**Claim:** A federated, protocol-based, charter-bound structure can scale restoration in ways no single organization can.

### 9.2 Why This Claim

- The trillion-tree objective requires capital, expertise, and operations beyond any single organization.
- Local stewardship requires local knowledge that no central organization can assemble.
- Multi-decade work requires institutional structures more durable than any single institution.
- Indigenous and community sovereignty require federated governance.
- Forking discipline (Charter §5.4) requires that no single organization controls the protocol.

### 9.3 Evidence for the Claim

- Internet protocols enable global coordination without central control.
- Open-source ecosystems demonstrate large-scale federation succeeding.
- Cooperative federations (credit unions, mutual insurance, etc.) have multi-century records.
- Indigenous federations have governance traditions of multi-generational endurance.

The claim is not novel; federated structures have track records. Whether Robotanica's specific instantiation of federation succeeds is the open question.

### 9.4 Assumptions

- Charter principles are durable enough to bind across decades and leaders.
- Protocol is implementable enough to support diverse federation members.
- Governance bodies (Council, Stewardship Office, AIERB) function with integrity.
- Forking is welcomed enough to remain a real check on capture.
- Open-source defaults survive commercial pressure.

### 9.5 Failure Modes

- Charter capture (per Threat Model).
- Protocol fragmentation that breaks interoperability.
- Federation governance becoming dominant institutions.
- Forks proliferating to the point where coordination dissolves.
- Open commitments eroding under partner pressure.

### 9.6 The Bet

Robotanica is betting that federation is more durable than monolithic organization at the scale and horizon required. The corpus is the discipline making that bet credible.

## 10. The Trillion-Tree Number, Examined

The headline metric — one trillion adult trees — is itself a claim. The Theory of Change should examine it.

### 10.1 Why a Trillion?

The number is roughly:

- **One-third of current global tree count** (~3 trillion trees globally per Crowther et al. 2015 and subsequent estimates).
- **Approaching potential global restoration capacity** as estimated by Bastin et al. 2019 and similar (with significant disputes about the upper bound).
- **A round number** at the order of magnitude where restoration could materially contribute to climate stabilization.

### 10.2 Why Not 100 Billion or 10 Trillion?

The number could plausibly be different:

- **100 billion trees** would be substantial but might not move the needle on global biosphere function.
- **10 trillion trees** is beyond plausible global restoration capacity given existing land use, biodiversity, and cultural constraints.
- **1 trillion** sits in the range that is large enough to matter and small enough to be conceivably achievable.

### 10.3 The Number is a Target, Not a Constraint

The mission is biosphere restoration. The trillion-tree number is the **operational form** of that mission for tracking purposes. If the work shows that fewer trees with greater ecological function deliver biosphere outcomes more effectively, the number adapts.

The Charter §1 commits to the trillion-tree objective as the primary metric. It does not commit Robotanica to that being the only valid framing.

### 10.4 The Adult-Tree Qualifier

The qualifier is more important than the number. "One trillion seedlings planted" is achievable cheaply with no biosphere outcome. "One trillion adult trees" requires the entire Theory of Change to hold across all six links.

The qualifier is the discipline.

## 11. Validation Strategy

How does Robotanica know if the Theory of Change is holding?

### 11.1 Per-Link Monitoring

Each link has its own monitoring:

- **Link 1 (Intervention to Establishment):** Establishment rates per cohort per recipe per region.
- **Link 2 (Establishment to Survival-to-Maturity):** Annual survival rates and projection models.
- **Link 3 (Survival to Ecosystem Recovery):** Multi-indicator ecosystem assessments.
- **Link 4 (Ecosystem Recovery to Biosphere Function):** Aggregate outcome contribution.
- **Link 5 (Biosphere to Civilizational):** Cited engagement with global biosphere indicators (acknowledging this link is partly outside Robotanica's measurement).

### 11.2 Falsification Tracking

The Theory of Change is **explicitly falsifiable**. If:

- Establishment rates routinely fall below 50% across recipes and regions, Link 1 is in trouble.
- Year-30 survival rates routinely fall below 40% projection, Link 2 is in trouble.
- Multi-indicator ecosystem assessments fail to show recovery, Link 3 is in trouble.
- Aggregate outcomes fail to scale with hectares, Link 4 is in trouble.
- Biosphere indicators fail to respond to scaled restoration, Link 5 is questioned.

The discipline is to **publish these failure thresholds in advance**, so that subsequent results can be honestly judged against them.

### 11.3 External Validation

External validators contribute:

- Scientific literature comparing Robotanica's outcomes to other restoration projects.
- Independent verifiers (per MRV §7) assessing Link 1-3 outcomes.
- IPCC, IPBES, and similar assessments contextualizing Link 4-5 questions.
- External critique (Threat Model §3.5) testing assumptions.

### 11.4 Refinement Cadence

The Theory of Change is reviewed annually for material new evidence. Substantial revision is reserved for the five-year comprehensive review per the corpus's amendment processes.

## 12. What Robotanica Is Not Claiming

Honesty requires stating what the Theory of Change does **not** claim:

- That restoration alone solves climate change. It is one component.
- That restoration substitutes for emissions reduction. It does not.
- That ecosystem recovery to historical reference is achievable everywhere. It is gradient.
- That biosphere function contributions are precisely calculable. They are uncertain.
- That trillion-tree achievement is guaranteed by the corpus. It is the work.
- That Robotanica is the only or best path to restoration. It is one approach.
- That the Theory of Change is final. It evolves.

These caveats are part of the theory. Without them, the claim is overreach; with them, the claim is accurate within its bounds.

## 13. Why This Theory of Change

The theory could have been structured differently. Alternative theories include:

- **Pure carbon sequestration:** "Plant trees → sequester carbon → mitigate climate." Simpler but ignores biodiversity, hydrology, and the survival-to-maturity discipline.
- **Indigenous sovereignty primacy:** "Support indigenous stewardship → biodiversity preserved → biosphere function." More direct on sovereignty but constrained in scale by indigenous-stewarded land.
- **Technology displacement:** "Robotic restoration → economic feasibility → scale." More techno-optimistic but underweights social and ecological complexity.

Robotanica's theory **integrates** elements from these alternatives:

- Carbon and ecosystem function valued together.
- Indigenous sovereignty structural rather than ancillary.
- Technology serving the work rather than driving it.

The integration is the project. The theory's distinctiveness is the integration, not any single link.

## 14. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in the Theory of Change:

- **Quantitative magnitudes at links 4-5.** How much restoration at what scale produces what biosphere and civilizational effect remains uncertain. The Theory acknowledges this; it does not resolve it.
- **The role of restoration relative to other interventions.** Restoration's relative weight in the basket of necessary interventions is contested. Whether to advocate for restoration as primary or restoration as one of many is a real choice.
- **Climate divergence breaking the chain.** If climate trajectory exceeds bounds, links 3-5 may break. Robotanica's posture under such conditions is not specified in this Theory.
- **Cultural and economic pathways.** The Theory focuses on ecological causation. Cultural and economic causation (how people relate to land, how economies value biosphere) are part of why or why not restoration succeeds; they are underspecified here.
- **Causation versus correlation.** Some claimed restoration outcomes may correlate with restoration without being caused by it (climate, regulatory change, market factors). Attribution discipline (MRV §9) addresses this but it remains an analytical challenge.
- **Tree count vs. biosphere function metric.** The Charter's emphasis on tree count drives this Theory; an alternative emphasis on biosphere function metrics (biodiversity index, carbon stock, ecosystem service value) would produce a different Theory.
- **Robotanica's specific contribution at scale.** How much of any global restoration outcome is attributable to Robotanica versus other actors is a counterfactual question that does not have clean answer at large scale.

## 15. Summary in One Page

**Mission:** Restore the biosphere. Operational metric: 10¹² adult trees.

**The chain:**

1. Robotanica intervenes (planting, tending, monitoring under Codex recipes with FPIC and Charter discipline).
2. Cohorts establish (60–90% survival through establishment per evidence base).
3. Established cohorts survive to maturity (target 50–80% over 30+ years; this is the hardest link).
4. Mature cohorts contribute to ecosystem recovery (multi-indicator outcomes per MRV).
5. Aggregate ecosystem recovery contributes to biosphere function (real but uncertain magnitude).
6. Biosphere function contributes to civilizational outcomes (necessary but not sufficient).

**The disciplines protecting each link:**

- Link 1: Codex discipline + Science Council review + OpenFactory quality.
- Link 2: Multi-decade Lease + reconciliation reserves + climate-aware provenance.
- Link 3: Multi-species native recipes + holobiont matching + connectivity awareness.
- Link 4: Bioregional balance + leakage monitoring + portfolio approach.
- Link 5: Honesty about restoration as one of multiple necessary components.

**The federation theory:** Federated structure with charter discipline scales restoration in ways monolithic organization cannot.

**The bet:** That discipline at every link, sustained across decades, produces aggregate outcomes that matter for biosphere function. That federation produces durability and reach beyond what Robotanica alone could achieve.

**The validation:** Falsifiable thresholds at each link, external verification, transparent failure when thresholds are missed.

This is the Theory of Change. It is a working hypothesis. It will be revised. The work is making it true.
