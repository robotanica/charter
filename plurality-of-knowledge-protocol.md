# Plurality of Knowledge Operational Protocol

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Operational specification
**Status:** Draft for ratification
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Gaia PRD, MRV Specification, Public Communication Doctrine, Genetic Strategy

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies how Robotanica operationalizes Charter §2.7 — the commitment to plurality of knowledge as a first-class principle.

The corpus repeatedly asserts that indigenous, traditional, and scientific knowledge are first-class. The MRV Specification (§7.6) commits to indigenous verification. The Genetic Strategy (§7) commits to indigenous seed sovereignty. The Public Communication Doctrine (§13) commits to indigenous voice. The AI Ethical Review Framework (§11.3) commits to sovereignty in agent operations.

But how this happens operationally — how indigenous knowledge actually enters the Codex, how traditional ecological knowledge interacts with quantitative MRV, how knowledge integrity is preserved across distinct traditions, how attribution and reciprocity actually work — is underspecified. The corpus repeatedly defers to "community-led process" without specifying mechanism.

This Protocol fills that gap.

The Protocol is **operational, not philosophical**. It does not attempt to resolve the question of how different knowledge systems relate to each other epistemologically. It specifies how Robotanica works with multiple knowledge traditions in practice, while respecting their distinctness.

The Protocol is **community-co-developed, not imposed**. The mechanisms specified here are starting positions. Specific community partnerships develop more specific protocols through consent processes. The Protocol provides framework; communities determine substance within it.

---

## 2. The Problem

### 2.1 Why Plurality Matters

Restoration cannot succeed through scientific knowledge alone. Communities have stewarded ecosystems for generations using knowledge that science is only beginning to recognize. Many critical practices — when to plant, where, with what companion species, in what relationship to seasonal cycles, with what tending — are encoded in cultural traditions that no scientific paper captures.

A project that treats scientific knowledge as primary and indigenous knowledge as supplementary will fail twice:

- **Operationally** — by missing the practical knowledge that makes restoration succeed in specific places.
- **Ethically** — by replicating patterns of extraction and erasure that have characterized most prior environmental projects.

### 2.2 The Risk of Bad Integration

There is a worse failure than ignoring indigenous knowledge: pretending to integrate it while extracting it.

- "Integration" that converts indigenous knowledge into scientific frameworks while discarding what doesn't fit.
- "Inclusion" that takes specific facts (this plant heals, that pattern indicates rain) without the cosmological context that makes the knowledge work.
- "Collaboration" that uses indigenous validation as legitimation while indigenous communities themselves benefit minimally.
- "Respect" expressed in language without structural changes in decision-making, attribution, or compensation.

The Protocol must structurally prevent these failures, not just verbally avoid them.

### 2.3 What Integrity Requires

Knowledge integrity across plurality requires:

- **Recognition** — Each tradition is recognized in its own terms.
- **Distinctness** — Traditions are not collapsed into a single epistemic frame.
- **Mutual translation** — Traditions can interact without one being the master language.
- **Attribution** — Knowledge contributions are attributed to their sources, with appropriate scope.
- **Reciprocity** — Contributions flow in multiple directions; not just from communities to Robotanica.
- **Sovereignty** — Communities determine how their knowledge is used.
- **Refusal** — Communities can refuse engagement, and refusal is honored.

This Protocol operationalizes these requirements.

## 3. Knowledge Categories

For operational purposes, Robotanica recognizes several knowledge categories. The categorization is pragmatic, not metaphysical. Specific knowledge may span categories.

### 3.1 Scientific Ecological Knowledge

Knowledge produced through scientific methodology: peer-reviewed publications, replicable studies, formalized models, observational records. Encompasses field ecology, climate science, soil science, biology, atmospheric science, hydrology, and adjacent disciplines.

Characteristics: usually quantitative, typically modular (claims separable from cosmological framework), institutionally produced, methodology contestable within the same epistemic frame.

### 3.2 Indigenous Ecological Knowledge

Knowledge held by indigenous peoples, developed through generations of relationship with specific landscapes. Traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) is a related term; both are used here.

Characteristics: often integrated with cosmology, ceremony, and language; site-specific and seasonally coherent; knowledge holders have responsibility for the knowledge; transmission is through practice, often inseparable from cultural continuity.

### 3.3 Local and Traditional Ecological Knowledge

Knowledge held by long-resident communities (often non-indigenous): pastoralists, farmers, fishers, foresters. Includes traditional practices that are documented or partially scientific in origin but maintained in practice for generations.

Characteristics: practically focused, often partially documented, transmission through local communities and apprenticeship.

### 3.4 Restoration Practitioner Knowledge

Knowledge accumulated through restoration practice itself: lessons from successful and failed projects, operational know-how, recipe variations.

Characteristics: practical, sometimes documented, sometimes tacit; growing rapidly; partially institutionalized through restoration networks.

### 3.5 Cross-Cutting Categories

Some knowledge spans categories:

- **Indigenous-scientific collaborative knowledge** — co-developed by indigenous and scientific researchers.
- **Citizen science observations** — contributed by individuals in defined frameworks.
- **Industry technical knowledge** — held by operators, nurseries, equipment suppliers.

The Protocol applies across all these categories, with category-specific protocols for the more sensitive areas (particularly indigenous knowledge per §6).

## 4. Principles

These principles bind Robotanica's engagement with knowledge across all categories.

### 4.1 Recognition Without Hierarchy

**KNOW-001** All knowledge categories are recognized as first-class in their own terms. None is treated as superior; none is treated as inferior.

**KNOW-002** Where knowledge from different categories conflicts, the conflict is named and engaged on its terms, not resolved by privileging one tradition.

### 4.2 Translation Discipline

**KNOW-010** Translation between knowledge categories is approached carefully. Translation is never neutral; it always introduces frame shifts and potential loss.

**KNOW-011** Translations preserve attribution to the source tradition. A scientific characterization of indigenous knowledge does not become "scientific knowledge"; it remains attributed to the originating community.

### 4.3 Refusal Honored

**KNOW-020** Communities and knowledge holders can refuse to share knowledge. Refusal is respected absolutely.

**KNOW-021** Knowledge that has been shared can be withdrawn from active use; consent is not perpetual.

### 4.4 Reciprocity

**KNOW-030** Knowledge flows are reciprocal. Communities sharing knowledge receive value in return: capacity, infrastructure, financial benefit, knowledge they need, technical assistance, recognition.

**KNOW-031** Reciprocity is negotiated per partnership, not standardized.

### 4.5 Compensation

**KNOW-040** Where indigenous or traditional knowledge contributes materially to recipes, methodology, or other Robotanica outputs, this is compensated through community benefit-sharing per the Stewardship Lease and partnership agreements.

**KNOW-041** Compensation is not pay-per-piece commodification; it is partnership-level resource flow recognizing collective contribution.

### 4.6 Attribution Discipline

**KNOW-050** Knowledge contributions are attributed at appropriate scope:

- Specific community for community-specific knowledge.
- Knowledge holders within communities where they consent and the community concurs.
- Scientific authorship per scientific norms for scientific contributions.
- Anonymous attribution where individual identification is inappropriate.

### 4.7 Sovereignty Structural

**KNOW-060** Communities determine how their knowledge is used. This sovereignty applies to access, scope, duration, and revocation.

### 4.8 No Extractive Integration

**KNOW-070** Robotanica does not "extract" indigenous or traditional knowledge into formats that strip it from its source context. Where formalization is needed (e.g., for Codex inclusion), the formalization preserves attribution and includes contextual links.

### 4.9 Plurality Visible

**KNOW-080** The Codex and other Robotanica outputs make plurality visible: distinct knowledge traditions are visibly distinct, not blended into a homogenized frame.

## 5. The Codex as Plural-Knowledge Repository

The Gaia Codex (per Gaia PRD §6) is where plurality is operationally demonstrated.

### 5.1 Codex Knowledge Layers

**LAY-001** The Codex is structured to recognize multiple knowledge layers:

- **Scientific layer** — peer-reviewed and formal scientific knowledge.
- **Practitioner layer** — restoration practitioner knowledge.
- **Indigenous and traditional layer** — community-contributed knowledge held under sovereignty.
- **Cross-references** — explicit links between layers without merging.

### 5.2 Recipe Authoring Rights

**LAY-010** Recipes can be authored across knowledge categories:

- Scientific contributors can propose recipes through peer-review process.
- Indigenous and traditional knowledge holders can author recipes in their own voice and frame, with their attribution.
- Cross-category recipes (multi-author with mixed sources) are explicitly so identified.

### 5.3 Distinct But Linked

**LAY-020** Indigenous and traditional knowledge in the Codex is distinct from scientific knowledge:

- Different metadata schemas reflect different traditions.
- Indigenous-authored entries are not normalized into scientific format.
- Scientific entries that draw on indigenous knowledge cite the indigenous source explicitly with appropriate attribution scope.

### 5.4 Sovereignty Markers

**LAY-030** Each Codex entry carries a sovereignty declaration per Protocol §10. Indigenous-contributed entries' sovereignty markers determine:

- Who can access the entry.
- For what purposes.
- For how long.
- Under what attribution.
- Under what conditions of revocation.

### 5.5 Translation Annotations

**LAY-040** Where Codex content is translated between categories or languages, translation annotations are preserved:

- Original language and tradition.
- Translator identity and qualification.
- Date of translation.
- Notes on translation choices.
- Indication of what may be lost in translation.

### 5.6 No Default to Scientific Frame

**LAY-050** The Codex does not default to scientific framing for queries about indigenous-contributed content. A query about a recipe contributed by a community returns the community's framing, not a scientific gloss.

### 5.7 Refused Content

**LAY-060** Communities may withdraw or refuse to add content; refusal is honored. The absence of indigenous content in the Codex for a region may reflect community decision, not absence of knowledge.

## 6. Indigenous Knowledge Protocols

Indigenous knowledge requires specific protocols beyond the general principles.

### 6.1 CARE Principles

**IND-001** Robotanica's engagement with indigenous knowledge follows the CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics).

### 6.2 FPIC for Knowledge Sharing

**IND-010** Free, Prior, and Informed Consent applies to knowledge sharing as it does to land-use decisions:

- Communities are fully informed about what knowledge is sought, by whom, for what purpose, with what compensation, with what attribution, and with what implications.
- Consent is given through community governance, not individual transactions.
- Consent for one purpose does not transfer to other purposes.
- Consent is revocable.

### 6.3 Recognition of Communal Ownership

**IND-020** Where knowledge is communal (held by the community, not individuals), Robotanica engages with the community's governance:

- Knowledge transfers occur through community processes.
- Compensation flows to the community (not just individuals).
- Attribution names the community, not just individuals (with individual recognition where appropriate).

### 6.4 Knowledge in Context

**IND-030** Indigenous knowledge is engaged in context, not extracted:

- Sites where knowledge is shared are part of the knowledge.
- Ceremonies, language, and cosmological framing are part of the knowledge.
- Knowledge separated from context is impoverished; full extraction is refused.

### 6.5 Indigenous Knowledge Holders

**IND-040** Specific knowledge holders within communities (elders, ceremonial specialists, herbalists, fire keepers) are recognized:

- Their knowledge transmission is supported, not bypassed.
- Their authority over their domains is respected.
- Their compensation reflects their role.

### 6.6 Traditional Ecological Knowledge as Verification

**IND-050** Indigenous knowledge can verify outcomes alongside scientific verification (per MRV §7.6):

- Indigenous verification is not "complementary" to scientific in the sense of secondary; it is parallel and equally valid.
- Verification reports can be generated through indigenous protocols on indigenous-stewarded parcels.
- These verifications are credit-issuance valid.

### 6.7 Reciprocity in Knowledge Exchange

**IND-060** Where Robotanica's scientific knowledge benefits indigenous communities (e.g., information about climate trajectories affecting their lands), this knowledge flows back. Knowledge exchange is multi-directional.

### 6.8 Indigenous Federations and Networks

**IND-070** Robotanica engages with indigenous federations and networks as appropriate:

- Inter-community knowledge sharing is supported but managed by communities themselves.
- Robotanica does not become a clearinghouse for indigenous knowledge across communities.
- Inter-community decisions about knowledge sharing are made by the communities involved.

## 7. Local and Traditional Knowledge Protocols

Local communities (non-indigenous) holding traditional ecological knowledge have parallel but distinct protocols.

### 7.1 Recognition

**LOC-001** Local and traditional ecological knowledge is recognized as first-class. Long-residency on the land establishes knowledge that cannot be replicated elsewhere.

### 7.2 Compensation and Attribution

**LOC-010** Compensation and attribution principles apply to local knowledge holders parallel to indigenous protocols, adapted to community structures.

### 7.3 Cultural Continuity Support

**LOC-020** Where local knowledge is at risk (aging knowledge holders, loss of practice), Robotanica's engagement may support documentation, transmission, and continuity in addition to direct integration into operations.

### 7.4 Distinction From Indigenous Protocols

**LOC-030** Local knowledge protocols are distinct from indigenous protocols. The distinction reflects:

- Different historical relationships to the land.
- Different sovereignty claims.
- Different governance structures.
- Different vulnerability profiles.

Conflation is refused. Each tradition is engaged on its own terms.

## 8. Practitioner Knowledge Integration

Restoration practitioner knowledge enters the Codex through documented learning.

### 8.1 Operator Learning Capture

**PRAC-001** Operators are supported in capturing learning from their work: what works, what fails, what surprises, what to try next.

**PRAC-002** Learning capture is a federation responsibility, not an individual operator's burden alone.

### 8.2 Federation Learning Cycles

**PRAC-010** Periodic federation learning cycles aggregate practitioner learning across operators and regions:

- Annual practitioner gatherings.
- Cross-regional knowledge exchange.
- Codex updates incorporating practitioner learnings.

### 8.3 Practitioner Authorship in the Codex

**PRAC-020** Practitioners are co-authors of Codex entries based on their work, with appropriate recognition.

### 8.4 Failure Documentation

**PRAC-030** Failed approaches are as important to capture as successful ones (per MRV §6.5 reporting symmetry). Practitioner failure documentation is structurally encouraged.

## 9. Scientific Knowledge Integration

Scientific knowledge enters the Codex through traditional academic channels and adapted ones.

### 9.1 Peer-Reviewed Publication

**SCI-001** Peer-reviewed scientific work is the primary entry of formal scientific knowledge into the Codex.

**SCI-002** Robotanica supports scientific publication: publishing operational data, contributing to scientific literature, supporting researcher access to data per sovereignty constraints.

### 9.2 Robotanica-Specific Scientific Contributions

**SCI-010** Robotanica's own work generates scientific knowledge: outcome data, methodology innovations, novel findings. This is published openly per Charter §5.1-5.3.

### 9.3 Scientific Council Verification

**SCI-020** The Science Council verifies major scientific claims entering Codex per the Council Charter.

### 9.4 Scientific-Indigenous Co-Production

**SCI-030** Where scientific work co-produces with indigenous communities, the protocols of §6 apply alongside scientific norms.

## 10. Knowledge Sovereignty Implementation

Sovereignty is implemented through specific mechanisms.

### 10.1 Sovereignty Declaration on Knowledge Entries

**SOV-001** Each knowledge entry in the Codex carries a sovereignty declaration:

- The sovereign party.
- Permitted access scope (public, federation members, named parties).
- Permitted use scope (Robotanica internal, federation, scientific publication, commercial use).
- Time bounds (perpetual, time-limited).
- Revocation rights.

### 10.2 Access Control

**SOV-010** Access to sovereignty-restricted knowledge enforces the sovereignty declaration:

- Technical: access controls in Gaia.
- Organizational: personnel training on sovereignty handling.
- Audit: access logs reviewed for compliance.

### 10.3 Withdrawal Procedures

**SOV-020** When a community withdraws consent for knowledge use:

- The knowledge is removed from active use immediately.
- Cached or distributed copies are addressed per the Data Lifecycle and Preservation Plan.
- Future use of the knowledge is prevented.
- Past use does not retroactively become unauthorized.

### 10.4 Conflict of Sovereignty

**SOV-030** Where multiple parties claim sovereignty over knowledge (different communities, communities and scientists), the conflict is engaged through governance, not unilateral resolution.

### 10.5 Sovereignty in Translations and Derivatives

**SOV-040** Translations and derivatives of sovereignty-restricted knowledge inherit the sovereignty:

- A scientific paper based on indigenous knowledge requires consent for the publication.
- A recipe derived from traditional practice cites and acknowledges the source.
- Derivative use does not weaken sovereignty; it preserves it.

## 11. Compensation and Reciprocity Mechanisms

Material compensation flows recognize knowledge contributions.

### 11.1 Community-Level Compensation

**COMP-001** Where communities contribute knowledge, compensation flows to the community per their governance:

- Direct payment per partnership agreement.
- In-kind support (training, infrastructure, equipment).
- Capacity building.
- Land restoration in their territory.

### 11.2 Knowledge Holder Recognition

**COMP-010** Where specific knowledge holders contribute (with community consent), they receive recognition:

- Public attribution (where consent applies).
- Direct compensation per partnership.
- Continued relationship and engagement.

### 11.3 Royalty-Like Structures Where Appropriate

**COMP-020** Where indigenous or traditional knowledge contributes to credit-generating outcomes, ongoing revenue sharing flows to the contributing communities:

- Defined fraction of credit revenue per Lease and partnership.
- Indefinite rather than time-limited (knowledge enabling ongoing outcomes warrants ongoing share).

### 11.4 Reciprocal Knowledge Sharing

**COMP-030** Robotanica's scientific knowledge is shared back to communities:

- Climate trajectory information for their lands.
- Ecological data they request.
- Technical training they want.

### 11.5 Capacity Building

**COMP-040** Where communities want to develop their own scientific capacity, indigenous-led research programs, or other knowledge infrastructure, Robotanica supports:

- Funding indigenous-led research where consistent with mission.
- Technical assistance to community-led programs.
- Partnership without paternalism.

### 11.6 No Knowledge Commodification

**COMP-050** Knowledge is not bought and sold per item. Compensation is partnership-level and relational, not transactional. Quoting a price for a piece of indigenous knowledge is refused.

## 12. Translation Discipline

Translation between knowledge frames requires care.

### 12.1 What Translation Means

**TRAN-001** Translation can mean:

- Linguistic translation (e.g., from one language to another).
- Frame translation (from indigenous cosmology to scientific framing).
- Format translation (from oral to written, from narrative to data table).

Each kind of translation has specific protocols.

### 12.2 Linguistic Translation

**TRAN-010** Linguistic translation is performed:

- Where possible, by speakers of both source and target language.
- With community involvement and consent.
- With preservation of source-language version.
- With annotation of translation choices.

### 12.3 Frame Translation

**TRAN-020** Frame translation (e.g., indigenous knowledge into scientific framework) is performed only where:

- The community consents to the specific translation.
- The community is involved in the translation.
- The source frame is preserved alongside the target frame, not replaced.
- Loss in translation is acknowledged.

**TRAN-021** Frame translation does not "scientize" indigenous knowledge. The translation is annotation, not normalization.

### 12.4 Format Translation

**TRAN-030** Format translation (e.g., oral knowledge to written, narrative to structured data) is performed:

- With community consent.
- Preserving the original format reference.
- Recognizing that format itself is meaning-bearing.

### 12.5 Refused Translations

**TRAN-040** Some translations are refused:

- Sacred knowledge translated to secular frames.
- Ceremonial knowledge separated from ceremony.
- Knowledge whose meaning depends on relationship contexts that cannot translate.

Where translation is refused, the knowledge remains in its source frame; Robotanica's operations adapt to work with the knowledge in its own form.

## 13. Knowledge in MRV

How plurality enters Measurement, Reporting, and Verification.

### 13.1 Indigenous Verification

**MRV-IND-001** Indigenous verifiers can perform verification per MRV §7.6:

- Verification protocols developed with indigenous communities.
- Verifiers from indigenous communities trained and accredited.
- Reports generated in indigenous language and framework where appropriate.
- Reports valid for credit issuance.

### 13.2 Multi-Modal Evidence

**MRV-MM-001** MRV can incorporate multiple modes of evidence (per MRV §5.1):

- Quantitative measurements.
- Qualitative observations.
- Indigenous and traditional verification.
- Practitioner field reports.

The combination strengthens verification; a single mode is rarely sufficient.

### 13.3 Disagreement Resolution

**MRV-DIS-001** Where verification across knowledge categories produces different conclusions, the disagreement is engaged as substantive:

- Both findings are documented.
- Investigation explores why the difference exists.
- Resolution may involve recipe revision, methodology refinement, or recognition that the categories highlight different aspects.

### 13.4 Knowledge as Outcome

**MRV-KNOW-001** Knowledge generation itself can be an outcome of restoration:

- Codex contributions documenting what was learned.
- Capacity building in communities and operators.
- Practitioner network knowledge accumulation.

These knowledge outcomes are tracked alongside ecological outcomes.

## 14. Governance

### 14.1 Knowledge Governance Bodies

Knowledge plurality is governed across multiple bodies:

- **Stewardship Office** — operational implementation, partnership oversight.
- **Science Council** — scientific knowledge entries, peer review, methodology.
- **Indigenous representation** — through Council seats per Council Charter §3.4 and through community partnerships.
- **AI Ethics Review Board** — for agent interaction with knowledge per AI Framework §11.3.

### 14.2 Conflict Resolution

**GOV-001** Conflicts about knowledge — disagreements about content, attribution, sovereignty, compensation — are resolved through governance:

- Direct community-Robotanica discussion first.
- Mediation through Stewardship Office.
- Council review where methodological.
- Federation governance where structural.

### 14.3 Annual Review

**GOV-010** This Protocol is reviewed annually with input from:

- Communities engaged with Robotanica.
- Scientific contributors.
- Practitioners.
- Council and Stewardship Office.

Annual review may produce updates; major revisions follow Charter governance.

### 14.4 Public Reporting on Plurality

**GOV-020** The annual report (Public Communication Doctrine §6.1) includes:

- Summary of knowledge contributions and partnerships.
- Compensation flows by category.
- Refusals and withdrawals.
- Issues and conflicts.
- Forward direction.

## 15. Refused Practices

The Protocol categorically refuses:

### 15.1 Knowledge Extraction

**REF-001** Extracting indigenous or traditional knowledge from communities for use elsewhere without ongoing relationship and benefit-sharing is refused.

### 15.2 Scientific Validation as Master

**REF-002** Treating scientific validation as the test indigenous knowledge must pass is refused. Scientific frameworks are one frame among several; indigenous knowledge does not require scientific endorsement to be valid.

### 15.3 Performative Inclusion

**REF-003** Including indigenous representation as performance — visible at meetings, absent from decisions — is refused. Inclusion that doesn't produce structural change is rejected.

### 15.4 Bioprospecting

**REF-004** Bioprospecting — extracting genetic or chemical material based on traditional knowledge for commercial use — is categorically refused per Charter §5.3 (no patenting) and Genetic Strategy §3.6.

### 15.5 Knowledge Stripping

**REF-005** Stripping indigenous knowledge of its cultural and cosmological context to deploy "the practical bits" is refused. Either Robotanica engages with the knowledge in its full context or it does not engage.

### 15.6 Speaking for Communities

**REF-006** Robotanica representing indigenous or traditional perspectives in public communication or policy advocacy without community consent is refused per Public Communication Doctrine §13.

## 16. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in operationalizing plurality:

- **Quantification interface.** How traditional knowledge that resists quantification interacts with quantitative MRV remains a hard question. The Protocol provides framework; specific cases require partnership-by-partnership development.
- **Cross-community knowledge sharing.** When one indigenous community holds knowledge another community could benefit from, the inter-community sharing process is community-determined, not Robotanica-managed. The boundaries of Robotanica's role require ongoing clarification.
- **Knowledge under threat.** When traditional knowledge faces existential threat (aging knowledge holders, displacement, cultural erosion), Robotanica's role in supporting preservation is welcome where invited but cannot substitute for cultural continuity.
- **Attribution at scale.** As the federation operates across many regions and communities, attribution becomes operationally complex. Specific systems for tracking and honoring attributions need development.
- **Sovereignty in distributed systems.** Knowledge stored in distributed systems (Gaia replicated across regions, federation members holding copies) creates sovereignty enforcement challenges. The Data Lifecycle Plan addresses sovereignty technically; the cultural and political dimensions need ongoing attention.
- **Generational knowledge transition.** Knowledge holders within communities change across generations. Continuity of relationships through these transitions requires intentional partnership work.
- **Conflicting indigenous claims.** Where indigenous communities have overlapping or conflicting claims to knowledge or territory, Robotanica engages without taking sides, supporting community-led resolution.
- **Western versus indigenous-led restoration ecology.** The very concept of "restoration ecology" is sometimes critiqued as Western. Robotanica's posture is to engage with this critique while continuing the work, recognizing the discipline itself is partial and evolving.

*Plurality of knowledge is not a slogan; it is a practice. The practice is to recognize that the knowledge required to restore the biosphere is held in multiple traditions, none of which alone is sufficient, none of which is reducible to another. This Protocol specifies how Robotanica engages with plurality operationally — through recognition, sovereignty, attribution, reciprocity, and refusal of extraction. The work is restoration; restoration requires this discipline; the discipline is the practice of plurality, not its proclamation.*
