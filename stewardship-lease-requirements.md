# The Stewardship Lease

## Requirements Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Requirements specification for a legal instrument
**Status:** Draft for legal counsel review
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, A3 PRD (forthcoming), Gaia PRD

---

## 1. Purpose and Status of This Document

The Stewardship Lease is the legal-financial instrument under which a landowner commits land to ecological restoration through Robotanica's federation. It is the actual product of A3.

This document is **not** the lease. It is the requirements specification from which the lease is drafted by qualified legal counsel in each jurisdiction. The lease will exist as multiple jurisdiction-specific instruments (a U.S. version, an EU version, a Brazilian version, an Indonesian version, and so on) that each satisfy the requirements stated here.

The instrument has no settled precedent. The closest analogues are conservation easements, long-term forestry leases, carbon project agreements, and indigenous co-management agreements, each of which the Stewardship Lease draws on but does not replicate. Where this document specifies what the instrument must accomplish without prescribing how, that gap is intentional and is left to legal counsel.

---

## 2. The Problem the Lease Solves

Robotanica needs to operate on land it does not own, on timescales that exceed any government's planning horizon, with returns that accrue to people not yet born, in jurisdictions with widely varying property law, and in a way that respects the rights of indigenous and local communities who may have prior claims.

Existing instruments fail in at least one of these dimensions:

- **Standard leases** — too short, terminable, do not bind successors in interest, and do not encode ecological obligations.
- **Conservation easements** — close in spirit but typically restrict use rather than mandate active restoration; jurisdictionally limited; not designed for federated operation.
- **Carbon project agreements** — focus on offset claims rather than ecosystem integrity; permanence provisions are often inadequate.
- **Co-management agreements** — appropriate for indigenous contexts but are not generalizable templates.

The Stewardship Lease must function as a *new class* of instrument: a long-duration, ecologically-binding, federation-compatible, sovereignty-respecting commitment that operates across jurisdictions.

## 3. Parties

### 3.1 Required Parties

- **The Landowner** — the party with formal title to the land, or the equivalent rights-holder under the relevant jurisdiction.
- **The Operator** — the federation member that conducts day-to-day stewardship operations on the parcel. May be Robotanica itself or another federation member.
- **The Federation** — represented through Robotanica's Stewardship Office, which holds the lease as the long-term counterparty independent of any specific operator.

### 3.2 Required Acknowledged Parties

- **The Indigenous or Local Community** with customary, traditional, or recognized prior rights to the land, where such exists. The lease may not be valid without their Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC), and they are parties for purposes of consent, benefit-sharing, and conflict resolution even where they are not signatories under the formal property law of the jurisdiction.

### 3.3 Successor Parties

The lease must bind successors in interest on all sides:

- A successor landowner (through sale, inheritance, or other transfer) is bound by the lease.
- A successor operator (through transfer, dissolution, or expulsion) inherits operational obligations.
- The Federation persists across organizational changes within Robotanica; if Robotanica dissolves, succession passes to a competent steward as specified in §10.

---

## 4. Term

### 4.1 Minimum Term

The lease has a minimum term consistent with the survival-to-maturity horizon of the target ecosystem. This will not be less than **fifty (50) years** for forest restoration in most contexts. Where target ecosystems require longer maturity (old-growth temperate or boreal forest), terms of seventy-five to one hundred years apply.

### 4.2 Auto-Renewal

The lease auto-renews in defined increments (typically twenty-five years) unless either the Landowner or the Federation gives notice of non-renewal within a stated window before term expiration, subject to §6 (Termination).

### 4.3 Why Not Perpetuity

Perpetual instruments are not available in all jurisdictions and are politically and ethically contested. The lease prefers long, renewable terms with explicit consent at renewal over perpetual instruments imposed once.

---

## 5. Obligations

### 5.1 Operator and Federation Obligations

The Operator and the Federation commit to:

- Conducting restoration in accordance with a recipe drawn from Gaia's interpretation layer, selected with the Landowner's informed consent and (where applicable) the Indigenous or Local Community's consent.
- Maintaining accurate, transparent records in Gaia of all interventions, observations, and outcomes on the parcel.
- Monitoring outcomes for the full duration of the lease, not merely the duration during which the Operator is paid for active intervention.
- Reporting to the Landowner on a defined cadence (minimum annual) on ecological state, intervention activity, and any deviations from the recipe.
- Repairing or compensating for any harm caused by Operator activity beyond the agreed recipe.
- Withdrawing from interventions that are demonstrably failing rather than persisting in pursuit of metrics that have already been compromised.

### 5.2 Landowner Obligations

The Landowner commits to:

- Granting the access and operating rights necessary for the agreed stewardship.
- Refraining from activities on the parcel that materially conflict with the stewardship recipe (specified explicitly in the lease, not by general reference).
- Providing accurate disclosure of historical land use, contamination, water rights, and existing easements relevant to restoration planning.
- Honoring the lease's binding effect on successors.

### 5.3 What the Landowner Is Not Obligated To

The lease must not require the Landowner to:

- Surrender title to the land.
- Grant rights beyond those necessary for the agreed stewardship.
- Bear the operational costs of the restoration, except as voluntarily contracted.
- Indemnify the Operator or Federation for harms caused by the Operator's actions or negligence.

### 5.4 Recipe Reference, Not Recipe Lock-In

The lease references the recipe by its Gaia URI at a specific version. The recipe may be updated within the lease term under defined conditions: when evidence in Gaia indicates the original recipe is failing or suboptimal; when climatic conditions shift materially; or when the Landowner consents to a recipe substitution. Recipe revisions do not terminate the lease; they amend it under §11.

## 6. Termination

### 6.1 Termination by the Landowner

The Landowner may terminate the lease for cause if the Operator or Federation materially breaches its obligations and fails to cure within a defined window. "Material breach" is defined to include: ecological harm beyond the recipe's stated risk envelope, failure to maintain monitoring, false reporting of outcomes, and operator misconduct.

The Landowner may not terminate for convenience during the term, except by buyout (§6.4).

### 6.2 Termination by the Federation

The Federation may terminate if the Landowner materially breaches obligations under §5.2, including unauthorized activities on the parcel that compromise the stewardship.

The Federation must offer succession (§6.5) before terminating where doing so would leave a partially-restored parcel without ongoing stewardship.

### 6.3 Termination by Mutual Agreement

The lease may be terminated at any time by written mutual agreement of all parties, including (where applicable) the Indigenous or Local Community.

### 6.4 Buyout

The lease may include a buyout mechanism by which the Landowner may terminate early in exchange for compensation that reflects:

- The cost of the restoration work performed to date.
- The value of the ecological state achieved (where this can be assessed).
- Any forward commitments (carbon credits, biodiversity credits) that have been issued against the parcel and would need to be unwound or made whole.

Buyout terms must be specified in the lease at signing, not negotiated under duress at termination.

### 6.5 Operator Succession

Where an Operator fails or withdraws but the Landowner and the Federation wish to continue the lease, succession to a new Operator proceeds under the Federation's protocols. The Landowner has a right of consent over the successor.

### 6.6 No Abandonment

Neither the Operator nor the Federation may abandon a parcel — leaving the parcel in a state where active restoration has begun but is not being maintained. Where continued stewardship is impossible, an explicit dissolution under §10 applies.

## 7. Rights and Outputs

### 7.1 Restoration Outputs

The lease must specify the disposition of restoration outputs, including:

- **Biological yields** (timber, fruit, non-timber forest products) where the recipe permits harvest. Default disposition: the Landowner retains rights subject to recipe-specified harvest constraints.
- **Carbon credits and biodiversity credits** generated from the parcel. Default disposition: revenue-sharing on terms specified in the lease, with explicit identification of who issues, holds, and is liable for the credits.
- **Ecosystem service payments** (water provisioning, pollinator services). Default disposition: shared, with terms specified.
- **Genetic and biological material** developed or selected through the work (improved seedlings, microbiome cultures). Default disposition: subject to the Charter's open-source commitments (§5.3 of the Charter — no patenting); access for the Landowner's continued use is preserved.

### 7.2 Data Outputs

Data generated about the parcel — observations, sensor readings, imagery, derived metrics — is governed by the data sovereignty provisions of §8.

### 7.3 No Implicit Rights

Any right not explicitly granted in the lease is retained by the Landowner. The Operator and Federation hold no implicit rights to the land, its products, or its data.

---

## 8. Data and Sovereignty

### 8.1 Data Generated About the Parcel

The lease must specify, for each category of data generated about the parcel:

- Who collects it.
- Where it is stored.
- Who has read access.
- Who has write access.
- What may be published, in what form, with what coarsening.

### 8.2 Default Provisions

Default provisions, which the lease may override on a per-parcel basis:

- Aggregate restoration outcomes (survival rate, biomass) are publishable.
- Precise locations of vulnerable species or sensitive sites are coarsened in public APIs.
- The Landowner has read access to all data generated about the parcel.
- The Landowner may request publication restrictions where exposure would create material risk.

### 8.3 Indigenous Data Sovereignty

Where the parcel falls under §3.1 of the Charter (Indigenous or Local Community involvement), the data regime defaults to community control under the CARE Principles:

- The community determines what data is collected.
- The community determines who has access to it.
- The community determines what is published.
- The community retains rights to traditional ecological knowledge contributed under the lease.

These provisions cannot be overridden by Operator or Federation interest.

### 8.4 Data Portability

If the lease terminates, the Landowner is entitled to a complete export of data generated about the parcel, in open formats, at no cost. This right does not extend to Robotanica's general platform code or aggregate data spanning multiple parcels.

## 9. Compensation and Benefit-Sharing

### 9.1 Forms of Compensation

The lease specifies the forms of compensation flowing between parties. These may include any combination of:

- **Lease payments** to the Landowner (fixed, performance-based, or hybrid).
- **Revenue shares** on restoration outputs (§7.1).
- **In-kind compensation** (services, infrastructure, training).
- **Community benefits** to local and indigenous communities (§9.3).

### 9.2 No Free Land

The lease may not be structured such that the Landowner provides land without material compensation. This is a structural protection against extractive arrangements with vulnerable Landowners.

The Federation may, under separate arrangements, receive grant or philanthropic funding to compensate Landowners who would otherwise be unable to participate; this is a Federation responsibility, not a Landowner concession.

### 9.3 Community Benefit-Sharing

Where the parcel is in or affects an indigenous or local community territory:

- A defined share of revenue and outputs flows to the community, on terms negotiated under FPIC.
- Local employment and training are first-priority for the Operator's labor needs.
- Traditional ecological knowledge contributed under the lease is compensated.

### 9.4 Transparency of Compensation

All compensation flows are recorded and visible to the parties. They are recorded in summary form in the public record, in keeping with the Charter's transparency commitments, with personally identifying information protected.

## 10. Failure and Dissolution

### 10.1 Site-Level Failure

When restoration on the parcel materially fails — through climatic event, contamination, unforeseen ecological collapse, or other cause:

- The Operator and Federation provide a full and prompt assessment, recorded in Gaia, transmitted to the Landowner, and entered into the public record.
- The recipe is reviewed; the Federation's Science Council may require recipe revision before further intervention.
- Any restoration credits issued against the parcel are reconciled honestly: credits that did not in fact occur are voided or made whole through other means.

### 10.2 Operator Failure

When an Operator fails (insolvency, withdrawal, expulsion from the Federation, or material breach), succession proceeds under §6.5. The Landowner is held harmless for the Operator's failure.

### 10.3 Federation Dissolution

If Robotanica's Federation itself dissolves:

- The lease's Federation role passes to a successor steward identified by the dissolution protocols of the Charter.
- If no successor is available, the lease terminates honorably: the Landowner is released, the Operator's obligations are settled, restoration outputs to date are recorded, and the parcel returns to the Landowner with full data delivered.
- Restoration credits issued against the parcel are reconciled before dissolution; the Federation may not dissolve while leaving credit liabilities unresolved.

### 10.4 Catastrophic Loss Outside Anyone's Control

Wildfire, hurricane, drought, regulatory change, or other events outside the parties' control may eliminate the parcel's restoration value. The lease must specify how losses in this category are allocated; default: each party bears its own losses, with no party liable to another for events outside its control. Insurance arrangements may be specified separately.

## 11. Amendment

### 11.1 Routine Amendment

Amendments that do not materially alter the rights, obligations, or compensation of the parties may be made by written agreement of the Operator and Federation, with notice to the Landowner.

### 11.2 Material Amendment

Material amendments — to recipe, term, compensation, rights, or any provision affecting the Landowner's interest — require written consent of all parties, including (where applicable) the Indigenous or Local Community.

### 11.3 Recipe Updates

Recipe updates under §5.4 are a structured form of amendment with a defined consent process, lighter than full material amendment but stronger than routine amendment.

### 11.4 Charter Conformance

No amendment may bring the lease into conflict with the un-amendable provisions of the Robotanica Charter (§8.1 of the Charter). An amendment that purports to do so is void.

---

## 12. Conflict Resolution

### 12.1 Local First

Disputes are resolved at the lowest level competent to resolve them, in keeping with §4.6 of the Charter. The Operator and Landowner attempt resolution directly before escalation.

### 12.2 Mediation

Where direct resolution fails, parties enter mediation through a mutually agreed mediator drawn from a Federation-maintained roster, or through a mediator selected by the parties independently.

### 12.3 Arbitration

Where mediation fails, binding arbitration applies. The lease specifies the seat, language, and rules of arbitration. The seat defaults to the jurisdiction of the parcel.

### 12.4 Indigenous Forums

Where the dispute involves indigenous parties, indigenous dispute resolution forums and customary processes take precedence over Federation procedures, where they exist and the indigenous party invokes them.

### 12.5 No Forced Jurisdiction Shopping

The lease may not require the Landowner to litigate in a jurisdiction other than the parcel's own, except by Landowner consent at signing.

---

## 13. Governing Law and Jurisdictional Variants

### 13.1 Governing Law

The lease is governed by the law of the jurisdiction where the parcel is located, unless all parties affirmatively choose otherwise.

### 13.2 Jurisdictional Variants

A jurisdictional variant of the lease is the form drafted by qualified legal counsel in the relevant jurisdiction. Each variant must satisfy all requirements of this specification. Variants that cannot satisfy a requirement are flagged in the variant's introduction, and the divergence is reported to the Federation for consideration.

### 13.3 No Variant Below Requirements

A jurisdictional variant may strengthen requirements (offer the Landowner more protection, the Indigenous or Local Community more sovereignty, the ecosystem more guarantee). It may not weaken them.

### 13.4 Open Variants

Each jurisdictional variant is published openly in the public record alongside this specification. Federation members and external parties may study, critique, and propose improvements through the amendment process of the Charter and the Federation's protocols.

## 14. What This Specification Does Not Cover

The following are deliberately out of scope and must be addressed in companion documents or by counsel:

- **Tax treatment** — Varies by jurisdiction. The lease should be structured to be tax-favorable to Landowners where possible, but specific provisions are jurisdictional.
- **Securities law** for revenue-share arrangements — Some compensation structures (e.g., participation in carbon credit revenues) may trigger securities regulation in some jurisdictions.
- **Specific indigenous protocols** — Each indigenous community has its own governance and decision processes. These cannot be specified in advance; they are honored through FPIC and through community-led processes at the time of lease formation.
- **Insurance** — Loss allocation under §10.4 may be supported by insurance arrangements that are themselves separate instruments.
- **Operator-Federation operating agreements** — The relationship between an individual Operator and the Federation is governed by the Federation Membership Agreement, not this lease.

## 15. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions where legal counsel input will determine the answer:

- **Standing for ecosystems.** Some jurisdictions (Ecuador, New Zealand, Bolivia, parts of the United States) recognize legal standing for ecosystems or natural features. Where this exists, can the lease incorporate the ecosystem itself as a party? Doing so would strengthen the durability of the instrument.
- **Cross-jurisdictional enforcement.** When a successor landowner or operator is in a different jurisdiction than the parcel, how is enforcement maintained? International instruments (such as the New York Convention for arbitration awards) help, but property rights enforcement is intrinsically jurisdictional.
- **Recording and registration.** What public registrar (land registry, conservation easement registry, custom registry maintained by the Federation) records the lease? Recording defends against subsequent purchasers in good faith but may be unavailable for novel instruments in some jurisdictions.
- **Tax and accounting.** How is the lease accounted for on the Landowner's books? On the Federation's? This affects whether Landowners can participate without adverse tax consequences.
- **Catastrophe coverage at scale.** Climate change makes catastrophic site loss increasingly probable. Insurance markets may not price this rationally. A Federation-level mutual or reinsurance arrangement may be necessary.
- **Standing of future generations.** No legal system robustly grants standing to future generations. Some scholars propose mechanisms (guardianship, trusts for the unborn). Whether the lease should attempt this, or rely on the Federation as effective steward, is unresolved.

## 16. Drafting Process

### 16.1 First Variants

The first jurisdictional variants are commissioned for jurisdictions where pilot operations begin. The selection criterion is operational priority informed by legal feasibility, not the reverse.

### 16.2 Counsel Selection

Counsel for each variant is selected for: experience with conservation easements or comparable instruments; experience with indigenous and community land rights; willingness to publish their drafted variant in the open record; and independence from any party that might pressure for weaker terms.

### 16.3 Public Comment

Each draft variant is posted for public comment for at least 90 days before adoption. Comment is solicited from federation members, indigenous and local communities, scientific advisors, and the public.

### 16.4 Iteration

Variants are revised over time as evidence from operation surfaces gaps, ambiguities, or improvements. Revision follows the amendment processes of §11 and the Charter's amendment process where structural issues are at stake.

## Appendix — Glossary (Lease-Specific)

- **Landowner** — The party with formal title or equivalent rights to the parcel.
- **Operator** — The federation member conducting day-to-day stewardship.
- **Federation** — Robotanica's federation, represented through the Stewardship Office.
- **FPIC** — Free, Prior, and Informed Consent. The principle that indigenous and local communities have the right to give or withhold consent to projects affecting them, on the basis of full information and without coercion.
- **CARE Principles** — Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics. The principles for indigenous data governance.
- **Recipe** — A versioned intervention template in Gaia's interpretation layer.
- **Recipe Instance** — The application of a recipe to a specific parcel under a specific lease.
- **Material Breach** — A breach of obligation that compromises the substance of the lease, as distinguished from a minor or curable breach.
- **Successor in Interest** — A party who succeeds to the rights or obligations of an original party through transfer, inheritance, dissolution, or other change.
