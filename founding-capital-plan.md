# Founding Capital Plan

## Operational Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Operational fundraising and capital plan
**Status:** Draft for founding governance review
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Funding Strategy, White Paper, Threat Model, Bioregional Strategy, Federation Membership Agreement

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

The Funding Strategy specified principles, refusals, and the structural defenses against capture. This Plan is the operational complement: how Robotanica actually raises the founding capital required to begin, who is approached, on what terms, in what sequence, with what governance, and against what milestones.

Without this Plan, the foundation documents are a thought experiment. With it — and with the founding parties to execute it — the project becomes operational.

This Plan covers the **first four years** of capital formation, from pre-founding through the end of Phase 1 (pilot operations). Capital formation beyond that horizon is governed by the Funding Strategy and its annual review cycles; specifics evolve as the project matures.

The Plan is **operational and tactical**. It is more concrete than the Funding Strategy, which means it will date faster. Annual review of this Plan is structural, not exceptional.

## 2. The Capital Problem in Concrete Terms

### 2.1 What Founding Requires

To go from this corpus to first Lease signed, first Cohort planted, first verification published, Robotanica needs to:

- Build and stand up the three platforms at functional v1.
- Hire the founding team.
- Establish founding governance bodies (Stewardship Office, Science Council, AI Ethics Review Board).
- Engage qualified counsel in at least one jurisdiction to draft the first Lease variant.
- Conduct FPIC processes with first community partners.
- Operate first pilot greenhouse production.
- Procure or develop initial robotic equipment.
- Conduct first plantings.
- Establish first verification arrangements.
- Maintain operations through 12-18 months of demonstration.

These are pre-revenue activities. Operating revenue and credit issuance follow demonstration.

### 2.2 Order-of-Magnitude Numbers

Indicative ranges. Specific numbers are subject to detailed budgeting and will be revised:

| Category | Year 1 | Years 2–4 cumulative |
|---|---|---|
| Founding team (15-25 FTE growing to 40-60) | $4–6M | $25–40M |
| Platform development (engineering, infra) | $3–5M | $20–35M |
| Legal (Lease drafting, jurisdictional work, governance instruments) | $1–2M | $5–10M |
| First-region operations (pilot greenhouse, fleet, plantings) | $2–4M | $20–40M |
| Community partnerships (FPIC, capacity building, benefit-sharing) | $1–2M | $10–20M |
| Science Council and Stewardship Office | $1M | $4–6M |
| Verification arrangements | $0.5M | $3–5M |
| Reserves and contingency | $2M | $10–20M |
| Administration and support | $1M | $6–10M |
| **Subtotal** | **$15.5–23M** | **$103–186M** |

**Total Year 1–4: approximately $120–210M.**

These ranges are deliberately wide. They will narrow as scoping work proceeds. The 1.7x range between low and high reflects genuine uncertainty about the ambition of pilot operations, the number of jurisdictional Lease variants pursued in early years, and the scale of community partnerships established.

### 2.3 Beyond the Founding Horizon

Years 5+ shift toward operating revenue, credit issuance, and reduced dependence on founding capital. The Funding Strategy and subsequent Capital Plans cover this transition. This Plan focuses on getting through founding to operational viability.

## 3. Capital Sequencing

Capital comes in phases. Each phase has defined purposes, target amounts, and milestones unlocking the next.

### 3.1 Phase 0 — Pre-Founding (Months 0–6)

**Purpose:** Move from a documented corpus to a founded entity ready to begin operations.

**Target:** $2–4M.

**Use of funds:**

- Founding team for the formation work itself (5-10 people).
- Legal incorporation in the founding jurisdiction.
- Initial Charter ratification process (consultation, refinement).
- First counsel engagement for Lease drafting.
- First scientific advisors engaged (pre-Council).
- Initial community engagement in candidate first-wave bioregions.
- Pilot scoping work.

**Source:** Founder commitments, mission-aligned philanthropic seed grants, or mission-aligned patient capital. Single-source dominance per Funding Strategy §3.1 may be temporarily exceeded during this phase but flagged and time-bound.

**Milestone to advance:** Charter ratified by founding parties; founding entity legally formed; first jurisdictional counsel engaged; Independent Selection Committee for Council seated.

### 3.2 Phase 1 — Founding (Months 6–18)

**Purpose:** Establish the operating base. Hire founding team. Build platform v1. Engage first communities. Draft first Lease.

**Target:** $20–35M raised across this phase, deployed in stages.

**Use of funds:**

- Hire core team to ~20-30 FTE.
- Build Gaia, A3, OpenFactory v1 (sufficient to support first Lease).
- Continue legal work; complete first Lease variant in at least one jurisdiction.
- FPIC process with first community partner(s).
- Procure initial fleet (purchase or partner-supplied).
- Establish first pilot greenhouse.
- Seat Science Council, Stewardship Office, AI Ethics Review Board.

**Source:** Diversified philanthropic capital, supplemented by sovereign grants where available. Multiple sources, no single >20% per Funding Strategy §3.1.

**Milestone to advance:** Platforms at v1; First Lease signed end-to-end; First Cohort planted; Council operational; First verifier accredited.

### 3.3 Phase 2 — Pilot Demonstration (Months 18–36)

**Purpose:** Prove the model works. Multiple Leases, multiple regions, demonstrated outcomes, demonstrated MRV, demonstrated discipline.

**Target:** $50–100M cumulative through end of Phase 2.

**Use of funds:**

- Scale team to ~40-60 FTE.
- Scale platform to multi-region operation.
- Multiple jurisdictional Lease variants drafted.
- Multiple first-wave bioregions engaged.
- Multiple operator and partner relationships.
- First verification cycles completed.
- First small-scale credit issuance.

**Source:** Continuing diversified philanthropic and sovereign sources, with first commercial credit pre-purchases (under MRV discipline) beginning. Funder diversity tightens — ratio compliance restored if it was deviated during Phase 0/1.

**Milestone to advance:** Multiple Leases active; Multiple Cohorts established; First credit issuance verified; First reconciliation event handled correctly; Federation members beyond founding cohort.

### 3.4 Phase 3 — Federation and Operating Revenue (Year 3 onward)

**Purpose:** Transition to a federation operating across multiple regions, with material operating revenue from credits, services, and ecosystem service payments.

**Target:** $100M+ annual capital deployment, with growing share from operating revenue.

**Source mix:** Continuing philanthropic backbone, growing credit revenue, sovereign biosphere bond pilots if developed, ecosystem service payments, operating revenue from federation services.

This Plan covers through Phase 2; Phase 3 governance is per the Funding Strategy.

## 4. Funder Categories and Approach

### 4.1 Tier 1 — Anchor Founding Capital

**Who:** Major mission-aligned foundations and patient-capital funds with billion-dollar-scale commitments to climate, biodiversity, and ecological restoration.

**Examples** (illustrative, not commitments):

- Bezos Earth Fund (climate & biodiversity)
- Bloomberg Philanthropies (climate)
- Gordon and Betty Moore Foundation (biodiversity)
- Wyss Foundation (conservation)
- ClimateWorks Foundation
- Walton Family Foundation (environment program)
- Rockefeller Foundation (innovative finance)
- Schmidt Family Foundation
- Robert Wood Johnson Foundation (where biosphere-health programs apply)
- IKEA Foundation (livelihoods linked to biosphere)
- Sovereign development funds with biosphere mandates (Norway's Climate and Forest Initiative, Germany's IKI, UK's BEIS)

**Target:** $50–100M from this tier across Phases 0–2, distributed across at least 5–8 funders per the diversity mandate.

**Approach:** Direct relationship, multi-meeting cycle, careful articulation of the project's structural defenses against capture. Anchor commitments unlock subsequent rounds.

**Terms:** Multi-year (3–5 year) grants with restricted-use specifications consistent with mission. No conditions compromising Charter principles. Public disclosure per Funding Strategy §3.2.

### 4.2 Tier 2 — Patient Equity and Mission Capital

**Who:** Mission-aligned investors accepting decade-plus horizons with target returns substantially below market rate. This is genuinely scarce capital.

**Examples** (illustrative):

- Patient capital from family offices with documented long-horizon mandates
- Mission-aligned LP capital from foundations' investment programs (often distinct from grant programs)
- Specific funds: Acumen, Capricorn Investment Group, Blue Haven Initiative, certain DBL Partners structures
- Some sovereign wealth funds with restoration mandates

**Target:** $30–60M across Phases 1–2 if patient equity structures are appropriate. This is contingent on legal structuring (Robotanica's entity form constrains what's possible).

**Approach:** Patient capital is a relationship, not a transaction. Commitments build over time as the project demonstrates discipline.

**Terms:** Bounded returns (Personnel Philosophy §3.2: no founder windfalls applies analogously to investor windfalls). Clear treatment in case of dissolution per Charter §7.4.

### 4.3 Tier 3 — Sovereign and Multilateral Grants

**Who:** Governments and multilateral institutions with restoration mandates.

**Examples:**

- Green Climate Fund
- Global Environment Facility (GEF)
- World Bank Forest Carbon Partnership Facility
- IFC (concessional)
- Regional development banks (IADB, AfDB, ADB) for region-specific work
- National restoration budgets where Robotanica operates
- EU programs (LIFE Programme, Horizon Europe restoration calls)

**Target:** $20–50M across Phases 1–2, varying with bioregional engagement.

**Approach:** Slower process than philanthropic. Often requires partnership with national or regional institutions. Often grants rather than loans for restoration work.

**Terms:** Sovereign and multilateral grants typically include detailed reporting and audit requirements compatible with Robotanica's transparency commitments. Loan terms reviewed for compatibility with reversibility (Funding Strategy §3.5).

### 4.4 Tier 4 — Pre-Purchase Credit Buyers

**Who:** Enterprise buyers committed to high-quality credits, willing to pay forward for verified outcomes.

**Examples** (with significant due diligence required for greenwashing risk per Funding Strategy §5):

- Frontier (Stripe-led carbon removal pre-purchase coalition)
- Microsoft, Google, Stripe, Salesforce climate purchasing programs
- Regenerative-aligned corporate commitments
- Public-sector procurement of restoration outcomes

**Target:** $10–30M during Phase 2 as credit issuance begins.

**Approach:** Strict adherence to MRV discipline. No credits issued for pre-purchase that haven't been verified. Pre-purchase contracts reflect the progressive issuance schedule (Funding Strategy §6).

**Terms:** Per the Stewardship Lease and MRV Specification. Pre-purchasers do not gain rights beyond credit ownership.

### 4.5 Tier 5 — Crowd and Community Capital

**Who:** Aligned individuals and communities supporting the mission directly.

**Forms:**

- Crowdfunding platforms (Kickstarter-style for mission projects).
- Community shares structures where legal.
- Indigenous and community co-investment for work in their own territories.

**Target:** $2–10M across Phases 1–2. Modest in scale, significant in stakeholder breadth.

**Approach:** Honest representation of the project, no over-promising. Reflects Charter §3.4 (open by default) — the project is supportable by aligned people regardless of size of contribution.

### 4.6 Categorically Refused

Per Funding Strategy §5:

- Fossil fuel industry funders (regardless of program branding)
- Industrial agriculture/forestry without regenerative discipline
- Mining and extractives without restoration discipline in core operations
- Authoritarian sovereign sources
- Funding requiring methodology compromise, exclusive rights, or concealment
- Funding from parties under active major environmental-harm investigation

## 5. Founding Capital Targets and Diversity

### 5.1 Phase 0 Target Profile

By end of Phase 0 (Month 6):

- $2–4M raised
- Minimum 3 independent funders (single-source dominance acceptable temporarily; tracked and time-bound)
- All from Tier 1 (anchor philanthropic) or founder commitments

### 5.2 Phase 1 Target Profile

By end of Phase 1 (Month 18):

- $25–40M cumulative raised
- Minimum 6 independent funders, at least 4 categories
- No single funder >25% of cumulative (transitional cap; tightens to 20% by end of Phase 2)
- At least 3 from Tier 1 (anchor)
- At least 1 from Tier 3 (sovereign/multilateral) if available

### 5.3 Phase 2 Target Profile

By end of Phase 2 (Month 36):

- $80–135M cumulative raised
- Minimum 10 independent funders across at least 4 categories
- Single-funder cap: 20% (Funding Strategy §3.1 in full effect)
- Tier 4 (pre-purchase credits) active for at least 12 months
- Tier 5 (crowd/community) active

### 5.4 Concentration Risk Tracking

The Funding Strategy mandates funder diversity. Operationally, this requires tracking:

- Cumulative capital by funder (in absolute terms and as percentage of total)
- Cumulative capital by category
- Pipeline visibility for next 12-24 months
- Concentration trend (improving toward diversification or worsening)

The Stewardship Office maintains this tracking and reports quarterly to Federation governance during the founding phases.

## 6. Approach Strategy

### 6.1 Pre-Approach Preparation

Before approaching any funder, Robotanica must have ready:

- The corpus (Charter, White Paper, key PRDs, Funding Strategy, Threat Model)
- A founding team (or articulated founder commitments) capable of executing
- A founding budget at the level of detail this Plan provides
- A timeline with milestones funders can verify
- An incorporated entity ready to receive funds
- References from advisors and early supporters

### 6.2 Tier 1 Approach Pattern

Anchor funders are not approached cold. The pattern:

1. **Warm introduction** through aligned advisors — board members, senior practitioners, prior grantees.
2. **Initial conversation** — articulate mission, structural defenses against capture, what makes this project worth the bet.
3. **Material exchange** — share corpus and budget, follow up on questions.
4. **Diligence cycle** — funder team reviews; multiple meetings; site visits as appropriate.
5. **Concept paper or LOI stage** — funder requests formal request; Robotanica produces.
6. **Final decision and grant agreement** — terms negotiated; transparency commitments confirmed.

Timeline per anchor funder: 6–18 months from first conversation to first dollar. Multiple anchors are pursued in parallel; closing of one does not gate others.

### 6.3 Tier 2 Approach Pattern

Patient capital is pursued through:

1. Direct outreach to known mission-aligned funds.
2. Networking through anchor philanthropic funders' investment arms.
3. Specific pitches to family offices with documented long-horizon mandates.

Timeline: similar to Tier 1, often longer. Patient capital is genuinely scarce; multiple parallel conversations are necessary.

### 6.4 Tier 3 Approach Pattern

Sovereign and multilateral grants typically require:

1. Engagement with national focal points (often a country office of the relevant institution).
2. Alignment with the institution's program priorities at the time.
3. Co-development of project proposals with national governmental counterparts.
4. Multilateral approval processes.

Timeline: 12–24 months from initial engagement to grant. Resource-intensive.

### 6.5 Tier 4 Approach Pattern

Credit pre-purchase relationships develop after Phase 1 demonstrates the MRV discipline:

1. Engagement with mission-aligned credit purchasing platforms (Frontier-style).
2. Direct relationships with enterprise climate teams.
3. Pilot pre-purchases at modest scale to demonstrate methodology.
4. Scale-up once methodology is verified through external audit.

### 6.6 Tier 5 Approach

Crowd and community capital is approached through:

1. Public communication (per Public Communication Doctrine §10).
2. Community engagement in regions where work is active.
3. Aligned advocacy organizations and movements.

Tier 5 follows Phase 1 demonstration; raising crowd capital before Phase 1 is generally premature.

## 7. Governance of Capital Decisions

### 7.1 Founding Funding Committee

During the founding phases (0–2), capital decisions are governed by a Funding Committee (per Funding Strategy §6.2):

- Stewardship Office representation
- Science Council representation (where relevant)
- At least one external independent member

Operational founding leadership advises but does not vote on Funding Committee decisions over a defined threshold.

### 7.2 What Requires Funding Committee Approval

- Single funder commitments above $5M (Phase 0/1) or $10M (Phase 2)
- All Tier 4 (commercial pre-purchase) commitments above $1M
- Any commitment from a funder whose primary activities include any potentially-incompatible category
- Any deviation from Funding Strategy §3.1 (single-funder caps)
- New funder relationships from previously-unrepresented categories

### 7.3 Refusal Discipline

Refusals follow the Funding Strategy §5 categorical limits. Refusals are recorded in the public record (per Funding Strategy §5.10):

- Funder identity disclosed unless funder requests confidentiality and Federation governance grants
- Reason for refusal stated
- Date and decision-making body recorded

### 7.4 Disclosure of Funding Relationships

Per Funding Strategy §3.2:

- All funding above $250K annually is publicly disclosed
- Funder's other major activities are disclosed in summary
- Use of funds is publicly disclosed in aggregate (not per-funder unless funder agrees)

Disclosures are part of the public record per Public Communication Doctrine §5.

## 8. Specific Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Anchor funder pulls out before commitment matures | Pursue multiple anchors in parallel; do not depend on any single anchor's closing for Phase 1 progression |
| Phase 0 takes longer than 6 months | Founder personal commitments bridge if necessary; accept slower start rather than degrade discipline |
| Single funder offers Phase 1 in full | Refused; Funding Strategy §3.1 single-source dominance limit |
| Funder requires terms incompatible with Charter | Refused per Funding Strategy §5; relationship may continue without funding |
| Greenwashing offer made attractive | Funding Committee review; refusal recorded publicly with reasoning |
| Sovereign funder's government changes during multi-year commitment | Diversification across sovereigns; political risk insurance considered |
| Patient capital harder to raise than projected | Heavier reliance on Tier 1; reduced ambition on growth rate during Phase 2 |
| Credit pre-purchase markets slow to develop | Phase 2 timeline extended; reduced reliance on credit revenue assumptions |
| First Lease formation takes longer than expected | Cash runway extended; less ambitious Phase 1 deliverables; Phase 2 delayed but principled |

## 9. Cash Management and Reserves

### 9.1 Cash Runway Discipline

At all times during founding phases:

- Minimum **18 months of operational runway** maintained
- Reserves separately tracked from operating cash
- Capital deployment paced to maintain runway

When runway falls below 18 months without committed incoming capital to extend, operations are paced down — hiring frozen, discretionary spend reduced, project ambition trimmed — before runway becomes critical.

### 9.2 Reserves

Three categories of reserves:

- **Operating reserves** — 6 months operating expense in liquid instruments at all times.
- **Reconciliation reserves** — funded as Phase 2 credit issuance begins, per MRV §10.3.
- **Contingency reserves** — for catastrophe, founder-departure, or other unplanned events. Built progressively.

### 9.3 Investment of Reserves

Reserves are held in:

- Treasury bills or comparable sovereign instruments (USD, EUR, or jurisdictional equivalent)
- Mission-aligned fixed-income (where viable instruments exist)
- Cash equivalents

Reserves are **not** held in:

- Equity markets (volatility incompatible with operational reliance)
- Cryptocurrency
- Instruments tied to fossil fuels or other categorically-refused industries
- Single-counterparty exposure beyond defined limits

### 9.4 Multi-Currency Discipline

Robotanica operates across jurisdictions. Currency exposure is managed through:

- Holding operating cash in the currency of operating expenses
- Hedging large planned currency conversions
- Avoiding speculative currency exposure

## 10. Bridge Financing and Founders' Capital

### 10.1 Founders' Initial Commitment

Founding parties commit personal capital, expertise, or both to enable Phase 0. This is generally:

- Founder sweat equity (work without market-rate compensation in early months)
- Initial out-of-pocket expenses
- Personal warranties to early counsel and advisors

This is not equity in the extractive sense. Per Personnel Philosophy §9, founders do not receive windfalls. Founder contributions are recognized appropriately within the bounded compensation framework, not through ownership lottery.

### 10.2 Bridge Financing

If anchor commitments arrive in tranches with timing gaps, bridge financing may be needed:

- Short-term grants from supportive Tier 1 funders willing to bridge
- Short-term loans from mission-aligned lenders (rare but possible)
- Founder deferred compensation

Bridge financing is documented, time-bounded, and disclosed.

### 10.3 No High-Pressure Capital

The project explicitly refuses bridge or any other capital that comes with:

- Personal guarantees from founders
- Equity dilution that compromises bounded-return commitments
- Conditions that compromise Charter principles even temporarily
- Terms creating leverage over the founders' decisions

## 11. Post-Phase-2 Outlook

### 11.1 Transition to Operating Revenue

By end of Phase 2, operating revenue should begin offsetting reliance on philanthropic capital:

- Federation membership fees from operators (modest)
- Service revenue (consulting, MRV support, training)
- First credit revenue (modest, as Phase 2 verifies and issues)
- First ecosystem service payments

These do not replace founding capital in Phase 3 but begin the transition.

### 11.2 Phase 3 and 4 Capital Strategy

Per the Funding Strategy:

- Maturing credit revenue (with reconciliation discipline)
- Sovereign biosphere bonds if developed
- Continuing philanthropic backbone
- Operating revenue at scale

This Plan does not specify Phase 3+ capital in detail; subsequent Capital Plans cover those phases.

### 11.3 The Trillion-Tree Horizon

The trillion-tree objective requires capital flows totaling at least the order of one trillion dollars over decades. This Plan covers approximately 0.01% of that scale — getting from zero to operational viability.

The discipline established during founding determines whether the orders of magnitude that follow can be raised honestly.

## 12. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in the founding capital strategy:

- **Founder commitments specifically.** This Plan generalizes founder commitment without naming founders. Once founders are identified, specific commitment levels and terms can be documented.
- **Anchor-funder identification.** The Plan lists illustrative anchor funders. Specific approaches require relationship development that has not yet occurred.
- **Patient capital availability at scale.** $30–60M in patient equity is the Plan's assumption; market conditions may not support it. Contingency: heavier philanthropic load.
- **Sovereign biosphere bond pilot timing.** This Plan does not depend on biosphere bonds, which is appropriate given their pre-pilot status. If a pilot can be developed during Phase 2, Phase 3 capital strategy shifts substantially.
- **Multi-country tax structure.** Receiving capital from multiple jurisdictions, deploying it across multiple jurisdictions, requires careful tax structuring. This is professional work outside this Plan's scope.
- **Founding entity legal form.** Whether Robotanica's founding entity is a foundation, a public-benefit corporation, a cooperative, or some hybrid affects what capital instruments are available. Decision precedes Phase 0.
- **Currency of accounting and reporting.** With operations across jurisdictions, primary accounting currency must be selected; criteria not yet documented.
- **Founder departure during Phase 0/1.** If a founder departs during the founding phases, capital and team continuity need protection. Specific protocols are not yet developed.
- **Anchor-funder due-diligence aggregation.** Multiple funders performing parallel due diligence is operationally heavy. Whether to pursue a coordinated diligence process (with funders sharing review) or individual processes is unresolved.
- **Endowment versus annual fundraising at scale.** As Robotanica matures, it may seek to build an endowment for long-horizon stability versus continuing annual fundraising. This decision is several years away.

## Appendix D — Glossary (Capital-Specific)

- **Anchor funder** — A major Tier 1 funder whose commitment unlocks subsequent rounds.
- **Bridge financing** — Short-term capital covering timing gaps between committed capital tranches.
- **Concentration risk** — Risk arising from disproportionate dependence on a single funder.
- **Diligence cycle** — The funder's evaluation process between initial conversation and commitment.
- **Funding Committee** — Governance body for capital decisions per Funding Strategy §6.2.
- **Patient capital** — Capital with explicit acceptance of long horizons and bounded returns.
- **Pre-purchase** — Buyer commitment to purchase credits in advance of issuance.
- **Reserves** — Capital held against operational, reconciliation, or contingency needs.
- **Tier (capital)** — One of five funder categories with distinct approach patterns.
