# Climate Scenario Assumptions

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Strategic and methodological specification
**Status:** Draft for ratification
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Bioregional Strategy, Genetic Strategy, MRV Specification, Theory of Change

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies the climate trajectory assumptions Robotanica uses for planning. It defines:

- The climate scenarios against which restoration is planned.
- The horizons used for different planning purposes.
- How divergence from assumptions is detected and how planning adapts.
- How climate uncertainty is incorporated into recipes, MRV, and bioregional strategy.
- What climate conditions are within Robotanica's planning bounds and what are outside them.

Multiple documents reference climate change, climate trajectories, and climate-driven uncertainty. The Bioregional Strategy plans against bioregional climate shifts. The Genetic Strategy permits climate-adjacent provenance under conditions. MRV permanence calculations depend on climate stability assumptions. The Theory of Change Links 4-5 depend on climate scenarios.

But until now no document has specified **what trajectory** Robotanica plans against. This document fills that gap.

The document is **honest about uncertainty**. Climate projections are themselves uncertain at multiple horizons. Robotanica's planning baseline is a working position, subject to annual review as climate science updates and as the trajectory itself unfolds.

---

## 2. The Problem

### 2.1 Why a Climate Baseline Matters

Without a stated climate trajectory, "climate-aware" planning is a slogan. With one, it is a discipline:

- Recipes can be designed against specific climate conditions and tested at specific horizons.
- Provenance decisions can reference specific climate analogs.
- Permanence calculations can incorporate specific risk profiles.
- Bioregional priorities can reflect specific climate-driven changes.
- Failure thresholds can be set in advance.

When climate diverges from baseline, the divergence triggers replanning. Without a baseline, divergence cannot be detected.

### 2.2 The Difficulty

Climate scenarios are not predictions. They are conditional projections: "if global emissions follow trajectory X, then climate at site Y at time Z is approximately P." The conditional structure means:

- Multiple trajectories are plausible.
- Outcomes depend on actions outside Robotanica's control.
- Specific local outcomes are uncertain even given global trajectories.
- Tipping points and non-linear changes are inadequately characterized in current models.

A baseline that pretends certainty is wrong. A baseline that admits all uncertainty is unusable. The discipline is to specify the working position with appropriate honesty about its limitations.

### 2.3 What This Document Does

- Specifies the working scenarios for planning.
- Specifies the horizons over which different planning operates.
- Specifies divergence detection and replanning triggers.
- Specifies what is in scope and what is out of scope for Robotanica's climate planning.

## 3. Principles

These principles bind climate planning and may not be set aside for analytical convenience.

### 3.1 Plan Against Multiple Scenarios

**CLIM-001** Robotanica's planning incorporates multiple climate scenarios, not a single trajectory. Recipes, provenance, and operations are robust across the scenario range, not optimized for a single point estimate.

### 3.2 Honest About Uncertainty

**CLIM-002** Climate projections are presented with their uncertainty. Point estimates without ranges are not used in planning documents.

### 3.3 Local Specificity Where Possible

**CLIM-010** Site-level planning uses regional and local climate projections where available, not global averages applied to local sites.

### 3.4 Conservative Where Stakes Are Long-Horizon

**CLIM-020** For long-horizon decisions (multi-decade Lease commitments, slow-establishing recipes), planning skews toward more pessimistic scenarios. The asymmetry is deliberate: optimistic planning produces maladapted outcomes if reality is worse than expected; pessimistic planning produces over-engineered outcomes if reality is better than expected.

### 3.5 Adapt as Trajectory Unfolds

**CLIM-030** Climate baseline is reviewed annually. Material divergence from baseline triggers replanning. Replanning is structural, not a sign of failure.

### 3.6 Acknowledge Bounded Planning

**CLIM-040** Some climate scenarios are beyond Robotanica's planning bounds (per §10). Acknowledging the bounds is honest; pretending the project can plan against any trajectory is dishonest.

### 3.7 Use IPCC and Peer-Reviewed Sources

**CLIM-050** Climate baseline is grounded in IPCC AR6 (and successors) and peer-reviewed climate science. Robotanica does not develop its own climate projections; it uses the field's best work.

### 3.8 Climate-Sovereignty Compatible

**CLIM-060** Climate planning honors indigenous and traditional knowledge of climate trajectories. Traditional ecological knowledge and observational records of climate change in indigenous and local contexts are first-class evidence alongside scientific projections.

## 4. Working Scenarios

For planning, Robotanica uses an ensemble of three scenarios spanning the plausible range.

### 4.1 Scenario A — "Successful Mitigation" (Lower-Bound Trajectory)

**Aligned with:** IPCC SSP1-2.6 (or equivalent low-emission pathway).

**Assumes:**

- Global net-zero emissions reached around mid-century.
- Limited overshoot of 1.5°C or no overshoot.
- Climate stabilizes after 2050 with moderate ongoing changes.

**Implications for Robotanica:**

- Recipes designed around current bioregional climate envelopes remain broadly viable.
- Tier A (strict local) provenance per Genetic Strategy remains broadly appropriate.
- Climate-adjacent provenance (Tier C) used selectively, not broadly.
- Catastrophic site loss from climate factors remains rare.

**Probability:** Difficult to quantify precisely; reflects an optimistic but not implausible trajectory if global mitigation succeeds.

### 4.2 Scenario B — "Middle Path" (Working Baseline)

**Aligned with:** IPCC SSP2-4.5 (or equivalent intermediate pathway).

**Assumes:**

- Global emissions plateau and slowly decline.
- ~2.5°C warming by 2100 (with regional variation).
- Significant climate impacts but not catastrophic worldwide.
- Tipping points possible but not pervasive.

**Implications for Robotanica:**

- Recipes need climate-aware design; some current bioregional climate envelopes shift materially.
- Tier C (climate-adjacent) provenance used more frequently.
- Tier D (assisted migration) considered in specific contexts.
- Reconciliation reserves sized for material climate-driven loss.
- Bioregional strategy adapts: some current bioregions face severe stress.

**Probability:** Treated as the working baseline given current emissions trajectories, while acknowledging the trajectory is not deterministic.

### 4.3 Scenario C — "High Emissions" (Stress Test)

**Aligned with:** IPCC SSP3-7.0 or SSP5-8.5 (or equivalent high-emission pathway).

**Assumes:**

- Continued high or rising emissions through mid-century.
- ~3.5°C+ warming by 2100.
- Severe climate impacts including pervasive tipping points.
- Significant ecosystem collapse in vulnerable regions.

**Implications for Robotanica:**

- Recipes need substantial climate adaptation.
- Tier C and D provenance become routine; Tier E (novel assemblage) considered in some contexts.
- Multiple bioregions become operationally infeasible or require fundamentally different approaches.
- Catastrophic site loss becomes expected, not exceptional.
- Permanence claims are weakened; reconciliation reserves are heavily drawn.

**Probability:** Lower than middle path under current trajectory but not implausible. Used as stress test against which planning robustness is evaluated.

### 4.4 Scenarios Used in Practice

**Default planning:** Scenario B (Middle Path) as primary working baseline.

**Stress testing:** Scenario C (High Emissions) as the test of robustness for recipes, reserves, and bioregional commitments.

**Optimistic case:** Scenario A (Successful Mitigation) as the upper-bound case for revenue projections and outcome trajectories.

**Site planning:** Specific sites use site-relevant downscaled scenarios from regional climate models, not global averages.

## 5. Planning Horizons

Different planning operates at different horizons, with appropriate climate trajectory data for each.

### 5.1 Operational Horizon (0-5 years)

**Climate input:** Recent observational data plus seasonal-to-decadal forecasts where available.

**Planning use:** Annual operational planning, seasonal scheduling, near-term recipe selection.

**Confidence:** Relatively high for trends; moderate for specific events.

### 5.2 Tactical Horizon (5-15 years)

**Climate input:** Decadal trajectories from regional climate models. Scenario divergence becoming material.

**Planning use:** Cohort establishment trajectories, recipe revision cycles, fleet capacity planning.

**Confidence:** Moderate. Trends are clearer than specific events; regional patterns are clearer than local.

### 5.3 Strategic Horizon (15-50 years)

**Climate input:** Multi-decade scenario projections. Substantial divergence between scenarios.

**Planning use:** Multi-decade Lease design, climate-aware recipe development, bioregional engagement strategy, permanence projections.

**Confidence:** Lower. Trajectories diverge significantly; specific localities have substantial uncertainty even given trajectory.

### 5.4 Long Horizon (50-100 years)

**Climate input:** Long-trajectory projections with substantial uncertainty. Tipping point considerations.

**Planning use:** Long-lived species recipes, very long Lease structures, ultimate ecosystem outcome projection.

**Confidence:** Low. The 100-year horizon is approximately the limit of meaningful climate planning; beyond that, scenarios diverge so substantially that detailed planning is unreliable.

### 5.5 Beyond Long Horizon (100+ years)

**Robotanica's posture:** Multi-generation work continues. Specific climate baselines beyond 100 years are not planning instruments; they are honesty about the long-horizon nature of the work.

The trillion-tree horizon may extend beyond 100 years for some cohorts. Planning at that horizon is qualitative ("we expect substantial change; recipes anticipate ecosystem reassembly") rather than quantitative.

## 6. Site-Level Climate Application

How global scenarios translate to site planning.

### 6.1 Downscaling

**SITE-001** Site-level planning uses downscaled regional climate projections where available, ideally with multiple model ensembles to reflect local uncertainty.

**SITE-002** Where downscaled projections are not available, regional projections from IPCC and regional climate centers are used.

**SITE-003** Local observational records (temperature, precipitation, extreme events) supplement projections to reflect local context.

### 6.2 Climate-Shifted Bioregion Mapping

**SITE-010** Each operational bioregion is mapped to its **climate-shifted analog** — the bioregion or set of bioregions whose current climate envelope most closely matches the projected future climate envelope of the site.

**SITE-011** Climate-shifted analogs are used for:

- Recipe adaptation (what species would work under projected conditions).
- Provenance decisions (Tier C climate-adjacent provenance per Genetic Strategy §4.3).
- Permanence projections (whether current ecosystem can persist).

**SITE-012** Where projected climate has no current analog (novel climate combinations), this is flagged. Recipes for novel climates are developed with extra caution and Council review.

### 6.3 Site-Specific Risk Assessment

**SITE-020** Each parcel's climate risk profile is assessed:

- Drought risk and trajectory.
- Fire frequency and severity trajectory.
- Flood risk.
- Pest and disease pressure trajectory.
- Heat stress projections.
- Hydrological trajectory.

Risk profiles inform recipe selection, monitoring intensity, reserve sizing for that parcel, and Lease term considerations.

### 6.4 Multi-Year Climate Variability

**SITE-030** Beyond trends, multi-year climate variability (ENSO, NAO, monsoon variability, etc.) is incorporated. Recipes accommodate variability not just trends.

## 7. Recipe-Climate Linkage

Recipes (per Gaia §5.2) incorporate climate assumptions explicitly.

### 7.1 Recipe Climate Specification

**REC-001** Each recipe specifies:

- The climate envelope under which it is designed to perform.
- The bioregional category and climate-shifted analog assumptions.
- The horizon over which performance is expected.
- The tipping points beyond which recipe may fail.

### 7.2 Climate-Adapted Recipes

**REC-010** Recipes designed for climate-adapted contexts include:

- Tier B/C provenance options.
- Species mix reflecting both current and projected ecosystem composition.
- Holobiont matching for projected conditions.
- Monitoring focused on early indicators of climate-driven divergence.

### 7.3 Recipe Revision Triggers

**REC-020** Climate divergence triggers recipe revision when:

- Local observed climate falls outside the recipe's design envelope persistently (multiple seasons).
- Trajectory projections shift materially due to updated climate science.
- Outcomes from existing applications show climate-related failure patterns.

**REC-021** Revision follows the Codex revision protocol with Council review.

### 7.4 Refused Recipes

**REC-030** Recipes whose performance assumes Scenario A (Successful Mitigation) and would fail under Scenario B are refused for sites where Scenario B is the working baseline. This is conservative planning per CLIM-020.

## 8. MRV-Climate Integration

MRV (per MRV Specification) incorporates climate assumptions.

### 8.1 Permanence Calculations

**MRV-CLIM-001** Permanence projections (MRV §10) incorporate climate scenarios:

- Default permanence calculations use Scenario B working baseline.
- Sensitivity analysis under Scenario C identifies vulnerable cohorts.
- Reconciliation reserves sized to handle Scenario B losses with Scenario C as stress test.

### 8.2 Counterfactual Baseline

**MRV-CLIM-010** Counterfactual baselines (per MRV §9) incorporate climate trends. The "without restoration" trajectory is not stable; it shifts under climate change. Restoration's contribution is evaluated against the appropriate moving baseline.

### 8.3 Outcome Attribution

**MRV-CLIM-020** Where outcomes are attributable to climate (favorable or unfavorable), this is acknowledged in attribution discipline (MRV §9). Restoration's actual contribution is the marginal effect over what climate alone would produce.

### 8.4 Failure Threshold Adjustments

**MRV-CLIM-030** Failure thresholds (MRV §6.5) are evaluated relative to climate scenario. A cohort that fails under Scenario C-like conditions but would have succeeded under Scenario A is a different kind of failure than recipe-design failure.

## 9. Bioregional-Climate Integration

The Bioregional Strategy adapts to climate trajectories.

### 9.1 Bioregion Selection

**BIO-CLIM-001** Bioregion selection (per Bioregional Strategy §4) incorporates climate vulnerability and trajectory:

- Bioregions facing severe Scenario B/C stress receive priority where restoration is feasible and meaningful.
- Bioregions becoming infeasible under Scenario C are reconsidered or approached differently.

### 9.2 Climate Refugia

**BIO-CLIM-010** Bioregions identified as climate refugia (where current ecosystems may persist despite climate change) are recognized:

- Refugia within bioregions are mapped where possible.
- Restoration in refugia uses Tier A provenance more confidently.
- Restoration outside refugia uses higher-tier provenance.

### 9.3 Bioregion-Specific Adaptation

**BIO-CLIM-020** Each engaged bioregion has its own climate-adapted strategy:

- Tropical forests: drought trajectory, dieback risk, pest pressure.
- Temperate forests: range shift, fire regime, drought.
- Boreal: temperature shift, permafrost, fire.
- Drylands: drought, vegetation transition.
- Mediterranean: fire, drought, ecosystem novel state.
- Wetlands: hydrology, sea-level rise where relevant.
- Mountains: range shift, refugia, hydrology.

### 9.4 Refused Regions Under Climate Stress

**BIO-CLIM-030** Bioregions where Scenario B/C conditions make restoration ecologically meaningless (where projected conditions support no viable ecosystem assemblage) are refused for engagement. These are rare but not impossible cases.

## 10. Bounded Planning

Robotanica's planning has bounds. Beyond these bounds, planning is replaced by acknowledgment.

### 10.1 What Is in Scope

**Within planning bounds:**

- Scenarios A, B, and C as outlined.
- Multi-decade horizons up to ~100 years.
- Local and regional climate variation.
- Tipping points considered as risks within ranges.

### 10.2 What Is Out of Scope

**Beyond planning bounds:**

- Catastrophic warming scenarios beyond Scenario C (sudden methane release, rapid Arctic transition, cascade tipping points).
- Geoengineering interventions transforming the climate baseline.
- Civilizational collapse scenarios where governance and infrastructure fail.
- Climate engineering succeeding to the point where ecosystem trajectories are deliberately reset.

### 10.3 Posture Beyond Bounds

**BOUND-001** Robotanica acknowledges that out-of-scope scenarios may occur. The project has no specific plans for them because:

- They are difficult to anticipate specifically.
- They would require fundamental restructuring beyond what current planning can provide.
- Acknowledging the limits is more honest than pretending plans exist.

**BOUND-002** If out-of-scope conditions develop, the project responds through governance, not pre-existing protocols.

### 10.4 Sensitivity to Bounds

**BOUND-010** Planning is reviewed for sensitivity to the bounds:

- If reserves and reconciliation discipline assume Scenario B, a Scenario C reality stresses them.
- If a substantial fraction of operations occur in regions becoming infeasible, the project's structural commitments come under stress.

The discipline of stress testing is to identify these sensitivities, not to eliminate them.

## 11. Divergence Detection and Replanning

How does Robotanica know when reality diverges from baseline?

### 11.1 Annual Climate Review

**REPLAN-001** Each year, the Stewardship Office and Science Council review:

- Updated IPCC and peer-reviewed climate projections.
- Observed climate at engaged sites versus baseline.
- Pattern of climate-driven outcomes (failures, adaptations, novel events).
- Tipping point and acceleration evidence.

### 11.2 Replanning Triggers

**REPLAN-010** Material divergence triggers replanning when:

- Observed local climate falls persistently outside the working baseline.
- Updated science shifts the central estimate of trajectories materially.
- Cumulative climate-driven loss exceeds reserve sizing.
- Sentinel events (severe disasters, ecosystem shifts) signal trajectory acceleration.

### 11.3 Replanning Process

**REPLAN-020** Replanning is governance:

- Identify which baseline assumptions have been invalidated.
- Identify which decisions and commitments are affected.
- Develop revised baseline.
- Update affected documents (Bioregional Strategy, Genetic Strategy, MRV permanence calculations, recipes).
- Communicate publicly per Public Communication Doctrine §6.5.

### 11.4 Cumulative Replanning

**REPLAN-030** Replanning happens incrementally as evidence accumulates, not solely at scheduled review cycles. Substantial cumulative replanning may be reflected in the baseline's next major revision.

## 12. Climate-Driven Catastrophe

The Threat Model (§4.6) and other documents reference climate-driven catastrophe. This Document specifies its handling.

### 12.1 What Counts as Climate Catastrophe

- Catastrophic site loss from extreme drought, fire, flood, or cyclone.
- Major shift in regional climate beyond projections.
- Cascade failures across multiple sites.
- Tipping point event (e.g., regional ecosystem collapse).

### 12.2 Response Discipline

Climate catastrophe responses follow:

- Immediate: emergency response per operational protocols (Pilot Operation Protocol §10.4).
- Short-term: site assessment, reconciliation per MRV §14, communication per Public Communication Doctrine §8.
- Long-term: recipe and bioregional strategy review, baseline implications.

### 12.3 Reserve Adequacy Under Catastrophe

**CAT-001** Reconciliation reserves are sized to handle expected climate catastrophe under Scenario B baseline.

**CAT-002** Catastrophe under Scenario C exceeds expected reserves; reserves are insufficient by design (sizing reserves for Scenario C would require capital that compromises operational viability).

**CAT-003** Where Scenario C-level catastrophe occurs, reserves are drawn fully and broader response is required: federation governance review, additional capital raise, possibly partial commitments revised.

## 13. Indigenous and Traditional Climate Knowledge

Climate planning incorporates indigenous and traditional ecological knowledge per Charter §3.3 and §2.7 (plurality of knowledge).

### 13.1 Recognition

**ITK-001** Indigenous and traditional knowledge of climate change in specific landscapes is first-class evidence alongside scientific projections.

### 13.2 Integration

**ITK-010** Indigenous observations of climate change in their territories — observed shifts in seasonality, species ranges, hydrology, weather extremes — inform site-level planning where communities consent to share this knowledge.

**ITK-011** Where indigenous knowledge points to climate trajectories or impacts not yet captured in scientific models, the divergence is investigated, not dismissed.

### 13.3 Sovereignty

**ITK-020** Indigenous communities determine what climate-related knowledge they share and on what terms. Climate planning respects this sovereignty per the Plurality of Knowledge Operational Protocol.

## 14. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in climate planning:

- **Scenario probability assignment.** Whether and how to assign probabilities to the working scenarios is contested. The Document presents scenarios as a range; some planning purposes might benefit from explicit probability weighting.
- **Tipping point characterization.** Tipping points are inadequately characterized in climate models. Planning that relies on tipping points being included accurately may be optimistic.
- **Geoengineering as a wildcard.** Solar radiation management, ocean fertilization, or other geoengineering interventions are not in the working baseline. Whether and when they should be incorporated is unresolved.
- **Site-level downscaling adequacy.** Climate downscaling at site scale has known limitations. Whether projections are adequate for site planning at the level of detail Robotanica needs is an open question per region.
- **Replanning cadence vs. continuity.** Annual replanning provides responsiveness; constant replanning undermines stability. Finding the right cadence is a balance.
- **Climate-driven jurisdictional change.** Climate change may drive political and jurisdictional changes (climate migration, conflict, regulatory shifts) affecting Robotanica's operations. These are partially in the Threat Model but not in this Document.
- **Communication of climate uncertainty to non-technical audiences.** Funders, partners, and communities may struggle with conditional projections and scenario ranges. Translating climate uncertainty into decision-relevant communication is an ongoing challenge.
- **Climate scenarios for marine and ocean restoration.** If Robotanica extends to marine restoration, the climate scenario framework needs marine-specific elements (sea temperature, acidification, sea level).
- **Climate adaptation vs mitigation balance.** Robotanica's primary contribution is mitigation (carbon sequestration, biosphere function). Climate adaptation contributions (resilience, refugia, watershed protection) are secondary but real. The relative weighting in planning is unspecified.

## 15. Review Cadence

This Document is reviewed:

- **Annually** for incorporation of new climate science, observational divergence, and operational learning.
- **Major review every 5 years** when IPCC produces major assessment reports.
- **Triggered review** when material divergence per §11.2 occurs.

The Stewardship Office and Science Council jointly own the review process.

## Appendix D — Glossary (Climate-Specific)

- **Climate-shifted analog** — A region whose current climate matches the projected future climate of another region.
- **Downscaling** — Methodology for translating global or regional climate projections to site-level.
- **Ensemble** — A collection of climate scenarios or model outputs reflecting uncertainty.
- **IPCC AR6** — IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (2021-2023) and successors.
- **NDC** — Nationally Determined Contribution under Paris Agreement.
- **Refugia** — Sites where current ecosystems may persist despite global climate change.
- **Replanning trigger** — A condition that initiates climate baseline revision.
- **Scenario** — A conditional climate trajectory used for planning.
- **SSP** — Shared Socioeconomic Pathways used in IPCC projections.
- **Tipping point** — A threshold beyond which a climate or ecosystem state shifts non-linearly.

---

*Climate is the substrate within which restoration occurs. It is not stable. Pretending otherwise leads to maladapted recipes, inadequate reserves, and broken commitments. This Document specifies what climate Robotanica plans against, with appropriate honesty about what is in scope and what is not. The discipline is to plan against the right scenarios, monitor divergence, and replan honestly when reality requires it. The work is restoration; the climate trajectory is the world the work happens in.*
