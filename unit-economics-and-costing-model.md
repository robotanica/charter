# Unit Economics and Costing Model

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Financial and operational model
**Status:** Draft for review and refinement
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Funding Strategy, Founding Capital Plan, MRV Specification, Bioregional Strategy

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies how Robotanica thinks about the cost of its work, at the unit level (per hectare, per cohort, per parcel) and at scale.

The corpus until now has been silent on actual numbers. The Funding Strategy specified principles. The Founding Capital Plan specified amounts at the project level. Neither addressed the underlying question: what does it cost to plant and steward a hectare to maturity, and how does that scale.

Without this answer, "is this affordable" is unanswerable. The trillion-tree objective requires capital at scale; whether that scale is $100 billion or $10 trillion or somewhere between depends on cost structure. Capital strategy and funder expectations both depend on knowing the answer.

This Model is **honest about uncertainty**. Restoration costs vary by orders of magnitude across bioregions, recipes, partnerships, and operational maturity. Single numbers without uncertainty bands are misleading. Where the Model gives ranges, the ranges are wide because the underlying ecology and operations are wide.

The Model is **a v0 starting point**, not a finished budget. As pilot operations generate real cost data, the Model is refined. As the bioregional portfolio diversifies, regional cost models emerge. Annual review is structural.

## 2. The Cost Question, Stated Honestly

### 2.1 Why Restoration Cost Varies So Widely

A hectare restored in central Brazil through community-led FMNR may cost $200. A hectare restored in temperate Europe through nursery-grown native diversity plantings may cost $40,000. The difference is not just labor cost; it reflects:

- **Density of intervention.** FMNR allows existing roots to regrow; Miyawaki-method plantings require thousands of seedlings per hectare.
- **Recipe complexity.** Single-species establishment is cheap; multi-species succession with holobiont matching is expensive.
- **Site condition.** Healthy soils planted into recover; severely degraded soils need site preparation.
- **Land cost and lease terms.** Lease payments to Landowners vary 100x across regions.
- **Labor cost.** Hourly wages vary even more.
- **Climate and seasonality.** Long growing seasons enable more intervention per year; short seasons compress operations.
- **Distance and logistics.** Remote sites cost multiples of accessible sites.
- **Monitoring intensity.** Decadal monitoring varies by recipe and verification level.
- **Operational maturity.** Year 1 costs more per hectare than Year 10 because of capability building.

The Model accepts this variability as primary. Rather than producing a single number, it produces a structured way of thinking about cost across categories.

### 2.2 What This Model Is and Is Not

The Model **is**:

- A framework for estimating cost across bioregional contexts.
- A baseline for sensitivity analysis (what changes if assumptions shift).
- A tool for sequencing — which engagements deliver outcomes per dollar.
- A reference against which actual operations are compared.
- A starting point for funder conversations.

The Model **is not**:

- A budget for any specific operation (those require detailed costing).
- A precise predictor of outcomes (those depend on ecology and execution).
- A static document (it evolves with operational data).

## 3. Cost Categories

Restoration cost decomposes into seven categories. Each contributes differently across bioregions, recipes, and phases.

### 3.1 Land

**What it includes:** Lease payments to Landowners; community benefit-sharing; sovereign or government access fees where applicable.

**What it does not include:** Purchase of land (Robotanica does not buy land per Charter §2.3).

**Variance:** $0 to $1,000+ per hectare per year depending on land productivity, alternative uses, and Landowner expectations.

**Notes:** The Stewardship Lease specifies compensation cannot be free-land arrangements (Lease Requirements §9.2). Even communal or low-cost land contexts include material compensation.

### 3.2 Site Preparation

**What it includes:** Soil rehabilitation, invasive removal, fire-risk reduction, hydrological restoration where needed, fencing in livestock-pressured contexts.

**What it does not include:** Routine clearing in already-degraded sites where the recipe assumes natural regeneration.

**Variance:** $50 to $5,000+ per hectare. Severely degraded sites at the high end; healthy sites at the low end.

### 3.3 Biological Production

**What it includes:** Seedlings (purchase or production), mycorrhizal inoculants, biochar amendments, companion species, soil microbiome additions where recipe specifies.

**Variance:** $100 to $10,000+ per hectare. Driven primarily by planting density and recipe complexity.

**Operational maturity factor:** OpenFactory's biological production scales with substantial cost reduction. Early operations buy seedlings (or partner with nurseries); mature operations produce internally with significant unit-cost reduction.

### 3.4 Field Operations

**What it includes:** Labor for planting, tending, monitoring; transportation; equipment depreciation; energy; consumables; safety equipment.

**Variance:** $50 to $3,000+ per hectare for first-year operations. Subsequent years lower (tending less labor-intensive than planting). Total over 30 years: $200 to $20,000.

**Robotic operations factor:** Where field robotics is applicable, operational costs reduce over time as fleets amortize and operating efficiency improves. Robotics is not universally applicable; some terrain, ecosystems, and recipes remain primarily human-led.

### 3.5 Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV)

**What it includes:** Sensor deployment and maintenance; satellite imagery costs; field monitoring labor; verification by independent verifiers; audit costs.

**Variance:** $20 to $500+ per hectare per year over the monitoring horizon (50+ years). Driven by required MRV intensity, verifier rates, and remote-sensing infrastructure costs.

**Notes:** MRV cost is real and ongoing for the full Lease horizon, not just the active intervention period. The "long quiet years" of monitoring (Personnel Philosophy §3.6) cost real money even when no drama is occurring.

### 3.6 Indirect and Platform Costs

**What it includes:** Allocation of platform development and operation (Gaia, A3, OpenFactory), governance bodies (Stewardship Office, Council), Federation infrastructure, legal and administrative costs.

**Variance:** Allocated across the active hectare base. At small scale, indirect costs per hectare are large; at planetary scale, they amortize substantially.

**Year 1 indirect allocation per hectare under stewardship:** Could be $10,000+ when the platform serves only a few hundred hectares. **Year 10 indirect allocation:** Could be $20–50 per hectare when the platform serves millions of hectares.

This is the **dominant scale economy** in Robotanica's cost structure.

### 3.7 Reserves and Contingency

**What it includes:** Reconciliation reserve allocation per credit issued (per MRV §10.3); operational contingency reserves; insurance premiums where viable.

**Variance:** 5–15% of total cost as a reserve allocation, depending on risk profile.

**Notes:** Reserves are part of cost from the perspective of capital required, even though they may not be deployed. They are not optional.

## 4. Cost Profiles by Bioregional Category

The following are indicative cost profiles per hectare, summed across the seven categories, presented as ranges that reflect honest uncertainty. Numbers are nominal in 2026 USD; cost trajectories over time per §6.

### 4.1 Tropical Forest Systems

**Per-hectare total cost over 30 years (Year 1 establishment + 29 years of stewardship and monitoring):**

| Component | Low | High |
|---|---|---|
| Land (lease + community share) | $300 | $5,000 |
| Site preparation | $100 | $3,000 |
| Biological production | $500 | $5,000 |
| Field operations (cumulative) | $300 | $4,000 |
| MRV (cumulative) | $400 | $3,000 |
| Reserves | $100 | $1,500 |
| **Per-hectare cost (excluding indirect)** | **$1,700** | **$21,500** |

Tropical forest systems benefit from year-round growing seasons and well-developed FMNR practices. The low end reflects FMNR-dominated approaches with strong community partnership; the high end reflects industrial Miyawaki-style plantings with intensive intervention.

Temperate forests have higher land costs, higher labor costs, and longer maturity timelines. Some temperate plantings approach $100,000 per hectare in high-cost-of-living regions with intensive recipes.

Drylands and grasslands benefit from FMNR and Holistic Management approaches that are inherently lower-cost per hectare than densely-planted forest restoration. The headline tree-count metric may be lower per hectare; the ecological and carbon outcomes can be substantial through soil restoration.

### 4.6 Wetland and Riparian

**Per-hectare cost over 30 years:**

Highly variable. Hydrological restoration can be $5,000–$50,000 per hectare alone in damaged systems. Mangrove restoration costs less per hectare than peatland restoration. Estimates withheld pending bioregional pilot data.

**Per-hectare cost over 50–75 years:**

Costs vary substantially by altitude band and access. Lower mountain costs may approach temperate forest costs; high-altitude alpine restoration approaches boreal cost profiles.

Specialized contexts; cost profiles developed per specific engagement.

## 5. Indirect Cost Allocation

Indirect costs (platform, governance, Federation infrastructure) are allocated across the active hectare base. The allocation curve is the single most consequential factor in Robotanica's cost trajectory.

### 5.1 Allocation Model

Indirect costs are roughly:

- **Year 1:** ~$10–20M annual platform and governance cost (Founding Capital Plan).
- **Year 5:** ~$30–60M annual.
- **Year 10:** ~$80–150M annual.
- **Year 20+:** Stabilizing as a function of federation membership and operational maturity.

Indirect costs do not scale linearly with hectares. They scale with platform complexity, federation size, governance scope, and supporting infrastructure.

### 5.2 Per-Hectare Indirect Cost Curve (Indicative)

| Stewarded hectares | Indirect cost per hectare per year |
|---|---|
| 10² (pilot) | $100,000+ |
| 10⁴ | $5,000–$10,000 |
| 10⁶ | $50–$100 |
| 10⁸ | $1–$5 |
| 10⁹ | <$1 |

This is the dominant scale economy. Pilot operations are indirect-heavy; planetary-scale operations are direct-cost-dominated.

### 5.3 Implications

The cost-per-hectare numbers in §4 exclude indirect cost. **Including indirect cost, pilot-stage hectares cost an order of magnitude more than steady-state hectares.** This is structurally important:

- Funders evaluating Robotanica's pilot phase must accept that pilot-phase per-hectare cost is unrepresentative.
- Carbon and biodiversity credit pricing in pilot phase reflects the real cost; subsequent pricing reflects the lower steady-state cost.
- The trillion-tree economic case depends on reaching scale where indirect costs amortize.

## 6. Cost Trajectories Over Time

### 6.1 Cost Inflation

Operational costs inflate over the project horizon. Real-world inflation of 2–4% per year over 50 years multiplies nominal costs 2.5–7x. Cost models are presented in 2026 dollars; real-world cost projections require inflation adjustment.

### 6.2 Capability-Driven Cost Reduction

Several categories see real cost reduction over time:

- **Biological production** as OpenFactory scales (40–60% reduction at maturity).
- **Field operations** as fleet robotics matures (20–40% reduction in applicable contexts).
- **MRV** as remote sensing and AI verification matures (10–30% reduction).
- **Indirect costs** through scale (per §5.2).

### 6.3 Climate-Driven Cost Increase

Some categories may see real cost increase:

- **Reserves** as catastrophe risk increases under climate change.
- **Site preparation** as more sites become severely degraded.
- **MRV** as monitoring methodology evolves to handle complexity.
- **Land** as restoration becomes more recognized and Landowners' alternative options expand.

The net trajectory is uncertain. The Model assumes net cost stability or modest reduction in real terms over 30 years; this assumption is reviewed annually.

### 6.4 Recipe Evolution

As recipes evolve in the Codex, cost profiles per recipe evolve. Simpler successful recipes drive cost down; more sophisticated recipes (holobiont matching, climate-adapted material) may drive cost up. The Model assumes recipe portfolio costs roughly stable over time.

## 7. Sensitivity Analysis

The cost ranges are wide because the underlying drivers are uncertain. Sensitivity analysis identifies which assumptions matter most.

### 7.1 High-Sensitivity Drivers

**Land cost.** A 10x change in land cost is plausible across regions; effects directly on total. Mitigation: bioregional diversification.

**Operational maturity.** Year-1 vs Year-10 operations differ by 2–4x in some categories. Mitigation: extended pilot phase before scale commitments.

**Recipe complexity.** Simple FMNR vs intensive Miyawaki differ by 10x+. Mitigation: portfolio approach across recipe types.

**Indirect cost amortization.** Pilot-stage indirect cost allocation is 1000x+ steady-state. Mitigation: realistic pilot-phase financing.

**Verifier costs.** Independent verification has limited capacity at scale; verifier costs may rise faster than other categories. Mitigation: verifier capacity-building investment.

### 7.2 Low-Sensitivity Drivers

**Specific seedling unit cost** (within reasonable bounds). Variations of 20–30% in seedling cost rarely shift total cost much.

**Specific sensor unit cost.** Sensors are a modest fraction of total; their unit cost is not dominant.

**Specific equipment depreciation timing.** Useful but not dominant.

### 7.3 Sensitivity Conclusion

The largest cost uncertainties are structural (recipe choice, bioregional context, operational maturity, indirect amortization), not operational. Cost optimization should focus on the structural levers, not on driving down unit costs of specific consumables.

## 8. Comparative Context

### 8.1 Per-Hectare Cost in Other Restoration Programs

For context (rough order-of-magnitude estimates from public sources, not precise comparisons):

- **Loess Plateau restoration (China):** ~$2,000 per hectare averaged across the 35,000 km² project.
- **Atlantic Forest restoration (Brazil):** ranges from $1,000 (community-led FMNR) to $20,000+ (industrial Miyawaki).
- **U.S. National Forest replanting:** varies from $500 (basic) to $5,000+ (intensive).
- **European reforestation programs:** typically $5,000–$30,000+ per hectare.
- **Mangrove restoration programs:** typically $3,000–$15,000 per hectare with significant variance.
- **Existing carbon-credit-funded projects:** range from claimed $500 per hectare (often inadequately verified) to $20,000 (rigorous verification).

Robotanica's cost profile sits **within these established ranges**, neither dramatically cheaper nor dramatically more expensive. The differentiation is **discipline** (verification rigor, sovereignty respect, multi-decade monitoring, bounded reconciliation), not cost.

### 8.2 Implication for Credit Pricing

Carbon credit prices in voluntary markets are highly variable: $5/tCO₂e for low-quality credits to $400+/tCO₂e for premium nature-based removal. Robotanica's discipline targets the upper-quality tier, where prices are higher and demand is growing.

Per-hectare carbon sequestration over 30 years varies widely by bioregion (10–500+ tCO₂e). Multiplied against credit prices, restoration revenue per hectare can range from negligible to substantial. Carbon revenue alone is often **insufficient to cover full restoration cost**, especially in tropical forest systems where land is competitive.

This is why the Funding Strategy emphasizes diversified revenue (credits, ecosystem services, sovereign payments, philanthropic backbone) rather than treating credits as the primary funding mechanism.

## 9. Total Project Economics at Scale

### 9.1 Headline Estimate

Reaching one trillion adult trees represents very roughly **300–600 million hectares** under stewardship (depending on planting density and survival assumptions, and acknowledging substantial overlap with FMNR-assisted natural regeneration that is much sparser per hectare).

Order-of-magnitude cost estimates:

| Cost component | Per hectare (mid-range) | At 500M hectares |
|---|---|---|
| Direct cost over 30+ years | $5,000 | $2.5T |
| Indirect cost amortized | $50 | $25B annually |
| Reserves and contingency | $1,000 | $500B |
| **Total order-of-magnitude** | | **$3T+ over project horizon** |

This number is rough. The actual figure depends on bioregional mix (drylands much cheaper per hectare than forests), recipe choice, partnership structure, and operational maturity. The **order of magnitude** — trillion-dollar capital flow over multiple decades — is robust.

### 9.2 Capital Inflow Required

Sustained capital inflow at scale:

- **Year 1–4 (founding):** $120–210M (per Founding Capital Plan).
- **Year 5–10 (Phase 2 / early Phase 3):** $1–5B annually.
- **Year 10–20 (full Phase 3):** $10–50B annually.
- **Year 20+ (mature operations):** $50–200B annually until trillion-tree milestone, with declining capital intensity per hectare as scale economies emerge.

These numbers are large. They are also the order of magnitude of carbon market revenue under modest assumptions about high-quality credit pricing, plus diversified ecosystem service payments, plus sustained philanthropic and sovereign capital. They are achievable; they require capital markets that recognize biosphere outcomes as economically valuable.

### 9.3 What This Means for Credit Markets

If carbon and biodiversity credits priced honestly were available at scale, restoration could be substantially self-financing in mature phases. The current state of credit markets — largely compromised by inadequate MRV — prevents this. Robotanica's posture is to **build the credit market it needs by demonstrating discipline**, not to wait for the market to mature.

## 10. The Affordability Question

### 10.1 Is the Trillion-Tree Objective Affordable?

**At total cost of approximately $3T over 30–50 years, the answer is yes**, in the following senses:

- It is comparable to a single nation's annual military budget.
- It is small compared to the carbon-removal economics of preventing climate damages.
- It is small compared to the value of biosphere services.
- It is large compared to current restoration funding and requires sustained capital market development.

The **affordability question is not "is the number too big"** — it is **"can capital be raised at this scale honestly without compromising mission"**. The Funding Strategy and Founding Capital Plan address this; the answer depends on:

- Credit markets maturing into honest discipline.
- Sovereign finance instruments developing for biosphere restoration.
- Sustained philanthropic and patient capital across decades.
- Operating revenue and ecosystem service payments scaling.

### 10.2 What Would Make It Unaffordable

The trillion-tree objective becomes unaffordable if:

- Indirect costs do not amortize as expected (operational scale economies fail).
- Credit markets remain compromised at scale (revenue does not flow as expected).
- Catastrophic site loss exceeds reserves (climate change outpaces restoration).
- Verification capacity does not scale (verifier costs balloon).
- Mission discipline fails and trust collapses (Robotanica's credit premium evaporates).

### 10.3 What Robotanica Is Betting On

Robotanica is betting that:

- Discipline produces a credit premium that justifies higher per-hectare costs.
- Bioregional diversification spreads risk and finds cost-effective approaches per region.
- Operational maturity drives indirect costs down to manageable levels at scale.
- Capital markets evolve to recognize biosphere value over multi-decade horizons.

These are bets, not certainties. The Model is honest about that.

## 11. Cost Discipline

### 11.1 Cost Reduction Without Mission Compromise

Robotanica seeks lower cost where it can be achieved without compromising:

- MRV rigor.
- Indigenous and community sovereignty.
- Genetic diversity discipline.
- Long-horizon monitoring.
- Worker safety and dignity.
- Reconciliation reserves.

Cost reduction in these areas is **categorically refused**. Cost reduction in operational efficiency, biological production, fleet utilization, indirect cost amortization, and similar is **actively pursued**.

### 11.2 Cost Transparency

Per-hectare cost data, aggregated and anonymized, is published in the public record (Public Communication Doctrine §5). This serves three purposes:

- **Funder confidence.** Funders see real numbers, not marketing.
- **Sector benchmark.** Other restoration projects can compare.
- **Discipline accountability.** If Robotanica's costs diverge from claims, the divergence is visible.

### 11.3 Avoiding Cost-Per-Unit Optimization Trap

Optimizing for low cost per tree planted is an anti-pattern. It incentivizes:

- Cheap recipes that may fail.
- Inadequate verification.
- Weakened community relationships.
- Reduced reserves.

Robotanica's optimization target is **cost per adult tree at maturity**, not cost per seedling planted. This metric is hard to measure in early years (you must wait decades to confirm) but it is the right metric.

## 12. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions in the cost model:

- **Inflation assumptions.** Different bioregions experience different inflation rates; the Model's assumption of 2–4% global inflation is rough.
- **Climate-driven cost trajectories.** Whether climate change net-increases or net-decreases restoration costs is contested. Both directions are plausible.
- **Operational learning rate.** How fast does cost decrease with operational experience? Different bioregions and recipes show different learning rates.
- **Insurance market development.** Whether catastrophe insurance markets develop for restoration determines reserve sizing. Currently underdeveloped.
- **Verifier capacity at scale.** Whether independent verifier capacity grows with demand or constrains it is a real uncertainty.
- **Land cost trajectories.** As restoration becomes more recognized and Landowners' options expand, land cost may rise faster than other categories.
- **Indirect cost step-changes.** Some indirect costs may step rather than scale smoothly (a new region requires substantial infrastructure investment before any operations occur).
- **Carbon and biodiversity market pricing.** Whether high-quality credit pricing rises (as buyers prioritize quality) or falls (as supply increases) substantially affects revenue.
- **The cost of doing it slowly.** Restoration delayed faces different ecological and economic conditions. The cost of postponement is real but hard to quantify.

## 13. Methodology Notes

### 13.1 Sources

The Model draws on:

- Published per-hectare costs from major restoration programs (Loess Plateau, Atlantic Forest, U.S. National Forest, European reforestation, mangrove programs).
- Carbon market price data from voluntary and compliance markets.
- Cost data from commercial nurseries and forestry operations.
- Academic literature on restoration costs and trajectories.
- Practitioner interviews where available.

These sources are imperfect — they vary in methodology, scope, and currency. The Model's wide ranges reflect honest uncertainty about the underlying data.

### 13.2 Updating Cadence

The Model is updated:

- **Annually** with operational data from active engagements.
- **Quarterly** during the founding phases as initial data emerges.
- **On material change** to underlying assumptions (recipe, climate trajectory, market conditions).

### 13.3 Public Versions

Each version is dated and preserved per the Data Lifecycle Plan. Comparing versions over time reveals how Robotanica's understanding of its own cost evolves.
