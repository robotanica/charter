# Genetic Strategy

## Position Paper (v0)

**Document type:** Position paper on contested ecology
**Status:** Draft for ratification
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Gaia PRD, Bioregional Strategy, MRV Specification, AI Ethical Review Framework

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

This document specifies Robotanica's position on the genetic dimensions of restoration: which seed materials are used, where they come from, how genetic diversity is maintained, and how restoration responds to climate change.

The white paper flagged this as an open question. The Bioregional Strategy referenced it. The Charter constrains it (no patenting per §5.3, indigenous sovereignty per §3.3, reversibility per §2.8). The AI Ethical Review Framework forbids autonomous genetic release (§6.7). This paper resolves what those constraints permit, recommend, and refuse — while acknowledging that the underlying ecology is contested.

This is a **position paper**, not a specification or framework. The format is deliberate. Genetic strategy in the Anthropocene is contested ecology; positions must be taken to act, but positions are not truths. Robotanica articulates where it stands and why, accepts that the standing may evolve, and engages the contestation honestly.

A note on scope. This paper covers terrestrial plants — the focus of the trillion-tree objective. Where Robotanica's work extends to other biota (mycorrhizae, soil microbiome, pollinators, companion fauna), parallel disciplines apply but are not exhaustively specified here.

## 2. The Contested Terrain

### 2.1 The Native-Provenance Tradition

Restoration ecology, as a discipline, has long defaulted to native species and local genetic provenance. The reasoning is sound:

- Local provenance preserves locally-adapted genetic material that has co-evolved with local climate, soils, and biota.
- Native species support intact food webs, holobiont relationships, and ecosystem function in ways non-native species often cannot.
- Genetic diversity within native populations is itself biodiversity worth preserving.
- Non-native introductions have a long, well-documented history of unintended consequences.

The default has served the field well, particularly in contexts of stable climate and intact reference ecosystems.

### 2.2 The Climate Argument

Climate change strains the default. By the time a tree planted today reaches maturity, the climate envelope at its planting site may have shifted toward conditions for which that genotype is not adapted. Local provenance, which optimizes for the climate of the past, may produce maladapted populations for the climate of the future.

The argument has consequences:

- Some restoration practitioners now advocate **predictive provenance**: sourcing from climate-analogous regions whose current climate matches the projected future climate of the planting site.
- Others advocate **assisted migration**: moving species, not just genotypes, to where their climate envelope is shifting.
- Others maintain that local provenance, with attention to within-population genetic diversity, retains sufficient adaptive capacity to handle climate shift.

### 2.3 The Sovereignty Dimension

Indigenous and local communities have stewarded local genetic material for generations. Some communities consider their seeds and their associated knowledge as inherently theirs, not commodities to be transplanted. Indigenous seed sovereignty is itself a structural commitment under the Charter (§3.3) and the CARE Principles.

Genetic strategy that ignores sovereignty — moving seeds across communities without consent, claiming "global" genetic material as a Federation resource, importing assisted-migration material without local consent — violates the Charter regardless of ecological merits.

### 2.4 The Biotech Frontier

Beyond conventional seed selection, modern biotechnology offers tools: gene editing (CRISPR), synthetic biology, transgenic species. These tools are operational in agriculture; they have not yet been used widely in restoration but the capability exists.

These technologies raise distinct concerns: irreversibility (gene flow into wild populations), uncertainty (interactions in complex ecosystems are unpredictable), governance (who decides which edits go into the genome of restoration material?), and sovereignty (who owns gene-edited seed?).

### 2.5 The Genuine Contestation

The contestation is real. Senior ecologists hold opposing positions in good faith. Indigenous traditions vary. Climate trajectories themselves are uncertain. There is no clean answer.

Robotanica's position takes this seriously. It does not claim certainty where contestation persists. It articulates a defensible default and the conditions under which the default is varied.

## 3. Principles

These principles bind genetic strategy and may not be set aside for operational convenience.

### 3.1 Local Provenance as Default

**GEN-001** Local genetic provenance is the default for restoration plantings. Variation from local provenance requires specific justification recorded in Gaia and reviewed under the protocols of §6.

### 3.2 Genetic Diversity Preservation

**GEN-010** Within-species genetic diversity is preserved structurally. Plantings draw from multiple parent populations; large-scale plantings of single-genotype clonal material are refused.

**GEN-011** Loss of within-population genetic diversity through restoration practice is itself ecological harm and is to be avoided.

### 3.3 Climate Awareness

**GEN-020** Restoration plantings consider projected climate trajectories over the project horizon. Local provenance is not a commitment to plant materials maladapted to projected future conditions.

**GEN-021** Climate-aware provenance does not require abandoning local provenance; many sites can be restored with local material that has sufficient genetic diversity to track climate shift.

### 3.4 Reversibility (Charter §2.8 Applied)

**GEN-030** Genetic interventions that may be irreversible — particularly those involving gene flow into wild populations — are subject to the strongest precautionary discipline.

**GEN-031** The reversibility principle extends to species introductions: introducing a species into a region where it does not exist creates risks of becoming established and spreading beyond intended scope. This requires high evidentiary thresholds.

### 3.5 Indigenous and Traditional Seed Sovereignty

**GEN-040** Indigenous and local communities have sovereignty over genetic material associated with their territories and traditions. Use of this material requires consent under FPIC and CARE Principles.

**GEN-041** Robotanica does not collect, propagate, or distribute genetic material from indigenous territories without the relevant community's consent on the specific use.

### 3.6 No Patenting

**GEN-050** Genetic material developed or selected through Robotanica's work is not patented. This applies to: cultivars, selected ecotypes, mycorrhizal inoculant strains, biochar microbial communities, and any other biological materials.

**GEN-051** Genetic material is shared openly under the Charter §5.3. Where defensive measures against patenting by other parties are needed, they take the form of public-domain dedication or open patent commons, not Robotanica-held patents.

### 3.7 Refusal of Genetic Modification and Gene Editing

**GEN-060** Robotanica does not use genetically modified or gene-edited seed material in restoration plantings. This includes transgenic species, CRISPR-edited cultivars, and synthetic biology constructs.

**GEN-061** This refusal applies even where such material is legally permitted in a jurisdiction and even where short-term benefits are claimed. The reversibility principle and the contested ecology of GMO release in restoration contexts justify refusal.

### 3.8 Holobiont Discipline

**GEN-070** Plantings include the holobiont, not just the plant: appropriate mycorrhizal inoculants, soil biota where relevant, companion species supporting ecological function.

**GEN-071** The microbiome and fungal communities used in restoration are subject to similar provenance discipline as plant material.

### 3.9 Documentation

**GEN-080** Every planting's genetic origin is documented in Gaia: parent population, collection method, any selection applied, propagation history, and at-planting genetic diversity metrics where measurable.

### 3.10 Continuous Review

**GEN-090** This Position is reviewed every five years comprehensively, or sooner if material new evidence warrants. Positions are not eternal truths; they are best-current judgments held open to revision.

## 4. Provenance Tiers

For practical operations, provenance is organized into tiers reflecting the degree of departure from strict local provenance.

### 4.1 Tier A — Strict Local

Material sourced from within the planting site or its immediate ecological neighborhood (typically defined by ecoregional or microclimatic boundaries).

This is the default tier. Most plantings should be Tier A unless specific justification supports another tier.

### 4.2 Tier B — Regional

Material sourced from within the same broader bioregional context as the planting site, not necessarily the immediate locality. Used where Tier A is infeasible (insufficient local material, severely degraded sites without seed stock) or where genetic diversity benefits from broader sourcing.

Tier B is a routine variation from Tier A; it does not require special review unless the regional sourcing crosses major ecological boundaries.

### 4.3 Tier C — Climate-Adjacent

Material sourced from regions whose current climate matches the projected future climate of the planting site (climate analogs). Used where projected climate shift suggests local provenance will be maladapted at maturity, and where evidence supports the climate analog.

Tier C requires Science Council review. It also requires:

- A documented projection of the planting site's climate at relevant horizons.
- Identification of the source region as an evidence-supported climate analog.
- Mixing with Tier A or B material to preserve genetic diversity.
- Monitoring at multiple horizons to assess outcome.

### 4.4 Tier D — Assisted Migration

Material sourced from beyond the natural range or natural climate envelope of the planting region, where the species' range is shifting and its survival in its historical range is no longer projected to be viable.

Tier D requires:

- Science Council approval.
- Evidence-based justification of the migration distance and the species' projected viability.
- Consideration of alternative recipes that do not require migration.
- Evaluation against potential invasion or naturalization concerns at the destination.
- Indigenous and community consent at both source and destination.
- Monitoring across decades to evaluate outcomes.

Tier D is rare and reserved for cases where local recipes are projected to fail without migration intervention.

### 4.5 Tier E — Novel Assemblage

Material from species not present at the planting site historically and not justifiable through climate-shift assisted migration, but proposed for ecological function or reconstruction purposes (e.g., functional analog species replacing extirpated ones).

Tier E is highly exceptional. It requires:

- Science Council approval at the highest review threshold.
- Federation governance approval.
- Indigenous and community consent.
- Detailed reversibility analysis.
- Long-horizon monitoring with explicit revision triggers.

Most restoration work should never reach Tier E. Where it is justified, it is justified case-by-case in the public record.

### 4.6 Tier Mixing

Plantings may mix tiers within a cohort. A cohort might be 70% Tier A, 25% Tier B, 5% Tier C — preserving local adaptation while introducing climate-resilience material. Mixing is documented in the planting record and reviewed under the rules of the highest tier present in the mix.

## 5. Genetic Diversity Discipline

### 5.1 Within-Species Diversity

**DIV-001** Plantings of any species draw from at least the minimum number of parent individuals or populations specified for that species in Gaia. Default minimum: 30 unrelated parent individuals for outcrossing species, with species-specific adjustment.

**DIV-002** Single-genotype clonal plantings at scale are refused. Where clonal material has restoration value (e.g., specific cultivars for soil stabilization), it is mixed with seed-grown material at substantial proportion.

### 5.2 Population Genetic Sampling

**DIV-010** Where seed is collected from wild populations, sampling protocols ensure representative within-population diversity. Avoid:

- Taking all seed from few individuals.
- Sampling only from convenient or accessible sub-populations.
- Repeated collection from the same maternal lines across years without rotation.

### 5.3 Cohort-Level Diversity Reporting

**DIV-020** Each Cohort's genetic diversity profile is reported to Gaia: number of parent populations, sampling protocol, estimated effective population size, any selection applied.

### 5.4 Multi-Species Plantings

**DIV-030** Recipes typically include multiple species, mirroring natural ecosystem assemblages. Single-species plantings are reserved for restoration contexts where single-species establishment is the appropriate first stage of succession.

### 5.5 No Genetic Bottlenecks

**DIV-040** Production scaling does not introduce genetic bottlenecks: when biological production scales (per OpenFactory), parent material is rotated and refreshed to prevent the production system itself from becoming a genetic homogenization mechanism.

## 6. Climate-Aware Provenance

### 6.1 When to Vary Default

**CLIM-001** Variation from Tier A toward Tiers B-D may be considered when:

- Projected climate at the planting site at maturity diverges materially from current climate.
- Local seed source is inadequate or absent (severely degraded sites).
- Documented evidence supports the source region as an analog.
- Indigenous and community partners support the variation.

**CLIM-002** Variation should not be considered solely on the basis of operational convenience or seed availability.

### 6.2 Climate Projection Discipline

**CLIM-010** Climate-aware provenance decisions reference:

- Multi-model ensemble climate projections at the planting site.
- Projection horizons matching the species' maturity timeline.
- Uncertainty ranges, not point projections.
- Local-scale variation where regional projections obscure relevant detail.

### 6.3 Hedge Against Projection Uncertainty

**CLIM-020** Where climate projections are uncertain, Tier mixing (per §4.6) hedges. Plantings include local material (best for current climate) and climate-adjacent material (better for projected climate) so that some portion of the cohort matches whichever climate trajectory unfolds.

### 6.4 Refugia Strategy

**CLIM-030** In some bioregions, climate refugia (sites where current ecosystems may persist despite global warming) are identified and prioritized for Tier A planting with strong local adaptation. Non-refugia sites in the same bioregion may use higher-tier provenance.

### 6.5 Regional Variation in Provenance Strategy

**CLIM-040** Provenance strategy varies by bioregion. Some bioregions face severe projected climate shift warranting more aggressive climate-adjacent provenance; others retain enough climate stability that Tier A remains broadly appropriate. Bioregional Strategy guides specific application.

## 7. Indigenous and Traditional Seed Sovereignty

### 7.1 Sovereignty Is Structural

**SOV-001** Indigenous and local communities have sovereignty over the genetic material associated with their territories, traditions, and stewardship. This sovereignty is structural in this Position, not procedural.

### 7.2 Consent Required

**SOV-010** Use of genetic material from indigenous or community territories requires FPIC for the specific use. Consent for one use does not transfer to other uses.

**SOV-011** Genetic material collected before community sovereignty was recognized may not be repurposed without retroactive consent where communities can be reasonably consulted.

### 7.3 Knowledge Attribution

**SOV-020** Where traditional ecological knowledge informs the selection or use of genetic material, attribution to the contributing community is structural and contributes to the Codex per Gaia §6.4.

### 7.4 Reciprocity in Genetic Exchange

**SOV-030** Where material is sourced from one community for use in another community's territory, both communities are involved in the consent process. Reciprocity arrangements (knowledge sharing, benefit sharing, capacity sharing) are negotiated under their governance.

### 7.5 Prohibition on Genetic Patrimony Removal

**SOV-040** Robotanica does not contribute to the removal of genetic material from indigenous territories for commercial benefit elsewhere. Material sourced from indigenous communities is used in restoration consistent with community consent, not commodified.

### 7.6 Seed Banks and Ex Situ Conservation

**SOV-050** Where ex situ conservation of indigenous-territory genetic material occurs (seed banks, gene banks), the conservation arrangements are made with the community's consent. The community retains rights to access and direction over the material.

## 8. Refused Approaches

### 8.1 GMO and Gene-Edited Material

**REF-001** Genetically modified organisms (transgenic plants) and gene-edited cultivars (CRISPR or comparable) are not used in Robotanica's restoration plantings.

**REF-002** This refusal applies regardless of:

- Legal permission in the jurisdiction.
- Claimed climate-adaptation or pest-resistance benefits.
- Source (academic, commercial, public).
- Whether the modification is "minor" or "natural-equivalent."

The refusal is grounded in:

- The reversibility principle (Charter §2.8): gene flow into wild populations is not reversible.
- The contested ecology: long-term consequences of GMO/gene-edited material in wild ecosystems are not adequately understood.
- The sovereignty dimension: indigenous and local communities have widely opposed GMO release in their territories.
- The patent dimension: most GMO and gene-edited material is patent-encumbered, conflicting with Charter §5.3.

### 8.2 Synthetic Biology Constructs

**REF-010** Synthetic biology constructs (organisms with substantially redesigned genetic material) are not used in Robotanica's restoration work.

### 8.3 Patents on Biological Material

**REF-020** Robotanica does not patent biological material. This applies to:

- Cultivars selected through traditional breeding.
- Microbial inoculants developed in Robotanica's facilities.
- Mycorrhizal strains identified or propagated.
- Any other biological material.

**REF-021** Where biological material developed through Robotanica's work would be subject to patent claims by others (e.g., a partner organization), Robotanica's contribution is structured to prevent patent enclosure: open dedication, defensive publication, or exclusion from the partnership.

### 8.4 Closed-Source Genetic Material

**REF-030** Robotanica does not use genetic material that requires payment of ongoing royalties, prohibits seed-saving by Landowners, or imposes other commercial restrictions inconsistent with Charter principles.

### 8.5 Endangered Species in Production

**REF-040** Endangered or threatened species are not used in restoration plantings except under conservation protocols approved by relevant species recovery authorities. Species recovery is distinct from general restoration; both have their place but they are not interchangeable.

## 9. Holobiont and Ecosystem Discipline

### 9.1 Plants Are Not Sticks

**HOLO-001** A planted tree without its mycorrhizae, pollinators, soil biota, and successional companions is, ecologically, a stick. Recipes specify holobiont composition, not just species lists.

### 9.2 Mycorrhizal Inoculation

**HOLO-010** Plantings include appropriate mycorrhizal inoculation. Mycorrhizal strains used:

- Are appropriate for the species and ecoregion.
- Are not patented and are not produced by patented processes.
- Are produced under conditions that do not introduce pathogens or invasive species.

### 9.3 Soil Biota

**HOLO-020** Where soil biota restoration is part of the recipe (for severely degraded sites), inoculation uses material from comparable healthy sites within the bioregion. Cross-bioregion soil transfer is reviewed for invasion risk.

### 9.4 Companion Species

**HOLO-030** Recipes specify companion species — pioneers, nitrogen-fixers, nurse trees, ground cover — that support successional restoration. Companion species are subject to the same provenance discipline as the primary species.

### 9.5 Pollinator Considerations

**HOLO-040** Plantings consider pollinator support, particularly for species depending on specific pollinators. Where pollinator populations are degraded, complementary pollinator habitat or active pollinator restoration may be part of the recipe.

## 10. Genetic Rescue

### 10.1 Definition

**RESC-001** Genetic rescue means the introduction of genetic material from one population into another, both of the same species, to address inbreeding depression, low genetic diversity, or related concerns in the receiving population.

### 10.2 When Permitted

**RESC-010** Genetic rescue is permitted under the following conditions:

- The receiving population shows evidence of genetic compromise affecting fitness.
- The donor population is genetically appropriate (same species, ecologically compatible source).
- Indigenous and community consent at both donor and recipient locations.
- Science Council review.
- Monitoring of genetic and ecological outcomes.

### 10.3 Distinction From Assisted Migration

**RESC-020** Genetic rescue moves material within a species' existing range to support existing populations. Assisted migration moves material beyond a species' existing range to enable colonization. The two are distinct interventions with distinct review thresholds.

### 10.4 Documentation

**RESC-030** Genetic rescue events are documented in Gaia with full provenance and outcome tracking.

## 11. Production-Side Genetic Discipline

### 11.1 Nursery Operations

**PROD-001** Nurseries operating under Robotanica's protocols (per OpenFactory §6.2) maintain genealogy from seed through deployed plant. Genealogy includes:

- Parent population identification.
- Collection date and method.
- Germination protocol.
- Selection events (if any) with documentation.
- Inoculation events.

### 11.2 Selection Discipline

**PROD-010** Selection within nurseries is conservative. Robotanica does not select for traits that diverge from natural population characteristics (faster growth, larger size, modified architecture) except where specifically authorized for restoration purposes.

**PROD-011** Where selection is applied, it is documented and reviewable. Selection that progressively narrows the genetic base of production lines is refused.

### 11.3 Refresh of Parent Material

**PROD-020** Production lines refresh parent material on regular cycles to prevent within-line drift. Refresh sources are documented and reviewed for diversity.

### 11.4 Quarantine and Biosecurity

**PROD-030** Nurseries operate under biosecurity protocols (per OpenFactory §6.2 OF-B-007) preventing pathogen and pest movement through production. Biosecurity discipline applies to the genetic strategy because pathogen movement compromises the value of the material being produced.

### 11.5 Seed Banks

**PROD-040** Robotanica may operate or partner with seed banks for ex situ genetic conservation. Seed bank operations honor provenance, sovereignty, and accessibility per this Position.

## 12. Implementation and Review

### 12.1 Operational Implementation

**IMPL-001** Operationally, this Position is implemented through:

- Recipes in Gaia specifying provenance tiers, parent population minimums, and holobiont composition.
- ProductionRun records in OpenFactory documenting genealogy.
- Cohort records in Gaia recording at-planting genetic state.
- Lease terms incorporating genetic strategy commitments where Landowner relationships warrant.

### 12.2 Review Authority

**IMPL-010** Authority over genetic strategy decisions is distributed:

- **Recipe-level provenance decisions** — Codex revision protocol with Science Council review for material variations.
- **Tier C-E decisions** — Science Council review specifically.
- **Tier D-E decisions** — Science Council plus Federation governance plus indigenous and community consent.
- **Refused approaches** — Categorical, no permission process; refusal stands.
- **Patent-related decisions** — Federation governance.

### 12.3 Documentation in Public Record

**IMPL-020** Significant genetic strategy decisions (Tier C and above; refused requests; rescue events) are documented in the public record per Charter and Protocol commitments.

### 12.4 Annual Genetic Strategy Report

**IMPL-030** The Science Council publishes an annual report on genetic strategy implementation: provenance distribution across plantings, climate-aware provenance trends, refused approaches encountered, indigenous and community engagement, evolving evidence on contested questions.

### 12.5 Five-Year Comprehensive Review

**IMPL-040** This Position is reviewed comprehensively every five years. Review considers:

- Outcomes from past plantings under the various provenance tiers.
- Evolving evidence on assisted migration outcomes.
- Climate trajectory updates.
- New biotechnology developments and the case for revisiting refused approaches.
- Indigenous and community feedback.
- External critique and academic engagement.

## 14. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions where evidence may shift the Position over time:

- **Threshold for assisted migration adoption.** When does climate trajectory justify moving from Tier A-B default to broader Tier C-D adoption? The threshold is currently judgment-based; clearer criteria may emerge from ecological research over the coming decades.
- **Climate-shifted bioregion analogs.** The concept of "climate analog" assumes that current climate envelopes accurately predict future climate envelopes. Climate change is producing some novel climate combinations not present anywhere today. How to source provenance for novel climates is unresolved.
- **Mycorrhizal and microbiome provenance.** Plant provenance discipline is more developed than mycorrhizal and microbiome provenance. Whether mycorrhizal communities should mirror plant provenance, or follow different rules, is an open ecological question.
- **Cisgenic and intragenic editing.** Some gene-editing approaches modify only genes already present in the species. Whether these approaches warrant the same refusal as transgenic and CRISPR-edited material is contested. The current Position refuses them; this may be revisited if evidence accumulates.
- **Endangered species in restoration.** The boundary between species recovery (which may justify intensive intervention) and general restoration is sometimes unclear. How to handle restoration adjacent to species recovery needs more development.
- **Climate-driven refugia identification.** Identifying refugia where current ecosystems may persist requires sophisticated climate modeling. The methodology is developing; Position implementation will need to evolve as it matures.
- **Indigenous seed sovereignty in conflict zones.** Where indigenous communities are displaced or in conflict, the practical operation of seed sovereignty is more complex. Specific protocols are needed.
- **Long-term monitoring of higher-tier plantings.** Tier C-E plantings require monitoring across decades. Whether the monitoring discipline survives organizational and personnel transitions across that horizon is open.

---

## 15. Conclusion

Genetic strategy in the Anthropocene is contested ecology. Climate change strains the traditions that served restoration well in stable conditions. Indigenous sovereignty constrains commodification of genetic material. Biotechnology offers tools whose long-term consequences are not adequately understood. There is no clean answer.

Robotanica's position takes positions where positions are needed to act:

- Local provenance is the default.
- Climate-aware variation is permitted under conditions, with Tier C-E review.
- Genetic diversity is preserved structurally.
- Indigenous and community sovereignty is honored structurally.
- GMO and gene-edited material are refused.
- Patenting is refused.
- Holobiont matching is part of restoration, not optional.

Each of these positions is held open to revision as evidence accumulates, as climate trajectories unfold, as indigenous and community partners inform the work, and as ecological understanding advances. The Position is current best judgment, not eternal truth.

## Appendix D — Glossary (Genetic-Strategy-Specific)

- **Assisted migration** — Movement of species or genotypes beyond their historical range to enable persistence under climate shift.
- **Climate analog** — A region whose current climate matches the projected future climate of another region.
- **Climate-adjacent provenance** — Sourcing material from regions whose current climate matches projected future climate of the planting site.
- **Cohort** — A group of plants of common provenance and planting event (shared with Gaia).
- **Cultivar** — A cultivated variety of a species, typically selected for specific traits.
- **Ecotype** — A locally adapted population within a species.
- **Effective population size** — A measure of genetic diversity within a population.
- **Ex situ conservation** — Conservation of genetic material away from its natural habitat (seed banks, gene banks).
- **Genetic rescue** — Introduction of genetic material into a population to address inbreeding or low diversity.
- **Holobiont** — A host organism considered together with its microbiota and symbionts.
- **Local provenance** — Genetic material from the planting site or its immediate ecological neighborhood.
- **Predictive provenance** — Sourcing material based on projections of future climate at the planting site.
- **Refugia** — Sites where current ecosystems may persist despite global climate change.

*Genetic strategy in the Anthropocene is the operational form of an unresolved ecological question. Robotanica takes positions where positions are needed, defends them with evidence and reasoning, holds them open to revision, and refuses the temptation to claim certainty where contestation is real. The work is restoration, not seed selection in isolation; the genetic discipline serves the restoration, not the reverse.*
