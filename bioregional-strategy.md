# Bioregional Strategy

## Specification (v0)

**Document type:** Strategic specification
**Status:** Draft for review
**Companion documents:** Robotanica Charter, Gaia PRD, A3 PRD, Funding Strategy, MRV Specification

---

## 1. Purpose and Status

Restoration is local. A trillion adult trees will not appear because the average global condition improves; they will appear because specific work happens on specific land in specific bioregions, conducted with knowledge appropriate to those places.

This document specifies how Robotanica decides where to work. It defines:

- The **bioregional ontology** — how regions are categorized for the purposes of strategy.
- The **selection criteria** — what determines whether and when Robotanica engages a region.
- The **regional strategy framework** — how strategy is built for a given region.
- The **phasing** — sequence and pace of regional engagement.
- The **regional governance model** — how decisions are made at regional scale within global protocol.
- The **cross-regional coordination** — how the federation works coherently across regions while respecting regional autonomy.

The Charter (§4.5) commits Robotanica to nested governance: local, regional, global. The Funding Strategy (§4) specifies how capital flows; this Strategy specifies where it flows to. The MRV Specification (§5.1) requires methods adapted to bioregional context; this Strategy specifies the regions those adaptations serve.

This document is strategy, not commitment. It defines the framework. Specific regional engagements are decisions made by Robotanica and the Federation, following this framework, recorded in the public record.

## 2. Why Bioregional Thinking

### 2.1 Restoration Is Place-Specific

A recipe that works in tropical Brazil does not work in temperate California. Soil microbiomes, climate envelopes, native species assemblages, fire regimes, hydrological patterns, and human histories differ profoundly across bioregions. Universal recipes do not exist; universal principles are operationalized through bioregional practice.

The Charter's commitment to ecological evidence (§2.6) requires bioregional rigor: methods that work in one bioregion are not assumed to work in another, and methods that fail in one bioregion are not necessarily failures of the methodology — they may be failures of bioregional fit.

### 2.2 The Bioregion Is the Natural Unit of Coordination

Below the bioregion, individual parcels and sites are the operational unit. Above the bioregion, the global federation is the protocol unit. The bioregion itself is where ecological knowledge is most coherent, where partnerships with indigenous and local stewards are most natural, where governance can be both contextually appropriate and operationally meaningful.

### 2.3 Climate Trajectories Differ by Bioregion

Climate change is not uniform. Some bioregions will warm more than others; some will dry; some will see hydrological reorganization. Restoration strategies must reflect bioregional climate trajectories, not global averages. Recipes that assume current bioregional conditions may need to anticipate shifts toward conditions of nearby bioregions over the project horizon.

### 2.4 Political and Regulatory Geography Is Bioregional

Bioregions cross national borders, but national regulation, property law, and indigenous governance frameworks are at jurisdictional scales below the bioregion. Strategy at the bioregional scale must integrate ecological coherence with political and legal heterogeneity.

## 3. Bioregional Ontology

### 3.1 Foundational Bioregional Categories

For Robotanica's strategic purposes, the planet is divided into bioregional categories. These are simplifications of the underlying ecological reality (the WWF ecoregions, Biodiversity Hotspots, IPBES regions, and Earth biomes are all more granular). They are sufficient for strategic decision-making; finer granularity is operationalized in Gaia's interpretation layer.

The categories are:

- **Tropical forest systems** — Amazon Basin, Congo Basin, Insular and mainland Southeast Asia, Atlantic Forest, Mesoamerica, smaller tropical systems.
- **Temperate forest systems** — Pacific temperate rainforests, eastern North American hardwoods, European broadleaf and mixed, East Asian temperate forests.
- **Boreal and high-latitude systems** — Canadian boreal, Russian taiga, Fennoscandian, Alaska/Yukon.
- **Drylands, grasslands, and savanna systems** — Sahel, East African savanna, Mongolian steppe, North American prairies, Patagonian and Australian drylands.
- **Mediterranean and chaparral systems** — Mediterranean Basin, California Floristic Province, Cape Floristic, Chilean matorral, southwestern Australia.
- **Wetland and riparian systems** — major river deltas, mangrove coasts, peatlands, large wetland complexes.
- **Mountain systems** — Andes, Himalaya/Hindu Kush, Alps and Caucasus, Rocky Mountains, eastern African highlands.
- **Tundra and high-altitude alpine** — Arctic tundra, alpine zones above tree line in mountain systems.
- **Island systems** — Pacific islands, Caribbean, Indian Ocean islands, others; treated separately due to endemism, isolation, and vulnerability.

### 3.2 Subregions

Each foundational category divides into subregions — finer ecological and political units that are the actual operational scale. Strategy is built at the subregion scale; the categories provide conceptual coherence above.

### 3.3 Codex Linkage

Bioregional definitions are first-class entities in Gaia's interpretation layer. Recipes are typed by bioregion (Gaia PRD §5.2). The codex is the authoritative source; this document describes the strategic framework that draws on it.

### 3.4 Climate-Shifted Bioregions

For strategic planning, each bioregion is paired with its **climate-shifted analog** — the bioregion or set of bioregions whose current climate envelopes most closely match where the source bioregion's climate is projected to shift over the project horizon. Climate-shifted analogs inform recipe design: what species and assemblages may need to be incorporated as the regional climate evolves.

## 4. Selection Criteria

Where Robotanica engages, in what sequence, and at what intensity, is decided by the following criteria. The criteria are weighted but not formula-driven; selection is ultimately governance judgment.

### 4.1 Ecological Leverage

**Selection-101** Carbon storage potential per hectare under realistic restoration trajectories. Tropical and boreal ecosystems lead, with regional variation.

**Selection-102** Biodiversity recovery potential, with priority for biodiversity hotspots and irreplaceable assemblages.

**Selection-103** Hydrological function — restoration's effect on watershed integrity, drought resilience, and downstream water security.

**Selection-104** Soil restoration potential — particularly in regions where soil degradation has been severe and recovery is feasible.

**Selection-105** Connectivity contribution — restoration that reconnects fragmented habitats, restores corridors, or enables species movement under climate shift.

### 4.2 Climate Vulnerability

**Selection-201** Regions where current ecosystems face severe climate-driven decline within the project horizon, where restoration may be the difference between persistence and collapse.

**Selection-202** Regions where assisted-migration and climate-adapted recipes are most needed, balanced against the genetic strategy commitments of the forthcoming Genetic Strategy document.

### 4.3 Land Availability and Tenure Clarity

**Selection-301** Availability of land suitable for restoration that is not already in good ecological condition (avoid greenwashing through restoration of land that needed no intervention).

**Selection-302** Clarity of land tenure — formal title, customary rights, indigenous claims — sufficient to enter durable Stewardship Leases without unresolved disputes.

**Selection-303** Willingness of Landowners and communities to enter the federation under the protocols and Lease structures.

### 4.4 Indigenous and Community Readiness

**Selection-401** Presence of indigenous or local communities with active stewardship and willingness to engage.

**Selection-402** Established indigenous governance bodies through which FPIC processes can proceed credibly.

**Selection-403** Existing relationships between potential federation partners and indigenous and local communities.

### 4.5 Political and Regulatory Feasibility

**Selection-501** Jurisdictions with legal frameworks compatible with the Stewardship Lease (or where compatible variants can be drafted).

**Selection-502** Stability of governance over the project horizon, balanced against the recognition that no jurisdiction is stable on a fifty-year horizon.

**Selection-503** Regulatory environment — permits, environmental review, land-use rules — that permits operations without prohibitive friction.

**Selection-504** Refusal to operate where local governance imposes conditions incompatible with the Charter (e.g., requirements for monoculture commercial species, prohibitions on indigenous data sovereignty).

### 4.6 Federation Partner Presence

**Selection-601** Existing federation members operating in the region, providing operational capacity.

**Selection-602** Mission-aligned partner organizations whose work in the region complements Robotanica's potential contribution.

**Selection-603** Scientific institutions and indigenous bodies in the region that can contribute to Codex and verification.

### 4.7 Operational Feasibility

**Selection-701** Logistical access — transportation, communications, basic infrastructure sufficient for fleet operations.

**Selection-702** Climate and seasonality compatible with operational rhythms (some regions have very narrow operational windows).

**Selection-703** Worker safety conditions per Charter §3.5 commitments to personnel.

**Selection-704** Costs that are sustainable for the funding sources available.

### 4.8 Mission Symbolism and Demonstration Value

**Selection-801** Regions whose successful restoration demonstrates the project's discipline at scale; this is a real but secondary criterion.

**Selection-802** Mission Symbolism shall not override ecological leverage or community readiness; flagship sites are valuable but not decisive.

### 4.9 Refused Region Categories

**Selection-901** Regions under active armed conflict where personnel safety cannot be assured.

**Selection-902** Regions where local governance categorically refuses Charter-compatible operations.

**Selection-903** Regions where the only available operating mode would compromise indigenous or community sovereignty.

### 4.10 The Honest Tradeoff

The criteria can favor different regions. A region may be ecologically critical but politically infeasible; another may be operationally easy but ecologically marginal. Robotanica's selection process makes these tradeoffs visible:

- A region selected primarily for ease (low cost, easy permits, available capital) is acknowledged as such; it may be the right choice but the rationale is recorded.
- A region selected primarily for ecological priority despite difficulty is acknowledged as such; the additional cost and risk are documented.
- The portfolio of regional engagements is reviewed periodically for balance: too many easy regions risks impact dilution; too many difficult regions risks operational stretch.

## 5. Regional Strategy Framework

For each region engaged, a regional strategy is developed before significant operations begin. The strategy includes:

### 5.1 Regional Profile

The profile documents:

- The bioregional category and subregions.
- Ecological state — current condition, historical state, reference trajectories.
- Climate trajectory — projected changes over the project horizon.
- Political and legal geography — jurisdictions, governance structures, indigenous and customary systems.
- Existing actors — federation members, partner organizations, scientific institutions, indigenous and community bodies, government agencies.
- Existing restoration efforts — both successes and failures, with lessons.

### 5.2 Regional Mission Translation

How Robotanica's global mission applies in this region. Specifically:

- Which bioregional ecosystems are restoration targets.
- Which recipes (existing or to-be-developed) are appropriate.
- What proportion of activity is restoration vs. protection vs. enhancement.
- What outcomes (carbon, biodiversity, hydrology, livelihoods) are foregrounded for this region.

### 5.3 Partnerships

Specific partnerships planned or active:

- Indigenous and community partnerships, with the consent processes underway.
- Federation member partnerships, with their roles defined.
- Scientific institutional partnerships.
- Government and multilateral partnerships.

### 5.4 Regional Governance

The governance structure for the region:

- Regional coordination body composition.
- Authority delegated from global, retained by global, or held entirely regionally.
- Relationships with national, sub-national, and traditional governance structures.

### 5.5 Operational Scope

Initial operational scope:

- Pilot parcels and recipes.
- Production and fleet capacity required.
- Monitoring and verification capacity required.
- Estimated capital and personnel.

### 5.6 Risk Assessment

Region-specific risks:

- Ecological risks (climate trajectory uncertainty, pest or disease, fire regime).
- Political risks (election cycles, conflict potential, regulatory shift).
- Community risks (consent durability, intra-community dynamics).
- Operational risks (logistics, weather, biosecurity).

### 5.7 Exit and Succession Planning

What withdrawal looks like if it becomes necessary:

- How active Leases continue (succession to other operators, return to landowner).
- How data is preserved and transferred.
- How communities are not left worse off than before engagement.

The exit plan is part of entry, not contingency afterthought.

## 6. Phasing Across Regions

### 6.1 Sequence Logic

Robotanica engages regions in sequence, not simultaneously. Sequence considers:

- **Capability building** — early regions build capabilities (recipes, verification, fleet operations) that subsequent regions inherit.
- **Risk management** — early regions are selected partly for manageability; later regions tackle more difficult contexts as the federation matures.
- **Capital efficiency** — early regions enable revenue and credit generation that funds later regional engagement.
- **Strategic balance** — early regions are not all in one biome or one continent; the portfolio diversifies from the beginning.

### 6.2 First-Wave Regions

The first wave of regional engagements is selected to:

- Demonstrate at least three different bioregional categories.
- Include at least one region with significant indigenous and community partnership.
- Include at least one region in the Global South and one in the Global North.
- Avoid concentrating risk in any single jurisdiction or political context.

Specific first-wave selection is made by the Federation governance body informed by the Stewardship Office and Science Council, recorded in the public record with rationale.

### 6.3 Subsequent Waves

Subsequent waves expand:

- Coverage across bioregional categories.
- Coverage across continents.
- Engagement with more challenging contexts as federation capability matures.
- Density within already-engaged regions.

### 6.4 Continuous Reassessment

Phasing is reviewed annually by the Stewardship Office. Regions may be:

- Continued at current intensity.
- Scaled up.
- Paused.
- Withdrawn.

Decisions are based on outcomes, partnerships, and the broader portfolio balance.

## 7. Regional Governance

### 7.1 Subsidiarity Applied

Per Charter §4.5 (nested governance) and §2.10 (subsidiarity), decisions belong at the lowest level competent to make them.

- **Site-level decisions** — within recipes and lease terms, made by site stewards.
- **Regional-level decisions** — recipe adaptations within Codex constraints, federation member coordination, regional partnerships, dispute mediation, made by regional coordination bodies.
- **Global-level decisions** — protocol changes, recipe template approvals, Charter amendments, federation-wide governance, made by global Federation governance bodies.

### 7.2 Regional Coordination Body

Each engaged region has a Regional Coordination Body (RCB) including:

- Representatives of federation members operating in the region.
- Representatives of indigenous and local communities active in the region's leases.
- Regional Stewardship Office representation.
- Regional Science Council representation (where regional Council subgroups exist).
- Government and multilateral liaisons where relevant.

The RCB meets regularly to coordinate operations, review outcomes, address regional issues, and represent the region in global Federation governance.

### 7.3 Recipe Adaptation Authority

Regional bodies have authority to adapt recipes within bounds defined by the Codex:

- Substituting locally-appropriate species within an ecosystem template.
- Adjusting density, succession, or tending protocols to local conditions.
- Adapting MRV protocols to regionally-specific indicators.

Adaptations beyond defined bounds require Codex revision through Gaia's interpretation layer protocol, with Science Council review.

### 7.4 Regional Sovereignty Within Federation

Regional bodies operate with substantial autonomy within Federation protocol. The Federation:

- Sets the protocol all regions follow.
- Sets the principles all regions honor.
- Provides reference implementations all regions may use or fork.
- Convenes governance where cross-regional coordination is needed.

The Federation does not micromanage regional operations.

### 7.5 Indigenous Governance Within Regions

Where indigenous communities have governance structures over their territories, those structures are recognized as primary for matters within their territories. Federation Regional Coordination Bodies coordinate with indigenous governance, not above it.

## 8. Cross-Regional Coordination

### 8.1 Codex as Shared Knowledge

Gaia's interpretation layer is shared across regions. Recipe lessons learned in one region inform recipe development in others. This is the structural form of "the federation learns": every region's experience becomes available to every other region's strategy.

### 8.2 Cross-Regional Review

The Federation governance body convenes cross-regional review at least annually:

- Comparing outcomes across regions to identify what is working and what is not.
- Surfacing methodological insights with cross-regional applicability.
- Identifying regions where additional support or course correction is warranted.
- Identifying regions whose lessons should accelerate strategy elsewhere.

### 8.3 Personnel Mobility

Personnel may move across regions, particularly for capability transfer. Personnel movement is voluntary, supports rather than substitutes for local capacity-building, and respects regional sovereignty.

### 8.4 Cross-Regional Partnerships

Some federation partners operate in multiple regions. Cross-regional partner relationships are coordinated through the Federation Membership Agreement and bilateral terms; neither global nor any single region directs them.

### 8.5 Scientific and Methodological Coherence

While methods adapt regionally, methodological coherence is maintained globally:

- MRV principles apply across regions.
- Protocol Specifications apply across regions.
- Charter applies across regions.

Coherence is not uniformity; it is the shared discipline within which regional adaptation occurs.

## 9. Bioregional Considerations (Summary)

The following are high-level strategic considerations per major bioregional category. These are starting orientations, not full regional strategies — full strategies are developed per §5 for specific regional engagements.

### 9.1 Tropical Forest Systems

- High carbon and biodiversity leverage.
- Complex governance: indigenous and traditional rights highly significant; jurisdictions varied.
- Climate trajectories include severe risks to existing forests (Amazon dieback, Congo drying).
- Substantial existing federation-aligned actors.
- Operational considerations: remote logistics, biosecurity (high pathogen pressure), community capacity.

### 9.2 Temperate Forest Systems

- Moderate carbon leverage; significant biodiversity and connectivity value.
- More developed regulatory and property-law contexts.
- Longer maturity timelines for adult-tree thresholds (oak, beech, cedar).
- Lower indigenous-governance prominence in some regions; significant in others (Pacific Northwest, parts of East Asia).
- Operational considerations: easier logistics, established forestry regulatory frameworks.

### 9.3 Boreal and High-Latitude Systems

- High carbon storage, much in soil and peat as well as biomass.
- Climate change impact severe and accelerating; permafrost loss, increased fire.
- Sparse human populations but indigenous communities in many areas.
- Long restoration timelines; recipes must account for very long establishment.
- Operational considerations: short operational windows, extreme conditions, fire regime increasingly disruptive.

### 9.4 Drylands, Grasslands, Savanna Systems

- Distinct from forest systems but critical to biosphere; soil carbon and ecological function dominate over above-ground biomass.
- Holistic Management and FMNR are particularly relevant methodologies.
- Significant indigenous and pastoralist governance.
- Climate change impact severe (increased aridity in many regions).
- Operational considerations: covers very large areas; herd-based methodologies require animal management.

### 9.5 Mediterranean and Chaparral Systems

- Biodiversity hotspots in all five Mediterranean regions.
- Fire-adapted ecosystems; restoration must work with fire, not against it.
- High population density in some regions complicates land availability.
- Climate change impact: increasing aridity, more extreme fire.
- Operational considerations: regionally-specific recipes; high-value land creates lease cost challenges.

### 9.6 Wetland and Riparian Systems

- Disproportionate carbon (peat) and biodiversity value.
- Hydrological restoration is often the precondition for ecological restoration.
- Mangroves are climate-buffer ecosystems with severe ongoing loss.
- Strong governance complexity (water rights, downstream effects).
- Operational considerations: specialized methodologies; dependence on hydrological context.

### 9.7 Mountain Systems

- Vertical zonation creates multiple ecosystem types in close proximity.
- Climate change shifts species ranges upward; some species run out of mountain.
- Indigenous and traditional governance significant in many mountain regions.
- Hydrological importance for downstream populations.
- Operational considerations: difficult logistics; specialized recipes per altitude band.

### 9.8 Tundra and High-Altitude Alpine

- Slow restoration timelines; some interventions are climate-adaptation rather than restoration.
- Climate change impact severe; permafrost-dependent ecosystems particularly threatened.
- Indigenous Arctic communities have substantial sovereignty.
- Operational considerations: extreme conditions; very narrow operational windows; high cost per intervention.

### 9.9 Island Systems

- High endemism, high vulnerability.
- Invasive species control often a precondition for restoration.
- Cultural importance often comparable to ecological importance.
- Climate change impact: sea-level rise threatens many island ecosystems directly.
- Operational considerations: logistics challenging; biosecurity critical (preventing introduction of new invasives); community sovereignty central.

## 10. Open Questions

These are unresolved questions about bioregional strategy:

- **Climate-shifted bioregion thresholds.** When does a bioregion's projected climate trajectory warrant fundamental recipe revision (assisted migration, ecosystem reassembly) versus adaptation within current ecology? The threshold is ecologically and politically contested.
- **High-conflict region engagement.** Some of the highest-leverage restoration sites are in regions of armed conflict or severe political instability. Refusal to engage risks abandoning those sites and communities; engagement risks personnel and operational integrity. The boundary needs to be drawn case by case but principles for the drawing are not yet specified.
- **Urban and peri-urban restoration.** Many bioregions intersect with major urban areas. Restoration within and adjacent to urban regions has different operational characteristics. Whether and how Robotanica engages urban restoration is unresolved.
- **Marine and coastal extension.** The mission is currently terrestrial. Coastal and marine ecosystems are degrading severely. Whether the Federation extends to marine restoration, or coordinates with separate marine-focused federations, is unresolved.
- **Cross-regional ecological corridors.** Some restoration value comes from connectivity at scales larger than single bioregions (continental migration corridors, climate-shift connectivity). How the federation coordinates corridor restoration across multiple regions is undeveloped.
- **Refugia versus restoration prioritization.** Within climate trajectory, some regions become climate refugia (suitable for current ecosystems despite global warming). Whether to prioritize refugia (preserving what can be preserved) or to prioritize areas of severe loss (rebuilding what was lost) is contested ecology and contested ethics.
- **Subsistence and livelihood interactions.** In many bioregions, restoration interacts with subsistence livelihoods. Restoration that displaces livelihoods is unjust; restoration that reinforces extractive livelihoods is self-defeating. The boundary is hard.

## Appendix C — Glossary (Bioregional-Specific)

- **Bioregion** — A region defined primarily by ecological coherence; the natural unit of restoration strategy.
- **Bioregional category** — Foundational classification for strategic purposes (tropical forest, drylands, etc.); finer granularity available in Codex.
- **Climate-shifted analog** — The bioregion or set of bioregions whose current climate envelope most closely matches a region's projected future climate.
- **Regional Coordination Body (RCB)** — The governance body for an engaged region.
- **Subregion** — Finer-grained bioregional unit; the operational scale.
- **First-wave region** — A region in the initial set of regional engagements.
- **Refugium / refugia** — Regions where current ecosystems may persist despite global climate change.

---

*A trillion adult trees will not appear because the global average improves. They will appear because specific places — bioregionally coherent, partnership-grounded, governance-respected, operationally capable — are restored, one at a time, in sequence, with care. This Strategy is the framework for choosing those places and approaching them as they deserve.*
