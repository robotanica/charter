# The Science Council Charter

**Subordinate Governance Document**
**Version 0.1 · Draft for Ratification**

---

## Preamble

The Robotanica Charter establishes the Science Council as a body holding veto authority over species choices, site recipes, and intervention protocols. The main Charter specifies the Council's existence, its multi-disciplinary structure, and its veto authority. This document specifies how the Council actually operates: who serves, how they are chosen, how decisions are made, how independence is maintained, and how the Council itself is held accountable.

The Council exists because robotics-led organizations will reliably propose monocultures of fast-growing exotics if no one is empowered to say no. The Council's purpose is to say no when ecological evidence requires it, even when commercial, operational, or political pressure prefers otherwise.

The Threat Model (§6.3 "The Council Capture") identifies the gradual erosion of Council independence as one of the most consequential failure modes of Robotanica. This document is the structural defense against that failure mode. Its provisions are deliberately rigid because the failure mode is gradual and hard to detect; rigidity at the constitutional layer is the discipline that prevents drift at the operational layer.

This Charter is subordinate to the main Robotanica Charter and may not conflict with it. Where conflict arises, the main Charter governs.

---

## Article I — Mission and Authority

### §1.1 Mission

The Science Council exists to ensure that Robotanica's restoration work is grounded in ecological evidence, biological integrity, and the plurality of relevant knowledge traditions. The Council is the ecological conscience of the project.

### §1.2 Scope of Authority

The Council holds authority over:

- **Recipe approval** — recipes in Gaia's interpretation layer require Council review before they may be applied at scale.
- **Recipe revision** — material revisions to existing recipes follow the same review.
- **Species and ecosystem ontology** — entries in Gaia that define what species are, what ecosystems are, and how they function.
- **Intervention protocols** — the methodologies by which restoration work is performed, including biological production specifications and field operation protocols.
- **MRV methodology** — the methodologies by which outcomes are measured, reported, and verified, per the MRV Specification.
- **Reconciliation events** of structural significance — when site failures or methodological corrections affect multiple recipes or large numbers of credits, Council review is required.
- **Verifier accreditation** — the standards by which independent verifiers are recognized.

### §1.3 Veto Authority

The Council's veto over its scope is binding. No operational governance body may override a Council veto.

A veto may be:

- **Categorical** — the Council rejects a proposal in its entirety.
- **Conditional** — the Council approves subject to specific changes.
- **Phased** — the Council approves limited application (e.g., specific bioregions or limited scale) pending further evidence.

### §1.4 Boundaries of Authority

The Council does not govern:

- Operational decisions within approved recipes (those are operator and steward authority).
- Federation membership and discipline (those are Federation governance).
- Lease formation, amendment, or termination (those are A3 and Stewardship Office authority, with FPIC and Landowner consent).
- Compensation, employment, or organizational matters within Robotanica.

### §1.5 Appeal

Council decisions may be appealed only through the main Charter's amendment process or through processes specifically authorized by the Federation governance body. Appeals do not stay the decision pending resolution.

## Article II — Composition

### §2.1 Council Size

The Council comprises **fifteen seated members** at full composition. Smaller transitional sizes are permitted during founding and during periods of transition; the minimum operational size is nine members.

### §2.2 Mandatory Composition Categories

No single discipline, methodology, or interest may dominate the Council. The following minimum representation is required:

- **At least three members** with primary expertise in ecology, including at least one with expertise in soil ecology and at least one with expertise in landscape and ecosystem ecology.
- **At least two members** with primary expertise in restoration practice, drawn from practitioners actively conducting field restoration, not exclusively academic.
- **At least three members** representing indigenous and traditional ecological knowledge traditions, selected through indigenous governance processes per §3.4. These members hold equal standing with members from formal scientific traditions.
- **At least two members** with primary expertise in plant biology, including botany, mycology, or related disciplines covering the holobiont.
- **At least one member** with primary expertise in climate science and its interactions with restoration ecology.
- **At least one member** with primary expertise in ethics, philosophy of science, or the social study of restoration; this seat exists to bring critical perspective to the Council's own practice.
- **At least one member** with primary expertise in economics or political economy of land use, qualified to assess the broader-system implications of recipe choices.

The remaining seats may be filled with additional expertise as the Council determines necessary.

### §2.3 Bioregional Representation

The Council collectively must include members with deep expertise in at least the following bioregional groupings:

- Tropical forest systems
- Temperate forest systems
- Boreal and high-latitude systems
- Drylands, grasslands, and savanna systems
- Wetland and riparian systems

Where Robotanica operates significantly in additional bioregions, additional representation is added.

### §2.4 Diversity Requirements

The Council must reflect:

- **Gender diversity** — no gender constitutes more than 60% of seated members.
- **Geographic diversity** — members are drawn from at least four continents at all times.
- **Career stage diversity** — at least three members are early-career (within ten years of credentialing or equivalent practical recognition); at least three members are senior (twenty or more years).
- **Methodological diversity** — adherents and critics of major methodologies (Miyawaki, Savory, FMNR, Society for Ecological Restoration mainstream, others) must both be represented; the Council may not become an echo chamber for any one school.

### §2.5 Independence Requirements

Members must be independent:

- Members may not be current employees of Robotanica or any operating Federation member.
- Members may not derive more than 25% of their personal income from Robotanica or its Federation members in aggregate, including consulting, board fees, and grant funding controlled by Robotanica.
- Members must disclose all financial and institutional ties annually to a public register.
- Past employment with Robotanica or Federation members is not disqualifying but is publicly disclosed; a cooling-off period of two years applies between leaving such employment and Council eligibility.

## Article III — Selection and Appointment

### §3.1 Founding Council

The founding Council is appointed by the founding parties of Robotanica through a public process satisfying §3.5. Founding members serve staggered terms per §4.1 to enable rotation.

### §3.2 Standing Selection Process

Subsequent members are selected through:

1. **Open nomination period** — at least 90 days during which any party may nominate candidates, with candidate consent.
2. **Review by an Independent Selection Committee** — a committee distinct from Robotanica leadership and from the existing Council, drawn from peer scientific bodies, indigenous councils, and federation members not under sanction.
3. **Public posting of finalists** — at least 60 days during which candidate qualifications and disclosures are public, and any party may submit comment or evidence of disqualifying conflict.
4. **Final selection by the Independent Selection Committee** — the existing Council may comment but does not select its own successors; this prevents the most direct form of capture.
5. **Ratification by the Federation governance body** — confirms procedural compliance, not substantive judgment over candidates.

### §3.3 Independent Selection Committee

The Independent Selection Committee is appointed for each selection cycle and includes:

- **At least three representatives** of recognized peer scientific bodies (Society for Ecological Restoration, IUCN, equivalent national academies, ecological societies).
- **At least two representatives** from indigenous and community governance bodies, selected by their own processes.
- **At least two representatives** of Federation members in good standing, selected by Federation processes.
- **No members of the existing Council**, no Robotanica employees, no representatives of major Robotanica funders.

### §3.4 Indigenous and Traditional Knowledge Seats

The seats reserved for indigenous and traditional ecological knowledge representation (§2.2) are filled through processes determined by indigenous governance bodies, not by the Independent Selection Committee. The Federation provides logistical and financial support for these processes but does not direct them.

Where multiple indigenous governance bodies have legitimate claim to representation, the seats are distributed to ensure plurality of indigenous traditions, not concentrated in a single tradition or region.

### §3.5 Process Public Record

Every step of selection is public: nominations, disclosures, Selection Committee composition and deliberations, and final decisions. This is the operational form of the Charter's transparency commitment and the structural defense against opaque capture.

### §3.6 Refusal of Capture

If, in the Independent Selection Committee's judgment, the nomination pool has been manipulated or the process compromised by external pressure, the Committee may refuse to select and may extend the selection cycle. This refusal is itself a public act with stated reasoning.

## Article IV — Terms and Rotation

### §4.1 Term Length

Members serve **six-year terms**. Terms are staggered such that approximately one-third of seats turn over every two years, preventing both wholesale replacement and extended entrenchment.

### §4.2 Term Limits

A member may serve a maximum of **two consecutive terms** (twelve years total). After two consecutive terms, a member must take at least one term off before being eligible for re-selection.

### §4.3 Vacancies

Vacancies between regular selection cycles are filled by an expedited selection process per Article III. Members appointed to fill vacancies serve the remainder of the prior term and are eligible for one full subsequent term.

### §4.4 Senior Members

A small number of seats (no more than three) may be designated **senior member** seats with eight-year terms, to provide continuity. Senior member status is determined at selection, not promotion, and senior members are still subject to the two-consecutive-term limit (sixteen years maximum).

## Article V — Decision-Making

### §5.1 Quorum

A quorum of **two-thirds** of seated members is required for any binding decision.

### §5.2 Voting Thresholds

- **Recipe approval** — simple majority of those present, with no member objecting on grounds of inadequate review.
- **Recipe revision (material)** — simple majority.
- **Veto** — a single member may invoke a veto on grounds of ecological harm, biological integrity, or violation of foundational principles. A veto stands unless overridden by a two-thirds majority of seated members (not just those present).
- **Methodology approval (MRV, intervention protocols)** — two-thirds majority of seated members.
- **Procedural matters** — simple majority of those present.

### §5.3 The Single-Member Veto

The single-member veto (§5.2) is the structural protection against majority capture. A Council that has been gradually shifted toward operational accommodation can still be checked by a single member with sufficient evidence of ecological harm. The two-thirds override requirement makes overriding a veto difficult enough to require genuine consensus.

A member invoking the veto must provide written reasoning and evidence; the veto and the reasoning enter the public record.

### §5.4 Deliberation Time

Material decisions require minimum deliberation time:

- **Recipe approval (new recipe at scale)** — at least 60 days from full proposal to decision.
- **Recipe revision (material)** — at least 30 days.
- **MRV methodology change** — at least 90 days.
- **Vetoes** — may be invoked at any time; the override process requires at least 30 days.

These minimums are floors; the Council may take more time as needed.

### §5.5 Recusal

Members must recuse themselves from decisions where they have material interest:

- Financial interest beyond ordinary academic citation.
- Authorship of the proposal under review (a member who developed a recipe may present it but not vote on it).
- Personal involvement in the operations under review.
- Conflict identified by another member or by external party, accepted by the recusing member.

Recusals are recorded; voting records show recusals explicitly.

### §5.6 Tie-Breaking

In the rare case of a tie that procedure cannot resolve, the matter is held over to the next Council meeting. If the tie persists, the Chair of the Council casts a deciding vote, with the deciding-vote use publicly recorded and explained. The Chair role is itself rotated per §6.3.

### §5.7 Public Voting

All Council votes are public. Each member's vote is recorded and reported. Members do not vote anonymously on Council matters.

### §5.8 Dissent

A member voting against the majority may submit a written dissent that becomes part of the decision record. Dissents are public and serve as both internal accountability and external signal of contested decisions.

## Article VI — Council Operations

### §6.1 Meeting Cadence

The Council meets:

- **Plenary sessions** — at least four times per year, with at least one in-person where feasible.
- **Subcommittee sessions** — as needed for specific reviews.
- **Emergency sessions** — convenable on 72-hour notice for urgent matters (catastrophic site events, emerging biosecurity threats, time-sensitive recipe disputes).

### §6.2 Subcommittees

The Council may form subcommittees for sustained work in specific areas (bioregional review, MRV methodology, recipe types). Subcommittees recommend; the full Council decides.

### §6.3 Chair and Officers

The Council elects from its members:

- A **Chair**, serving a two-year non-consecutive term, responsible for setting agendas and presiding over meetings.
- A **Vice-Chair**, serving similarly, providing continuity and substituting for the Chair as needed.
- A **Recorder**, responsible for the integrity of meeting records and the public reporting per Article VII.

Chair, Vice-Chair, and Recorder roles rotate; no member holds the same officer role for more than two consecutive terms.

### §6.4 Working Language

The Council operates in the languages of its members, with full translation and interpretation provided. Meeting records and decisions are published in English and Spanish at minimum, and in other languages as members and stakeholders require. No member's voice is privileged or marginalized because of language.

### §6.5 Support Staff

The Council is supported by a small staff funded by Robotanica but reporting to the Council, not to Robotanica leadership. Staff support meeting logistics, document preparation, public reporting, and research synthesis. Staff do not vote and have no decision authority.

### §6.6 Compensation

Council members are compensated for their work:

- Annual stipend at a level reflecting the time commitment (estimated 20-30% of full-time professional engagement).
- Compensation calibrated to regional cost of living and professional standards, not standardized in a way that disadvantages members from regions with lower cost structures.
- Travel and accommodation for in-person meetings.
- Compensation is public per the Charter's transparency commitment on compensation flows.

Compensation may not exceed the independence threshold of §2.5: members may not derive more than 25% of personal income from Robotanica.

### §6.7 Insurance and Legal Protection

Members are covered by insurance and legal protection for actions taken in their Council role. This is necessary because Council decisions may be the target of litigation by affected parties. Protection extends to actions consistent with this Charter; it does not extend to misconduct.

## Article VII — Public Reporting and Transparency

### §7.1 Decision Records

Every Council decision is recorded with:

- The question
- The proposal as submitted
- Evidence considered
- Members present and members recused
- Vote tally with each member's vote
- Decision and conditions
- Dissents if any
- Date

These records are published in the public record per the main Charter and the A3 Governance Protocol.

### §7.2 Annual Report

The Council publishes an annual report including:

- Decisions made during the year
- Vetoes invoked
- Pending matters and their status
- Methodological developments
- Open issues for the coming year
- Council composition and any changes
- Compensation paid (per §6.6)

The annual report is a public document, accessible in the Federation's languages.

### §7.3 Member Disclosures

Each member files an annual disclosure of:

- All institutional affiliations
- Financial interests including investments, board positions, consulting
- Family member affiliations relevant to Council scope
- Any other relationships that could create conflict of interest

Disclosures are public and updated within 30 days of material change.

### §7.4 Confidentiality Boundaries

Council deliberations may include confidential information (sensitive site locations, indigenous community data, draft recipes from federation members). Confidentiality is bounded:

- Sovereignty-protected information remains protected per the main Charter.
- Confidentiality may not be invoked to conceal Council decisions, vote records, or member disclosures.
- Council members may discuss matters in summary form with their professional communities; specific confidential information is protected.

Breach of confidentiality discipline is grounds for removal under §8.3.

## Article VIII — Discipline and Removal

### §8.1 Grounds for Removal

A member may be removed for:

- Material breach of independence requirements (undisclosed conflict, exceeding the income threshold).
- Breach of confidentiality discipline.
- Pattern of insufficient recusal.
- Violation of the Robotanica Charter or this subordinate Charter.
- Persistent inability to perform the role (extended absence, incapacity).

A member may not be removed for unpopular but substantively defensible decisions, including invoking veto authority. Disagreement with a member's decisions is not grounds for removal.

### §8.2 Removal Process

Removal proceeds through:

1. **Complaint** — filed by any party with standing (Council member, Federation member, public party with substantive evidence).
2. **Investigation** — by an independent investigator appointed by the Independent Selection Committee for this purpose, distinct from any party with conflicting interest.
3. **Hearing** — the affected member is given notice, evidence, and the opportunity to respond, with representation of their choosing.
4. **Decision** — by a two-thirds majority of seated Council members not subject to the same complaint, with the affected member recused.
5. **Public record** — the complaint, investigation findings, hearing summary, and decision are recorded in the public record, with personal information protected as appropriate.

### §8.3 Discipline Short of Removal

Where conduct does not warrant removal, the Council may issue:

- **Censure** — formal recorded condemnation of specific conduct.
- **Required training or recusal expansion** — the member must take additional steps to manage conflict or demonstrate competence in specific areas.
- **Suspension** — temporary loss of voting rights, with defined conditions for reinstatement.

### §8.4 Voluntary Resignation

A member may resign at any time. Resignation is recorded; if resignation occurs while a complaint is pending, the complaint proceeds and findings are recorded even if the member is no longer seated.

## Article IX — Relationship to Other Governance Bodies

### §9.1 Stewardship Office

The Stewardship Office holds Lease relationships and lease succession authority. The Council does not direct the Stewardship Office's relationship work but does inform it through recipe and ecological judgments.

The Stewardship Office may bring specific situations to the Council for ecological review; the Council may flag situations for Stewardship Office attention when ecological evidence warrants.

### §9.2 Federation Governance

Federation governance handles membership, conformance, and federation-level operational coordination. The Council does not govern Federation membership but its judgments inform Federation review of operators whose work involves recipe deviations or ecological concerns.

### §9.3 A3, Gaia, and OpenFactory Engineering

The platform engineering organizations build and operate the platforms. The Council reviews recipes, methodologies, and ecological matters within the platforms but does not direct platform engineering itself. Where ecological requirements imply platform changes, the Council and platform engineering coordinate.

### §9.4 External Bodies

The Council may engage with external scientific and indigenous governance bodies as peers. The Council does not subordinate itself to any single external body and is not subordinate to external accreditation.

## Article X — Amendment

### §10.1 Subordinate to Main Charter

This subordinate Charter cannot conflict with the main Robotanica Charter. Provisions of the main Charter that are un-amendable (Charter §8.1) remain so; this subordinate Charter cannot circumvent them.

### §10.2 What May Be Amended

Most provisions of this Charter may be amended through §10.4. The following provisions are designated as un-amendable in this subordinate Charter:

- **§1.3 Veto Authority** — the Council's veto over its scope is binding and cannot be overridden by operational governance.
- **§2.2 Mandatory Composition Categories** — the multi-disciplinary structure cannot be reduced to a single school or interest.
- **§2.5 Independence Requirements** — members must remain structurally independent of Robotanica and Federation members.
- **§3.4 Indigenous and Traditional Knowledge Seats** — selection through indigenous governance processes cannot be replaced by Federation appointment.
- **§5.3 The Single-Member Veto** — a single member's ability to invoke a veto subject to two-thirds override.
- **§7 Public Reporting** — decisions, vote records, and disclosures remain public.

To amend these provisions is to dissolve the Council and constitute a different body.

### §10.3 Comprehensive Review

This Charter is reviewed comprehensively every ten years by the Council and the Independent Selection Committee jointly. Review does not require amendment; it requires the questions to be asked.

### §10.4 Amendment Process

Amendments may be proposed by the Council, the Federation governance body, the Stewardship Office, or any member of the Federation in good standing.

Amendments require:

- Public posting for at least 180 days
- Consultation with the Council, with indigenous governance bodies, and with the Federation
- Approval by a two-thirds majority of the seated Council
- Approval by the Federation governance body
- Confirmation that amendments do not conflict with main Charter provisions

### §10.5 Public Record of Amendment Process

All proposed amendments, all comment received, and all decisions are preserved in the public record, including amendments that fail.

## Article XI — Adoption and Transition

### §11.1 Initial Adoption

This Charter takes effect upon ratification by the founding parties of Robotanica.

### §11.2 Transitional Provisions

During the founding period, the Council may operate with smaller composition (minimum nine members) and shorter terms while approaching the standing structure. Transitional periods may not exceed three years from initial seating.

### §11.3 Public Posting

This Charter is posted publicly upon ratification and remains continuously available in open formats and in the Federation's languages.

## Appendix A — Open Questions

These are unresolved questions that the founding Council and Federation will address as the Council operates:

- **Indigenous governance plurality.** Where multiple indigenous governance bodies have legitimate claim to nominating seats and have differing nomination processes, how is plurality structured fairly? §3.4 sketches a principle but does not yet specify a process.
- **Member liability across jurisdictions.** Council members in different jurisdictions face different liability regimes for ecological decisions. §6.7 addresses insurance but jurisdictional variability needs further work.
- **Council's relationship to AI agents.** As Robotanica's agents propose recipes and analyses, the Council's review process needs to accommodate agent-derived material with appropriate skepticism. The current MRV Specification flags this; this Charter does not yet specify procedures.
- **Cross-Council coordination.** As the Federation grows, regional or specialized Councils may emerge. The relationship between such bodies and this Council is not yet specified.
- **Council members under threat.** Members making unpopular decisions in contested contexts may face professional, financial, or personal threats. Protection mechanisms beyond §6.7 may be needed at scale.
- **Continuity through civilizational disruption.** During major disruption (pandemic, conflict, financial collapse), the Council must continue to function. Continuity protocols are sketched but not detailed.

- **Independence threshold** — The 25% income limit defined in §2.5.

- **Recusal** — A member's withdrawal from a specific decision due to conflict.
